# Letter 57 — Erik Swanson

- **Date:** 2026-05-05
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/S7-2026-15/s7202615-768847-2357514.html

---

Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Erik Swanson

I am writing to express my strong opposition to the Commission's proposal to permit certain issuers to report on a semiannual basis rather than quarterly. Reducing the frequency of required disclosures would materially weaken the transparency framework that underpins U.S. capital markets.

Quarterly reporting has long served as a foundational mechanism for ensuring that investors receive timely, decision-useful information about an issuer's financial condition, risk exposures, and operational performance. Moving to a semiannual cadence would create extended periods in which material developments may go unreported, increasing information asymmetry between insiders and the investing public. This would undermine market efficiency and elevate the risk of mispricing, volatility, and unequal access to critical data.

The Commission has repeatedly emphasized that transparency is essential to investor protection and to maintaining confidence in U.S. markets. Allowing issuers to report only twice per year runs counter to that principle. In an environment where business conditions, macroeconomic factors, and risk profiles can shift rapidly, less frequent reporting would leave investors with outdated information for longer periods and impair their ability to make informed decisions.

While the proposal suggests that reduced reporting may lessen compliance burdens, any marginal administrative benefit is outweighed by the significant costs to market integrity. The existing quarterly framework strikes an appropriate balance between issuer obligations and investor needs, and it should be preserved.

For these reasons, I respectfully urge the Commission not to adopt the proposed rule permitting semiannual reporting. Maintaining robust, frequent disclosure requirements is essential to protecting investors and sustaining the transparency that has long distinguished U.S. capital markets.

Thank you for considering this comment.
