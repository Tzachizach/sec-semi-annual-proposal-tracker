Subject: S7-2026-15 — Webform Comments from Noah Kerchner
From: Noah Kerchner

I strongly oppose the SEC's proposal to allow public companies the option to file semiannual reports on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q. This change would represent a significant step backward for investor protection, market efficiency, and the integrity of U.S. capital markets. If the Commission is inclined to revisit interim reporting frequency, it should move in the opposite direction: toward more timely, more frequent, and more transparent disclosures that better serve investors in today's fast-moving markets.

Core Objections to the Proposal:

Reduced Transparency and Increased Information Asymmetry. Quarterly reporting provides investors with timely, audited/reviewed financial statements and MD&A updates every three months. Doubling the gap to six months would leave investors — particularly retail investors — operating with stale information for extended periods. Material developments in revenue trends, cost structures, liquidity, or risks could remain hidden or only partially disclosed via 8-Ks, which are narrower and lack the comprehensive context of a full 10-Q. This asymmetry favors insiders, large institutions with private channels, and sophisticated analysts at the expense of average investors.

Heightened Risk of Misconduct and Market Surprises. Longer intervals between verified disclosures create greater opportunities for earnings management, channel stuffing, or delayed recognition of problems. History shows that less frequent reporting correlates with larger restatements or scandals when issues finally surface. Investors deserve early warning signals, not six-month black boxes. The proposal's reliance on 8-Ks and voluntary disclosures is insufficient — those tools have never fully substituted for the discipline and comparability of mandatory quarterly filings.

Damage to Market Efficiency and Liquidity. U.S. markets thrive on high-quality, timely information. Quarterly reports underpin analyst models, institutional oversight, executive compensation alignment, and efficient price discovery. Allowing companies to opt into semi-annual reporting would fragment the market, create a two-tiered system (frequent vs. infrequent reporters), complicate cross-company comparisons, and potentially increase volatility when half-year results finally arrive. Smaller companies, which the proposal ostensibly helps, may suffer most: reduced transparency often raises (not lowers) their cost of capital as investors demand higher risk premiums.

Weak Justification and Insufficient Safeguards. Compliance costs, while real, have not prevented record numbers of public companies or robust capital formation in recent decades. Many burdens stem from overly complex rules elsewhere — not the core quarterly cadence. The proposal's "optional" nature does not mitigate harm; companies most inclined to opt out may be those with the weakest disclosures or performance. Investor demand and competitive pressure will not reliably fill the gap, especially for mid- and small-cap firms.

Recommendations:

Rather than loosening requirements, the SEC should modernize and strengthen interim reporting to reflect 21st-century technology, data availability, and investor expectations:

- Require more frequent key disclosures: Mandate monthly or quarterly updates on critical metrics (e.g., revenue, customer metrics, liquidity, material contracts) via streamlined 8-K exhibits or a new short-form report.
- Enhance 10-Q quality and timeliness: Shorten filing deadlines for large accelerated filers, require more forward-looking disclosures, segment reporting, and real-time XBRL tagging.
- Strengthen MD&A and risk updates: Require quarterly discussion of emerging risks, supply chain issues, cybersecurity, ESG factors, and management's outlook.
- Improve overall transparency: Broaden auditor review requirements, enhance segment and disaggregated data, mandate KPI disclosure.

The United States has long led the world in disclosure standards precisely because of rigorous, frequent reporting. Weakening that foundation risks eroding the comparative advantage of our public markets. I urge the Commission to withdraw this proposal and instead pursue reforms that deliver more frequent, higher-quality, and more accessible information to all investors.
