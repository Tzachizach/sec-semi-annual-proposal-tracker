Dear Commissioners and Staff:

         The Investor Choice Advocates Network (“ICAN”) appreciates the opportunity to
comment on the Securities and Exchange Commission’s proposed rule to allow public companies
to file semiannual reports on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q (“Proposed
Rule”).1 ICAN is a nonprofit public interest law firm and advocacy organization dedicated to
expanding access to capital markets and protecting the fundamental right of investors to make their
own informed financial decisions.
         ICAN supports the Proposed Rule and its expansion of investor choice. The ability of
investors to freely choose where to invest their capital is a cornerstone of efficient markets, and
the Proposed Rule advances that principle by giving investors and companies alike the ability to
choose a reporting cadence that best reflects their needs. Critics argue that less frequent mandatory
reporting harms retail investors by reducing available information. As discussed below, this
concern, while sincere, gives investors too little credit and the government too much, for we share
the critics’ commitment to keeping retail investors informed and simply trust that investors, given
the choice, will demand the information they need. The market, not regulators, is best positioned
to determine the optimal level of reporting frequency.

1. The Proposed Rule Expands Investor Choice Without Reducing Anyone’s Options

away from any investor. Any company that wishes to continue filing quarterly reports on Form
10-Q may continue to do so. Any investor who values frequent disclosure may simply choose to

        This is the essence of investor choice. Markets function best when participants can sort

        Critics who favor mandatory quarterly reporting implicitly assume that market discipline
is insufficient to protect investors who prefer more frequent disclosure. That assumption is
unwarranted. A company that elects semiannual reporting over the objections of its shareholder
base will face swift market consequences: reduced trading volume, diminished analyst coverage,
and a higher cost of capital. Institutional and retail investors alike can, and will, express their
preference through their investment decisions, avoiding semiannual filers in favor of companies
that report quarterly.
        A less liquid stock means fewer analysts, costlier fundraising, and a weaker position in
capital transactions and hiring. The market creates these pressures on its own, without any
regulatory mandate. As one prominent financial commentator noted shortly after the Proposed
Rule was released, large established companies are unlikely to shift to semiannual reporting
precisely because their investors expect quarterly updates and the companies understand the
market cost of disappointing that expectation.2 The Commission need not mandate what the market
will effectively require. ICAN’s mission is to expand market access and protect investor autonomy.
A rule that gives investors more choices is precisely the kind of market-expanding reform we
support.
        The mandatory quarterly reporting requirement also implicates a deeper structural concern.
Congress designed the Exchange Act's periodic reporting framework as a disclosure regime — one
intended to ensure that investors receive the information they need to make their own investment
decisions, not to restrict which investment options are available to them in the first place. A merits-
based regulator substitutes the government's judgment for the investor's by prohibiting access to
investments the regulator deems insufficiently attractive; a disclosure-based regulator ensures that
investors receive full and accurate information and then trusts them to decide. The existing
mandatory quarterly reporting rule, to the extent it bars investors from accessing public companies
that would choose to report semiannually, functions as the former: it withholds market access not
because any required disclosure has been omitted, but because a regulator has deemed quarterly
reporting to be a precondition for participation. The Proposed Rule corrects this structural drift by
returning the reporting-frequency decision to companies and investors, consistent with the

2. The “Less Information” Criticism Does Not Withstand Scrutiny
       The most common objection to the Proposed Rule is that quarterly reports provide investors
with more information, and therefore their elimination would harm retail investors. This concern
deserves a direct response.
        First, switching to semiannual reporting does not give companies a pass on disclosing
material news. Under Form 8-K, companies are already required to report significant
developments promptly as they occur, such as earnings surprises, executive changes, mergers,
regulatory actions, and dozens of other triggering events, and the Proposed Rule leaves those
obligations entirely intact. Semiannual filers will continue to report material developments as they
arise. The Proposed Rule reduces the frequency of scheduled interim reports; it does not create a
disclosure blackout.
        Second, the “more information is always better” argument confuses quantity with quality.
As Harvard Law Professor Hal Scott, Director of the Committee on Capital Markets Regulation,
has observed, the quarterly reporting regime is closely intertwined with the practice of issuing
quarterly earnings guidance, a practice widely cited as a driver of “short-termism,” pushing
management to optimize for meeting three-month earnings estimates rather than long-term value
creation.4 Academic research reinforces this concern: empirical studies have documented that
increased reporting frequency is associated with a meaningful decline in corporate investment and
an increase in real earnings management—findings consistent with the short-termism hypothesis.5

3. Reducing Compliance Costs Strengthens U.S. Public Markets
       Beyond investor choice, the Proposed Rule serves a compelling public-interest goal:
reducing the regulatory burden that has contributed to the declining number of publicly traded
companies in the United States and the lengthening of private holding periods. The Commission’s
own economic analysis estimates that filing three quarterly reports per year imposes approximately
$330,0009 in annual direct compliance costs per issuer before accounting for the cost of the Form
10-K, costs the SEC expects will be meaningfully reduced under the Proposed Rule, with the
savings reinvested in business operations, growth, and long-term strategy.10
       For large, established companies, this cost is manageable. For newly public companies,
smaller issuers, and companies in capital-intensive industries, it is a significant diversion of
resources from research and development, hiring, and capital investment. The burden of quarterly

4. The Proposed Rule Preserves Meaningful Disclosure Standards
        ICAN also wishes to highlight that the Proposed Rule is carefully designed to preserve the
quality of disclosure for companies that elect semiannual reporting. New Form 10-S would require
the same narrative disclosures and financial information currently required by Form 10-Q,
covering a six-month fiscal period rather than a quarter.18 Auditor review of semiannual financial
statements would remain required. The filing deadlines for Form 10-S align with those for Form
10-Q (40 or 45 days depending on filer status).19 The Proposed Rule offers a genuine middle path:
a structured, auditor-reviewed semiannual report, not a regulatory free-for-all.

Conclusion
        The Proposed Rule represents a thoughtful, market-respecting reform that expands investor
choice, reduces unnecessary compliance burdens, and aligns U.S. reporting practices with
international standards. The concerns raised by critics, while sincere, give investors too little credit
and the government too much. ICAN trusts that investors can and will make those judgments for
themselves. ICAN supports the Commission in finalizing the Proposed Rule for the reasons set
forth above.
