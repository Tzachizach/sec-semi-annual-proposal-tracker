Dear Securities and Exchange Commission, 

I am writing to formally state my objection to the proposal to discontinue quarterly reporting requirements. Moving to a semi-annual reporting schedule poses significant risks to ordinary investors and the integrity of the market. 

Reducing the frequency of disclosures creates a six-month window during which investors would effectively be "flying blind." A great deal can go wrong in half a year—such as failed product launches, ballooning debt, or litigation—and current laws ensure this information is surfaced quarterly. Under the proposed rule, companies could withhold negative news for six months, providing ample time for insiders to sell shares before the public is made aware of the situation. 

Furthermore, less frequent reporting provides more time for financial fraud to grow. Major accounting scandals are historically enabled by opacity in financial disclosures. While quarterly filings do not prevent fraud entirely, they significantly shorten the window in which it can fester. 

The "optional" nature of this proposal is also misleading. Once large companies shift to semi-annual filings, smaller competitors will face pressure to follow suit to reduce compliance costs. What begins as a voluntary choice will quickly become the new industry standard, lowering investor expectations across the board. 

Finally, the supposed cost savings of this change are modest and primarily benefit company executives and legal teams. Meanwhile, the burden of poorer information falls directly on ordinary investors whose retirement and education funds depend on market transparency. 

While insiders and institutional investors will maintain a clear view of a business's health, this proposal leaves the average investor in the dark. I urge you to maintain the current quarterly reporting requirements. 

Sincerely, 

Susan Mindler 
Average Investor
