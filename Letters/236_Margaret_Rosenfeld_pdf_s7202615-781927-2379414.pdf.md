Dear Ms. Countryman:
I write in response to the Commission’s request for comment on the proposed amendments to Exchange
Act Rules 13a-13 and 15d-13 that would permit domestic reporting companies to file a semiannual report
on new Form 10-S in lieu of two of three quarterly reports on Form 10-Q. I have practiced as a corporate
and securities lawyer for more than twenty-seven years, including as a partner at Freshfields Bruckhaus
Deringer, Paul Weiss Rifkind Wharton & Garrison, K&L Gates, and Smith Anderson, and in senior in-house
roles as chief legal officer building legal functions for public market readiness. The perspective I bring to
this comment letter is shaped by having advised, and served as de facto general counsel to, public
companies across the full range of the reporting company population. That has included large accelerated
filers with deep institutional ownership, established analyst coverage, active capital markets programs,
and the full institutional disclosure architecture that mature public company status requires. It has also
included smaller reporting companies, emerging growth companies, and recently public issuers operating
in earlier stages of that institutional progression, where the quarterly reporting cadence often feels
disproportionate to the operational tempo of the business. The disclosure liability gap and on-ramp
questions I address in this letter come from running quarterly reporting cycles at both ends of that range,
not from one end. I submit this letter in my capacity as Founder of Zukunft Advisory.
I do not oppose the proposal. The Commission’s policy rationale for offering reporting companies an
alternative to the existing quarterly cadence is defensible, and the cost-reduction objective is real for the
subset of issuers for whom quarterly reporting has always been disproportionate to the operational
tempo of the business. My concern with the proposal as currently drafted is narrower and, I believe,

addressable. The proposing release does not adequately engage with what replaces the quarterly cadence
for companies that elect semiannual reporting and continue to communicate with the market on a
quarterly basis, which the release itself contemplates and which I believe will be the majority of electing
issuers in practice. The institutional scaffolding that has historically made quarterly market
communications defensible is anchored in the existence of the Form 10-Q. Removing the formal filing
without addressing what supports the continuing communications creates a disclosure liability gap that
the proposing release does not name and that the comment file to date has not addressed in operational
terms.
The comment file to date is informative on what it does include, and equally so on what it does not. The
early commenters have approached the proposal from academic, retail investor, market-function, and
technology-infrastructure standpoints. These are useful perspectives, and the Commission will benefit
from them. What the comment file does not yet contain in meaningful volume is commentary from
practitioners who have run public company quarterly reporting cycles from the inside, who have
negotiated the institutional architecture that supports those filings, and who have advised boards and
management teams through the disclosure liability exposure that quarterly market communications
generate. This letter is offered to address that gap. The arguments that follow are operational rather than
theoretical, and the recommendations are calibrated to what the Commission would need to do in the
final release to make the proposed framework workable for the issuers most likely to elect into it.
This letter offers five contributions. First, it identifies the disclosure liability gap created by the hybrid
approach the proposing release contemplates. Second, it surfaces the audit committee, disclosure
committee, and auditor review questions that follow from removing the formal quarterly filing while
continuing quarterly market communications. Third, it proposes a four-component framework that
semiannual filers should be expected to implement on top of the election to preserve the institutional
discipline that has historically protected quarterly communications. Fourth, it addresses who the election
actually fits and raises the on-ramp question that follows from the bifurcation the proposal will create
between large accelerated filers (who will overwhelmingly not elect) and smaller, newer, and
development-stage issuers (who will). Fifth, it responds directly to several of the Commission’s numbered
requests for comment and offers three specific recommendations for what the final release should
contain.
I. The disclosure liability gap created by the hybrid approach
The proposing release explicitly contemplates that semiannual filers may continue to provide voluntary
quarterly information, including through earnings releases, conference calls, and voluntary inclusion of
quarterly financial information within the Form 10-S filing itself. The release describes this as a hybrid
approach that may represent a middle ground for companies seeking to balance cost savings with market
expectations. The Commission’s description is accurate so far as it goes. What the release does not engage
with is that the hybrid approach, in practice, will be the operating posture of the substantial majority of
electing issuers, not a middle ground reserved for those with specific institutional ownership profiles or
capital markets needs.
The reasons are structural. Analyst coverage of U.S. public companies is built around quarterly models.
Institutional investors maintain valuation frameworks that require quarterly revenue, earnings, and
guidance updates. Lenders incorporate financial covenant certification and reporting requirements into
credit agreements that often track the Form 10-Q schedule by default. Boards have organized their
meeting calendars and audit committee cadences around the quarterly filing cycle for decades. Investor

relations functions have built their annual programs around four earnings cycles. The semiannual election
does not change any of those expectations on day one. It removes one structural rationale for meeting
them, but the underlying demand for quarterly information from analysts, institutional investors, lenders,
and the broader market remains.
Most issuers that elect semiannual reporting will therefore continue to communicate with the market on
a quarterly cadence. They will issue earnings releases. They will hold earnings calls. They will provide
guidance and update it. They will respond to analyst questions. They will hold one-on-one investor
meetings. What changes is not the existence of those communications. What changes is the institutional
scaffolding that historically sat behind them.
The Form 10-Q is a Section 18 filed document. It carries CEO and CFO certifications under Sections 302
and 906 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. It is reviewed by the independent auditor under PCAOB AS
4105 (or the analogous interim review standard applicable to the relevant filer). It runs through a
disclosure committee process required by Item 307 and Item 308 of Regulation S-K. It is approved by the
audit committee. Item 4 of Form 10-Q requires an express attestation regarding the effectiveness of
disclosure controls and procedures and any changes in internal control over financial reporting. The Form
10-Q is the artifact produced at the end of that process, but the process itself is the institutional discipline
that makes the quarterly communications surrounding the filing defensible.
When a company communicates quarterly without producing a Form 10-Q, that scaffolding is not
automatically present. The earnings release is furnished under Item 2.02 of Form 8-K, not filed under
Section 18. The auditor has performed no interim review because there is no periodic report to review.
The disclosure committee may or may not have run a full process, because there is no statutory filing
deadline driving it. The audit committee may or may not have completed a substantive review of the
underlying financial statements, because the only quarterly artifact is a furnished press release rather
than a certified filing. The CEO and CFO sign off on the release, but they make no certifications under
Sections 302 or 906 because no underlying periodic report exists to certify. The forward-looking
statements in the prepared remarks rest on a cautionary statement framework that, in the absence of a
contemporaneous Form 10-Q, has no reaffirmed risk factor universe to anchor to. The earnings call
proceeds with the same Q&A, the same guidance discussion, and the same competitive and operational
commentary that a quarterly earnings call has always included, but the institutional record sitting behind
those communications is materially thinner than it would be if a Form 10-Q were filed
contemporaneously.
This gap is not theoretical. It is exactly the fact pattern that the plaintiffs’ securities class action bar will
construct cases around. The defense of a Rule 10b-5 case predicated on quarterly forward-looking
statements, guidance, or operational commentary has historically relied substantially on the architecture
of the periodic reporting process. The certifications, the auditor involvement, the disclosure committee
discipline, the audit committee review, the Item 4 attestation. That architecture, taken as a whole, is what
permits the issuer to argue at the pleading stage and beyond that the challenged statements were the
product of a deliberate and supervised disclosure process rather than ad hoc executive judgment. The
Form 10-S, filed twice a year, does not anchor the quarterly off-cycle communications in the same way.
Plaintiffs’ counsel will identify the gap. The Commission should anticipate it.
II. The audit committee, disclosure committee, and auditor review questions
Three operational questions follow directly from the disclosure liability gap and warrant the Commission’s
attention before the proposal is finalized.

A. The auditor review question
The auditing profession has not yet articulated publicly how it will approach interim review of quarterly
financial information for semiannual filers that continue to issue quarterly earnings releases. Under AU-C
930 / AS 4105, an interim review is generally requested in connection with a periodic report containing
reviewed interim financial information. Whether and on what terms a registered public accounting firm
will perform interim review work outside the context of a Form 10-Q filing, and at what fee level, is
unresolved. For most issuers electing semiannual reporting, particularly those with active capital markets
activity, meaningful institutional ownership, or any material disclosure events expected during the offcycle quarter, an interim review of the quarterly financial information that underlies the earnings release
will continue to be necessary as a matter of disclosure prudence regardless of what the Commission
formally requires. If the auditor will not perform that review without a Form 10-Q, the issuer will be
communicating quarterly without the auditor scaffolding that has historically supported quarterly
communications. If the auditor will perform the review, the cost savings the proposing release
contemplates are at least partly illusory.
The Commission should consider directly engaging with the PCAOB and the AICPA in advance of adoption
to clarify the auditor’s posture on interim review work for semiannual filers, and should consider whether
companion staff guidance or PCAOB rulemaking is needed to ensure a consistent profession-wide
approach. Without that clarification, the proposal as adopted will leave electing issuers and their boards
to negotiate auditor involvement on a case-by-case basis, with predictable variability in cost, scope, and
rigor across the issuer population.
B. The audit committee oversight question
Audit committees of public companies have organized their oversight cadence around the quarterly Form
10-Q cycle for decades. The agenda items, the meeting frequency, the auditor interactions, the financial
statement review, the disclosure committee report-out, the sub-certifications, the internal audit
reporting, the management representation letters, and the documented sign-off processes are all built
around four cycles per fiscal year with a defined statutory artifact at the conclusion of each. Moving to
two formal cycles per fiscal year does not reduce the audit committee’s oversight responsibility. It
removes the rhythm that has historically structured how that responsibility is discharged.
An audit committee that elects to maintain quarterly review discipline despite the move to semiannual
filings will be doing more work, not less, because it will be running its full process without the external
statutory deadline that has historically driven it. An audit committee that permits the off-cycle quarters
to become lighter-touch reviews will be accepting a material reduction in oversight at exactly the
moments when the company is still communicating quarterly to the market. Neither outcome is what the
cost-savings framing of the proposal anticipates. The Commission should consider whether the final
release should include express guidance to audit committees of electing companies regarding the level of
off-cycle quarter oversight expected, and should consider whether stock exchange listing standards
governing audit committee responsibilities require corresponding amendment to address the semiannual
filer context.
C. The disclosure committee question
Item 307 of Regulation S-K requires an express attestation by the principal executive and principal
financial officers regarding the effectiveness of disclosure controls and procedures, made in each Form
10-Q and Form 10-K. The disclosure committee process that supports that attestation has, in practice,
become a quarterly institutional discipline at most public companies. For semiannual filers, the Item 307

attestation will be made only in the Form 10-S and Form 10-K. The off-cycle quarter earnings release and
conference call will not be supported by a contemporaneous Item 307 attestation, because no periodic
report is being filed. The question whether the off-cycle quarter receives the same disclosure committee
discipline as the on-cycle quarter is left entirely to issuer judgment under the proposal as drafted.
The Commission should consider whether semiannual filers should be required to disclose, in their Form
10-S or Form 10-K, whether the disclosure committee has reviewed any voluntary quarterly information
released during the off-cycle period to a standard substantially equivalent to the standard applied to a
Form 10-Q. A disclosure of this kind would not require the disclosure committee process to be applied. It
would require the issuer to state whether it was applied, which is itself a meaningful disclosure to
investors and a meaningful protection against the disclosure liability gap I have described.
III. A proposed framework for semiannual filers that continue quarterly market communications
If a company elects semiannual reporting and continues to engage the market on a quarterly cadence,
which I expect will be the operating posture of the substantial majority of electing issuers, the institutional
scaffolding that has historically made quarterly communications defensible should be preserved
deliberately rather than allowed to erode by inattention. I propose four components, which I respectfully
suggest the Commission consider either as elements of the final rule, as elements of companion staff
guidance, or as expectations articulated in the adopting release’s discussion of the hybrid approach.
Component 1: A voluntary quarterly business update document, treated as a deliberate disclosure
artifact
Semiannual filers that issue quarterly earnings releases should be expected to treat those releases as
deliberate disclosure artifacts subject to substantially the same internal review and approval process
applied to a Form 10-Q. The document should contain the financial information, MD&A discussion,
material developments, and forward-looking statement framework the market actually expects. It may
be furnished under Item 2.02 or filed under Item 8.01 of Form 8-K depending on the issuer’s circumstances
and counsel’s judgment regarding the litigation calculus. The Commission should consider clarifying in the
adopting release that the institutional record sitting behind a voluntary quarterly release is a relevant
factor in any subsequent Rule 10b-5 analysis, and that issuers that elect semiannual reporting do not
thereby reduce the standard of care expected in connection with their continuing quarterly
communications.
Component 2: Auditor involvement calibrated to the issuer’s risk profile
As described above, the Commission should engage with the PCAOB and the AICPA to clarify the
profession’s posture on interim review work for semiannual filers. In the absence of profession-wide
clarification, electing issuers should be expected to obtain interim review work in connection with
quarterly financial information released to the market wherever the issuer’s circumstances make such
review prudent. The Commission should consider whether the final release should include a discussion of
the factors relevant to that determination, including institutional ownership profile, capital markets
activity, expected material disclosure events during the off-cycle period, and the issuer’s history of
restatements or material weakness disclosures.
Component 3: A preserved disclosure committee and audit committee process operating on a
quarterly cadence

Audit committee charters, disclosure committee charters, and internal disclosure controls policies of
electing issuers should be updated to reflect the semiannual reporting cadence and to make clear that
off-cycle quarters receive substantially the same governance treatment as on-cycle quarters even though
the artifact at the conclusion of the off-cycle quarter is a furnished release rather than a filed periodic
report. The Commission should consider whether the adopting release should articulate the expectation
that electing issuers maintain a documented disclosure and audit committee process for off-cycle quarter
communications, and whether disclosure of the existence of that process should be required in the Form
10-S or Form 10-K.
Component 4: A redesigned investor relations cadence
Removing two formal quarterly earnings cycles from the issuer’s annual disclosure calendar does not
reduce the underlying demand for institutional engagement. It diffuses that engagement across a larger
number of less structured touchpoints. Investor day presentations, conference appearances, sell-side
analyst meetings, one-on-one investor calls, and mid-period management road shows will be the vehicles
through which institutional engagement continues, and each of those touchpoints carries its own
Regulation FD exposure. Regulation FD does not turn on the cadence of formal periodic reporting. It turns
on whether material non-public information is disclosed selectively. Spreading the same engagement
demand across a larger surface area, without the concentrated discipline of four well-rehearsed earnings
cycles per year, increases Regulation FD risk for electing issuers, and most issuers’ Regulation FD processes
are not currently designed to manage the larger surface area. The Commission should consider whether
the adopting release should articulate the expectation that electing issuers conduct a deliberate review
of their investor relations protocols and Regulation FD procedures in connection with the election.
IV. Who the election actually fits, and the on-ramp question
The proposing release identifies smaller reporting companies, emerging growth companies, and
companies in industries where investors focus on business and regulatory developments rather than
interim financial results as the natural beneficiaries of the election. I agree with that identification as far
as it goes. The election will be genuinely well-suited to a specific subset of the public company population.
Development-stage companies whose investors track milestones rather than revenue trajectories.
Companies without active capital markets activity. Companies without bank financing structured around
quarterly covenant certifications. Companies without meaningful sell-side analyst coverage. Companies
whose institutional ownership profile does not generate quarterly engagement demand. For issuers in
this category, the quarterly cadence has often been disproportionate to the operational tempo of the
business, and the cost savings the proposing release contemplates will be more fully realized because the
underlying market demand for quarterly information is genuinely lower.
The question the proposing release does not engage with, and the question I respectfully submit the
Commission should address in the final release, is what happens to these issuers when they outgrow the
conditions that made the election attractive in the first place. The natural arc of a public company that
goes public as a smaller reporting company or an emerging growth company is one in which, over time,
the company grows revenues, attracts analyst coverage, develops capital markets ambitions, takes on
bank financing, accumulates institutional ownership, and eventually graduates to accelerated or large
accelerated filer status. The on-ramp from early-stage public company to mature public company is a real
institutional progression, and the disclosure cadence the company runs on during that progression is part
of the institutional infrastructure that supports it. The question is whether the semiannual election helps

issuers traverse that on-ramp or whether it institutionalizes a lighter-touch disclosure posture that
becomes a structural impediment when the company is ready to graduate.
The case for the election as an on-ramp aid is straightforward. Lower reporting cost during the early public
years lets the company conserve capital for business growth, which is precisely the Commission’s stated
policy objective. Less management distraction during the early public years lets the executive team focus
on operational execution at the moment when execution matters most. Fewer Sunday nights spent on
the Form 10-Q cycle is not a trivial benefit for a thinly-resourced management team running a recently
public company. For issuers whose early public years are genuinely about building the business rather
than engaging the capital markets, the election may be exactly the kind of regulatory flexibility that
supports the on-ramp.
The case against the election as an on-ramp aid is also straightforward. The disclosure cadence a company
runs on shapes the institutional discipline the company builds around its public reporting. A company that
has filed Form 10-Q quarterly from the date of its initial public offering has, by the time it is ready for
accelerated filer status, developed a disclosure committee process, an audit committee oversight
cadence, an investor relations function, an internal audit posture, and an executive team comfortable
with the quarterly reporting rhythm. A company that has filed Form 10-S semiannually for the same period
has not necessarily developed any of those institutional muscles, because the statutory cadence has not
required them to be exercised. When the company is ready to access the capital markets in size, to attract
sell-side analyst coverage, to take on bank financing structured around quarterly covenants, or to be
acquired by or acquire another public company, the institutional infrastructure that supports those
activities is not necessarily in place. Building that infrastructure under deadline pressure, while
simultaneously executing the transaction or relationship that requires it, is materially harder than building
it organically over the course of the public company life cycle.
The honest answer is that the on-ramp question is bidirectional. The election will help some smaller
reporting companies and emerging growth companies that would otherwise be unable to bear the cost
of being public at all. It will also box in some smaller reporting companies and emerging growth companies
that elect in early and then find themselves underprepared for the institutional demands of mature public
company status when they reach it. The Commission should be candid about that tradeoff in the final
release rather than presenting the election as an unqualified benefit to the smaller issuer population.
Two structural consequences follow from this analysis. First, larger issuers will overwhelmingly not elect
semiannual reporting. The institutional ownership, analyst coverage, capital markets activity, and lender
expectations that characterize the large-cap public company population make the election operationally
unworkable for those issuers regardless of its cost-savings appeal. The Commission should expect the
elected population to be concentrated almost entirely in smaller reporting companies, emerging growth
companies, and development-stage issuers. Second, the resulting bifurcation between quarterly filers
(predominantly mature, larger-cap, institutionally-owned) and semiannual filers (predominantly smaller,
newer, development-stage) is a structural change to the public company population that the proposing
release does not address as such. Whether that bifurcation is consistent with the Commission’s broader
investor protection objectives, and whether it supports or undermines the policy goal of encouraging
more companies to go and remain public, is a question the Commission should engage with in the final
release.
The on-ramp question matters most for the companies the proposing release is most focused on
supporting. The Commission should consider, in the final release, articulating the expectation that issuers
electing semiannual reporting maintain a deliberate institutional posture that preserves the option to

graduate to quarterly reporting cleanly when business circumstances warrant. The four-component
framework set forth in Section III above is one way to operationalize that expectation. Without it, the
election risks producing a class of public companies that has captured the early-stage cost savings but lost
the institutional discipline needed to make the on-ramp work.
V. Responses to specific requests for comment
I respond below to several of the Commission’s numbered requests for comment that bear directly on the
issues raised in this letter.
On voluntary quarterly disclosures and the hybrid approach
The Commission asks whether companies that elect semiannual reporting will continue to provide
voluntary quarterly information and, if so, what form that information will take and whether the
Commission should provide guidance. My view, based on twenty-seven years of practice advising public
companies, is that the substantial majority of electing issuers will continue to provide quarterly
information in some form because the underlying market demand for that information will not change.
The Commission should provide guidance, ideally in the adopting release, on the standard of care
expected in connection with voluntary quarterly disclosures, including the expectation that disclosure
committee and audit committee discipline will be applied substantially as it would be in connection with
a Form 10-Q. Without that guidance, the proposal effectively creates a class of quarterly market
communications that fall outside the institutional discipline that has historically supported quarterly
disclosure in the United States, and that result is inconsistent with the investor protection objectives the
Commission has articulated as central to its mission.
On internal controls and quarterly review
The Commission asks whether the proposal would affect internal controls or quarterly accountant
reviews. My view is that the proposal materially affects both. Internal control over financial reporting is
operationally a continuous function, but the Section 302 and 906 certifications, the Item 4 attestation,
and the disclosure controls and procedures evaluation that accompany each Form 10-Q create discrete
points at which internal control evaluation is documented, attested to, and made part of the institutional
record. Removing two of those points per fiscal year does not change the underlying control environment,
but it removes two of the formal occasions on which the control environment is evaluated and
documented. For accountant reviews, as discussed in Section II.A above, the profession’s posture is
unresolved, and the Commission should engage with the PCAOB and the AICPA to clarify it in advance of
adoption.
On insider trading policies and trading window practices
The Commission asks whether the proposal would affect insider trading policies and trading window
practices. My view is that semiannual reporting will significantly complicate trading window
administration for electing issuers and will require careful redesign of Rule 10b5-1 plan practices. Most
public company insider trading policies define blackout periods by reference to the quarterly earnings
cycle and the Form 10-Q filing. For semiannual filers, the absence of two of those quarterly anchors will
require either an extension of blackout periods (effectively reducing trading windows substantially) or a
redesign of the blackout framework around the voluntary quarterly disclosure cadence. Rule 10b5-1 plans
entered into under the December 2022 amendments contain cooling-off periods, certifications, and other
architecture predicated on the existence of a regular periodic reporting cadence. The Commission should

consider whether companion guidance on Rule 10b5-1 plan administration for semiannual filers is
appropriate.
On capital markets activity and registered offerings
The Commission asks how the proposal will affect the ability of electing issuers to conduct registered
offerings, including the staleness of financial statements for registration statement purposes. My view is
that the Regulation S-X staleness amendments included in the proposal address the technical question
but do not address the practical market question, which is whether underwriters, investors, and rating
agencies will be willing to participate in offerings supported only by semiannual financial information. In
my experience advising issuers in registered offerings, the comfort letter framework under SAS 100 and
the underwriter due diligence framework under Section 11 both rely heavily on the availability of reviewed
interim financial information that is reasonably current. The 135-day rule under existing PCAOB standards
for negative assurance in comfort letters, which the Commission has acknowledged in the proposing
release, is the most concrete example of this dependency. The Commission should consider whether final
adoption should be coordinated with PCAOB action to ensure that semiannual filers have a workable path
to access the capital markets between Form 10-S filings. Absent such coordination, electing issuers may
find that the cost savings from semiannual reporting are offset by reduced flexibility in accessing the
capital markets, which would frustrate the proposal’s policy objective of reducing the burden of public
company status.
VI. Specific recommendations for the final release
I respectfully offer three specific recommendations for the Commission’s consideration in connection with
final adoption of the proposed amendments.
First, the adopting release should articulate the expectation that semiannual filers that continue to
provide voluntary quarterly information apply substantially the same disclosure committee and audit
committee discipline to that information as they would apply to a Form 10-Q. The adopting release should
make clear that the election of semiannual reporting does not reduce the standard of care expected in
connection with quarterly market communications, and that the institutional record supporting voluntary
quarterly disclosures is a relevant factor in any subsequent enforcement or private action analysis.
Second, the Commission should coordinate with the PCAOB and the AICPA to clarify the auditing
profession’s posture on interim review work for semiannual filers in advance of final adoption. Without
such coordination, electing issuers and their boards will be required to negotiate auditor involvement on
a case-by-case basis, with predictable variability in cost, scope, and rigor that will undermine both the
cost-savings rationale of the proposal and the disclosure integrity of the resulting periodic reporting
framework.
Third, the Commission should require electing issuers to disclose, in their Form 10-S and Form 10-K, the
issuer’s practice with respect to off-cycle quarterly market communications, including whether the issuer
issues quarterly earnings releases, whether disclosure committee and audit committee review processes
are applied to those communications, and whether interim review work is performed by the issuer’s
independent registered public accounting firm in connection with those communications. A disclosure
requirement of this kind would not mandate any particular practice. It would require the issuer to state
its practice, which is itself a meaningful disclosure to investors and a meaningful safeguard against the
disclosure liability gap I have described.

VII. Conclusion
The Commission’s objective in proposing optional semiannual reporting is defensible. The proposal would
offer real relief to a subset of reporting companies for whom the quarterly cadence has always been
disproportionate to the operational tempo of the business, and the election structure preserves the
existing quarterly framework as the default for issuers for whom it remains appropriate. My concern is
not with the election. It is with the operational consequences of the hybrid approach the proposing
release contemplates, with the disclosure liability gap that follows from removing the formal quarterly
filing without addressing what supports the continuing quarterly market communications that will, in
practice, accompany the election for most electing issuers, and with the on-ramp question that follows
from the bifurcation the proposal will produce within the public company population.
The framework and recommendations set forth above are intended to be constructive. They accept the
policy choice the Commission has made and address the operational architecture that needs to
accompany it. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss any of the issues raised in this letter with the
staff. I can be reached at the contact information below.
