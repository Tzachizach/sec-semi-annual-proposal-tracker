Comment Statement to the Securities and Exchange Commission
Re: Proposed Amendments to Permit Optional Semiannual Reporting by Public Companies
Release No. 2026-42

Dear Commissioners of the SEC,

I am writing to formally express my opposition to the Commission's proposal (Release No. 2026-42) to permit public companies the option of semiannual reporting via Form 10-S. While I recognize the Commission's intent to alleviate regulatory burdens and mitigate "short-termism," this proposal represents a significant retreat from the transparency and data-frequency standards that have historically made the U.S. capital markets the most trusted and liquid in the world.

My opposition is based on the following primary concerns:

**1. Erosion of Transparency and Price Discovery**
The quarterly reporting cycle (Form 10-Q) provides the essential "heartbeat" of market information. Moving to a six-month reporting interval would create massive informational "dead zones," leading to increased market volatility as investors are forced to rely on speculation rather than verified financial data for half the year. This degradation of information quality will inevitably impair efficient price discovery.

**2. Information Asymmetry and Insider Risk**
Extended reporting gaps provide a structural advantage to insiders and institutional entities with direct access to management, to the profound detriment of retail investors. Widening the window between public disclosures increases the risk of information leakage and heightens the potential for insider trading, undermining the SEC's core mission of investor protection.

**3. Technological Obsolescence of the "Regulatory Burden" Argument**
The proposal's justification — that quarterly reporting is an undue administrative burden — fails to account for the current technological landscape. In 2026, the widespread adoption of Generative AI, automated XBRL integration, and real-time ledger systems has fundamentally transformed financial reporting.
- Reduced Compliance Costs: AI-driven financial reporting tools have significantly lowered the marginal cost and labor required to produce 10-Q filings.
- Modernized Oversight: If a public company possesses the digital infrastructure to compete in the modern market, it possesses the capability to provide quarterly snapshots. To suggest otherwise ignores the efficiencies gained through contemporary enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems.

**4. Market Fragmentation and Comparability Issues**
An "optional" reporting framework will lead to a bifurcated market. Investors will struggle to compare a quarterly-reporting company against a semiannual competitor. This lack of standardization will likely result in a "transparency discount" for semiannual filers, potentially increasing their cost of capital — the exact opposite of the proposal's intended effect.

**5. Failure to Address "Short-Termism"**
There is no empirical evidence that reducing the frequency of financial reporting will cause management to prioritize long-term growth. True long-termism is driven by corporate culture and executive compensation structures, not by withholding material financial information from the public for six months at a time.

**Conclusion**
At a time when technology allows for more transparency, not less, the SEC should be leveraging modern technologies to make disclosures more accessible and timely, rather than permitting companies to retreat into a six-month silence. I urge the Commission to withdraw this proposal and maintain the quarterly reporting standards that ensure a level playing field for all market participants.

Sincerely,
Omer Yousif, PhD
