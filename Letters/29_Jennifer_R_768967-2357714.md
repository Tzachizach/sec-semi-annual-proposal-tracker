# Letter 29 — Jennifer R

To the Securities and Exchange Commission:

I am writing to oppose the Commission's proposed rule permitting public companies to replace quarterly Form 10-Q reporting with semiannual reporting on new Form 10-S.
I understand the stated goal of reducing compliance burdens and giving companies more flexibility. However, the public markets do not exist only for the convenience of reporting companies. They exist because investors, employees, retirees, pension funds, analysts, and the broader public can rely on timely, standardized, and comparable information. Reducing mandatory reporting from quarterly to semiannual would weaken that foundation.

Quarterly reporting is not just an administrative exercise. It is one of the core transparency mechanisms that allows investors to evaluate company performance, monitor risk, compare companies, and detect changes before they become larger problems. Moving to semiannual reporting would create longer periods where material business trends, financial deterioration, operational risks, liquidity concerns, or governance issues may remain less visible to the public.

This proposal would also increase information asymmetry. Large institutional investors, insiders, sophisticated analysts, and market participants with access to alternative data may still find ways to monitor company performance between reports. Ordinary investors will not have the same access. A system that gives public companies less frequent disclosure obligations risks widening the gap between those with privileged or expensive information channels and those relying on public filings.

I am also concerned that reducing reporting frequency could increase the risk of insider trading or perceived unfairness in the market. When companies go six months between required interim reports, insiders and select market participants may have access to meaningful performance information long before the public does. Even if existing rules still require disclosure of certain material events, semiannual reporting would reduce the regular cadence of structured, comprehensive financial disclosure.

The proposal also raises concerns about comparability. If some companies continue quarterly reporting while others move to semiannual reporting, investors may lose a consistent basis for comparing companies across industries. Optional reporting flexibility may sound reasonable, but in practice it could create a fragmented disclosure landscape where companies with weaker performance have an incentive to disclose less frequently.

The Commission's mission includes protecting investors, maintaining fair and efficient markets, and facilitating capital formation. This proposal may reduce costs for some registrants, but those savings should not come at the expense of transparency, investor protection, market confidence, and fair access to information. For these reasons, I urge the Commission not to adopt the proposed semiannual reporting option. Public companies that access U.S. capital markets should continue to provide quarterly Form 10-Q reports as a baseline obligation of public ownership.
