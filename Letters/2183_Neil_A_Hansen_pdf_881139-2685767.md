Dear Ms. Countryman:

As Senior Vice President and Chief Financial Officer of Exxon Mobil Corporation, I
appreciate the opportunity to comment on the Commission’s proposed rule regarding
semiannual reporting on new Form 10-S for domestic registrants. ExxonMobil supports
this proposal and the Commission’s broader efforts to modernize the disclosure
framework in a manner that enhances efficiency, reduces unnecessary complexity, and
eases regulatory burdens, while preserving robust investor protections. We believe the
Commission’s disclosure regime should be principles-based, scalable, and flexible,
recognizing that a one-size-fits-all approach does not reflect the diversity of issuer
profiles, business models, or investor needs across the public markets.

We believe the Commission’s proposal appropriately acknowledges that the public
disclosure ecosystem has evolved significantly since the onset of periodic reporting.
Investors today access and consume information through a wide range of channels
beyond periodic reports, including earnings releases furnished on Form 8-K, investor
presentations, webcasts, and issuer websites. Based on feedback from our investors and
others in the financial community, we observe that substantially more emphasis is placed
on these timelier communications and broader avenues of information than on Form 10-
Q itself. In this respect, the proposal reflects modern market practices and investor
behavior, rather than legacy reporting conventions.

We agree that disclosures should focus on material information that is most relevant and
useful to investor decision-making. The value of interim reporting lies in the quality, clarity,
and relevance of the information provided, rather than frequency alone. Semiannual
reporting, when considered alongside existing voluntary disclosures, Form 8-K
requirements, and the requirements of Regulation FD, preserves the flow of material
information to the market while reducing the time and cost burden associated with the

preparation and review of periodic reports that do not meaningfully enhance investor
understanding.

ExxonMobil is supportive of the Commission’s semiannual reporting proposal and
appreciates its pragmatic approach in retaining the existing interim reporting framework.
Maintaining the same underlying standards that govern current quarterly reporting will
promote consistency, comparability, and operational efficiency for preparers and users.
We believe it represents a thoughtful reassessment of long-standing reporting practices
in the context of currently available information channels, market behavior, and investor
expectations, without compromising long-standing and important investor protections.
Rather than responding to the Commission’s request for comment on a
question-by-question basis, we structured our feedback below around the overarching
themes that we believe are central to the success of this proposal.

Continuing Availability of Timely and Material Information to Investors
As prefaced above, we do not believe a move to semiannual reporting would result in a
reduction of material or timely information available to investors because material
quarterly information is disclosed independently of the Form 10-Qs. Feedback from our
investors and others in the financial community indicates that they increasingly rely on
earnings releases, webcasts, investor presentations, Form 8-K filings, and company
investor relations websites, rather than Form 10-Q itself, as their primary sources of issuer
information. Companies also routinely communicate with the market through a wide range
of other voluntary channels, including public-facing reports and disclosures addressing
comprehensive business and strategic matters. This broader, more dynamic disclosure
ecosystem currently provides investors with continuous access to material developments.
The proposed framework appropriately recognizes that the purpose of periodic
reporting—maintaining reasonably current, publicly-available information—is now
achieved through multiple channels that are more user-friendly and often more effective.

Importantly, this evolution in investor information consumption has not diminished market
transparency, efficiency, or access to information for market participants, whether they
are small or large investors. Existing regulations, particularly Regulation FD and Form 8-
K filing requirements, provide a robust framework for the broad and timely dissemination
of material information. Issuers are strongly incentivized to communicate material
developments promptly to the public to maintain investor confidence and support efficient
market valuation. We expect issuers will continue to evolve their disclosure practices to
meet investor expectations and market demands, irrespective of periodic reporting
frequency.

Accordingly, semiannual reporting would modernize the reporting framework without
compromising investor protection or the quality of information available to the market, and
better align disclosure requirements with how investors access, evaluate, and use
information today. We agree the proposal is a thoughtful and measured approach that
preserves a system designed to produce material, timely, and decision-useful disclosures
to enable well-informed and sound investment decisions while reducing unnecessary
compliance burdens and promoting efficiency for both companies and investors.

Optional, Not Mandatory, Semiannual Reporting to Provide Flexibility
We believe semiannual reporting should be optional rather than mandatory and should
be available to all issuers. An optional framework appropriately recognizes that
companies differ in size, maturity, industry, complexity, investor base, geographic
footprint, and disclosure practices. Some issuers may determine that quarterly reporting
on Form 10-Q remains preferable due to investor expectations, business cyclicality,
capital markets considerations, or other operational factors. Others may determine that
semiannual reporting on Form 10-S better balances their investor needs with reporting
burdens. Allowing issuers to make this determination preserves flexibility and promotes
market-based outcomes without imposing a one-size-fits-all framework. We feel that
each company is in the best position to evaluate the efficiency benefits that ultimately
accrue to shareholders from adopting a semiannual approach relative to the value of
maintaining quarterly reporting.

As mandatory semiannual reporting does not allow companies to make this value
assessment, it could produce unintended consequences, including forcing companies
with diverse investor needs into a uniform approach that may be suboptimal. We therefore
believe investor and issuer considerations, rather than regulatory mandate, should largely
determine the cadence and format of interim reporting.

New Optional Form 8-K Item 8.02 (1Q/3Q Financial Statements) for Further
Flexibility
We recommend that, in connection with a move toward optional semiannual reporting,
the Commission adopt a new Item 8.02 for Form 8-K that would permit issuers to file (not
furnish) first or third-quarter financial statements and related information on an optional,
as-needed basis. This optional mechanism would further enhance flexibility while
addressing potential concerns around timely financial transparency from both issuer and
investor perspectives. It would also address many of the Commission’s thoughtful
questions about quarterly earnings releases and their treatment in a semiannual reporting
regime, which we address in sections further below.

Unlike a full Form 10-Q, the proposed Item 8.02 should be carefully scoped to require
only condensed consolidated financial statements and incremental, decision-useful
updates that are material changes from the issuer’s most recent Form 10-K or Form 10-
S, rather than a comprehensive refresh of all quarterly disclosures required under GAAP.
Eliminating or limiting repeat footnote disclosures and certain MD&A elements—such as
routine liquidity discussions and segment disclosures—would meaningfully reduce
preparer burden and disclosure volume, while preserving investor access to new, material
information. Many of these recurring disclosures provide limited incremental insight on a
quarterly cadence and are largely duplicative of annual reporting, yet they carry
disproportionate cost and complexity. This approach is consistent with our view that as
disclosure requirements have increased over the past several years, it has become more
difficult for investors to focus on what is truly important. To avoid unintentionally triggering
the full suite of ASC 270 interim reporting requirements or creating conflict with existing
GAAP presentation and disclosure frameworks, the Commission could clarify that

                                                                                     3|Page
financial statements filed under this Item are permitted to be presented on a simplified,
updated basis, accompanied by a principles-based requirement to disclose only material
changes or developments. The Item could also expressly distinguish these submissions
from “complete interim financial statements” for GAAP purposes, thereby allowing issuers
to tailor disclosures without implying full compliance with Form 10-Q content
requirements. Structuring the submission as a “filed”, not furnished, Form 8-K item,
consistent with the framework for Item 8.01 and other material event disclosures, would
also allow incorporation by reference into Securities Act registration statements and the
Exchange Act reporting record.

The proposed Item 8.02 would offer a pragmatic, market-responsive solution to potential
concerns around semiannual reporting and is consistent with the Commission’s long-
standing principles-based approach to disclosure, permitting issuers to respond to
changes in their business or markets without regulatory friction. This proposal may also
encourage more issuers to opt-in to the semiannual reporting framework, particularly
during the early stages of adoption, by not unduly constraining reporting flexibility.
Companies frequently encounter events—such as mergers, restructurings, credit rating
changes, or market disruptions—that warrant near-term financial updates. The proposed
Item 8.02 would enable issuers to have the option to provide decision-useful information
when it may be most relevant, rather than deferring disclosure until the next Form 10-S
or Form 10-K. At the same time, it could potentially accommodate contractual obligations,
including debt covenants and indentures tied to quarterly reporting or Section 13 and
15(d) requirements of the Exchange Act—without requiring widespread amendments or
workarounds of financing agreements, providing compliance certainty without full Form
10-Q preparation.

Overall, we believe the proposal strikes a practical balance between flexibility and
investor protection. By coupling optional semiannual reporting with a clear pathway to
disclose material financial updates through filed Item 8.02 reports, the proposal mitigates
concerns about reduced visibility into material financial developments, information
asymmetry between sophisticated and retail investors, and potential increases in cost of
capital under a semiannual reporting regime. Further, the proposal advances the
Commission’s efforts to modernize the periodic reporting architecture to recognize that a
uniform quarterly reporting mandate does not suit the diversity of issuer profiles,
industries, and investor bases, without compromising the furnished earnings release
framework. Together, these measures would deliver meaningful cost savings, preserve
investor protections, and modernize the disclosure system with a flexible, issuer-specific
approach.

Sustained Comparability Across Periods and Peers
We do not believe semiannual reporting would materially impact investors’ ability to
compare same-company or peer performance over time. If we elect semiannual reporting,
we expect to continue providing quarterly financial disclosures through earnings releases
furnished on Form 8-K, investor presentations, postings to the Investors section of our
website, or, if adopted, through new Item 8.02 of Form 8-K, as appropriate. This allows
us to satisfy investor demand, provide financial and operational updates, and address

                                                                                 4|Page
significant developments and events affecting the business. Other issuers that choose
semiannual reporting likely will follow a similar process to provide material information to
the market. These disclosures, combined with annual and semiannual reports, should
largely preserve comparability at both the company-specific and peer levels.

Treatment of Earnings Releases and Form 8-K Item 2.02
We support maintaining the current Item 2.02 framework that permits issuers to furnish
earnings releases on Form 8-K and do not believe a transition to filed status is warranted.
The Commission has long recognized an important and deliberate distinction between
information that is furnished and information that is filed, and earnings announcements
are a clear example of disclosures for which furnished treatment is appropriate. Through
Item 2.02 of Form 8-K, the SEC expressly permits issuers to furnish earnings releases,
reflecting the Commission’s view that these communications serve a different purpose
than formal periodic filings.

The Commission’s furnished information framework promotes robust and candid
disclosure. It appropriately recognizes that earnings releases are management
communications intended to provide timely information to the market. By limiting liability
exposure relative to filed documents, the framework encourages issuers to provide
prompt disclosure, supplemental context, operational insights, and forward-looking
information that investors value, but which might otherwise be constrained or omitted if
earnings releases were required to be filed. This approach aligns with Regulation FD’s
objective of broad and timely dissemination of material information without discouraging
informative communication.

Moreover, the furnished treatment reflects the practical reality that earnings releases often
include a mix of GAAP financial information, non-GAAP measures, estimates, and
forward-looking statements. Elevating these communications to filed status would
increase legal exposure, lengthen preparation timelines, and expand internal and external
review processes, potentially delaying disclosure and reducing the utility of the
information to investors. These outcomes would undermine the intended efficiencies
offered by semiannual reporting and are not aligned with the Commission’s stated
objectives.

In short, by permitting earnings announcements to be furnished rather than filed, the
Commission has appropriately tailored the disclosure framework to the nature and
purpose of these communications. Maintaining this distinction supports efficient,
transparent markets while preserving investor protection and is consistent with the
Commission’s broader objectives of modernizing disclosure requirements and reducing
unnecessary regulatory burden.

Incorporation by Reference Concerns
If the Commission were to require earnings releases to be filed on Form 8-K, we strongly
recommend that such releases not be incorporated by reference into registration
statements, Form 10-K, Form 10-Q, or the new Form 10-S semiannual report. While
earnings releases are subject to disclosure controls and procedures, they are not

                                                                                   5|Page
currently subject to internal control over financial reporting (ICFR). Requiring
incorporation by reference would effectively force issuers to bring earnings release
processes into ICFR scope, increasing cost and complexity, which are contrary to the
proposal’s stated objectives.

In addition, earnings releases typically include non-GAAP measures, preliminary
estimates, forward-looking information, and operational metrics that are commonly used
for investor communication but may not be appropriate or permissible in filed documents
subject to stricter disclosure standards. For example, non-GAAP financial measures
included in an earnings release often rely on adjustment methodologies and presentation
flexibility that align with Regulation G and Item 2.02 of Form 8-K, but would face more
restrictive treatment if incorporated into filed reports. Similarly, qualitative commentary
provided during earnings calls or highlighted on company websites is designed to provide
context rather than to meet the formal requirements of Commission filings.

The current distinctions among public disclosures made through company websites,
earnings calls, furnished Form 8-Ks, and filed documents reflect differing liability
frameworks, levels of formality, and control requirements, and these distinctions are
generally well understood by issuers, investors, and other market participants. For
instance, furnished Form 8-Ks are not subject to the same level of liability under the
Exchange Act as filed reports, and website disclosures and earnings call materials
operate under yet another set of expectations regarding completeness and precision.
Altering these longstanding distinctions—by effectively elevating earnings releases to
filed status or incorporating them by reference into periodic reports—could blur these
boundaries, introduce interpretive uncertainty, and create confusion regarding applicable
disclosure standards, liability exposure, and required levels of internal control

Auditor Review of Financial Information in Earnings Releases
The Commission requested feedback on whether to require an auditor review of financial
information in any first or third quarter earnings releases of an issuer that elects
semiannual reporting. The Commission also asked if an issuer which elects semiannual
reporting would retain its independent public accountant to perform a review of its
financial statements at the end of each quarter.

In this context, we consider the financial statements to be the following five primary
statements: consolidated statements of income, comprehensive income, balance sheet,
cash flows, and changes in equity. We consider financial information in an earnings
release to encompass information presented outside of the financial statements, even if
derived from elements of the financial statements.

We support permitting issuers that elect semiannual reporting to determine, based on
their facts and circumstances, whether to obtain an independent public accounting firm’s
review of first- or third-quarter financial statements included in earnings releases. Allowing
issuer optionality in this area is consistent with the Commission’s objective of reducing
compliance burden while preserving investor protection through transparency.



                                                                                    6|Page
Our independent public accounting firm does perform a review of our interim financial
statements that are included in our Form 10-Q but does not audit or review our earnings
releases and does not provide assurance on those communications. The firm’s
involvement is generally limited to reading the earnings release to consider whether it
contains information that is materially inconsistent with the audited financial statements
or information obtained during the audit. This limited involvement does not constitute a
formal review or provide any level of assurance.

Earnings releases are management communications that summarize results and trends
– they are not prepared or labeled as reviewed or audited financial statements. As
discussed in the preceding sections, earnings releases typically communicate a broad
spectrum of information, such as US-GAAP compliant financial information, non-GAAP
measures, operational detail, projections and forecasts – including updates to such
previously issued information – and other forward-looking statements. In addition to
increasing a company’s compliance cost, requiring an auditor review of financial
information contained in these communications would significantly expand the scope of
an auditor’s responsibilities and likely necessitate a revision to the current assurance
standards as promulgated by the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board. A review
requirement would be misaligned with the current regulatory framework, under which
financial information not filed with the Commission is not subject to auditor assurance.

As previously noted, while earnings releases are subject to disclosure controls and
procedures, they are not typically within the scope of ICFR, and we do not believe ICFR
should extend to the earnings release process. Expanding ICFR to encompass earnings
releases would introduce unnecessary complexity by requiring additional controls,
documentation, testing, and audit coordination around processes that are designed to be
timely and responsive to market expectations. Such an expansion would reintroduce
much of the burden that semiannual reporting is intended to alleviate, diminish the
operational flexibility of the earnings release process, increase compliance costs, and
create timing pressures that are not aligned with its purpose.

We support the long-standing requirement for an auditor’s review of interim financial
statements when an issuer files a Form 10-Q. Furthermore, if the Commission were to
adopt our aforementioned proposal for the new optional Item 8.02 of Form 8-K, we believe
a mandatory auditor review of the five primary financial statements is appropriate. In that
circumstance, an auditor review remains an important component of the overall disclosure
framework and provides investors with additional assurance regarding the reliability of
interim financial statements prepared in accordance with Regulation S-X.

Access to Capital Markets and Financial Statement Staleness
Under the current framework, financial statement staleness is governed by Rule 3-12 of
Regulation S-X, which deems financial statements “stale” once they exceed specified age
thresholds, generally 130 days for accelerated filers and 135 days for other filers. This
framework applies uniformly to registration statements, including those on Form S-3, and
to subsequent offerings conducted under those registrations.



                                                                                 7|Page
The Commission correctly identified that the existing framework, which is built around a
quarterly reporting cadence on Form 10-Q filings to refresh incorporated financial
information and prevent staleness, would render semiannual reporting impractical if left
unrevised. We support the Commission’s proposal to consolidate Rule 3-12 into proposed
Rule 3-01 to synchronize the age of financial statement requirements with the cadence
selected by the issuer.

However, while the proposed amendments resolve the threshold question of whether a
registration statement can satisfy the regulatory age requirements, they do not address
the separate constraint arising from PCAOB Auditing Standard AS 6101. PCAOB AS
6101 limits an auditor’s ability to provide negative assurance in a comfort letter to
underwriters beyond 135 days from the most recently audited or reviewed period. The
restriction imposed by AS 6101 becomes particularly acute for issuers that rely on shelf
registration to access the capital markets on demand. A shelf registration is designed to
provide continuous, on-demand access to capital markets, but that access will likely be
impaired absent an alignment of AS 6101 to the Commission’s proposal of a semiannual
reporting cadence. The Commission recognized that “an investor in a registrant that is a
semiannual filer may not receive interim financial statements that are as current as would
be required today." This gap, which may extend for several months between required
semiannual filings, creates meaningful execution risk for shelf takedowns and could
increase the cost of capital.

The Commission directly asks whether "reporting companies that elect to become
semiannual filers [would] nonetheless decide to include quarterly or more recent financial
information in Securities Act registration statements or prospectuses based on market
practices or liability concerns." The Commission could address this concern through our
proposal for a new Item 8.02 of Form 8-K to allow semiannual reporting issuers the option
to file quarterly financial statements.

Further, the Commission should confirm that an issuer that voluntarily files interim
financial information between required semiannual filings through an Item 8.02 of Form
8-K does not thereby alter its reporting obligations or create an expectation of continued
voluntary disclosure at the same frequency. This safe harbor would preserve the voluntary
nature of any supplemental disclosure while giving issuers flexibility to respond to
dynamic market demands without legal risk and support capital markets formation.

Considerations Related to Indenture and Debt Agreements
Our existing indentures generally include two types of interim reporting provisions. First,
certain indentures require the Company to furnish periodic reports in accordance with
Sections 13 or 15(d) of the Exchange Act. These provisions are inherently dynamic and
would continue to operate as intended under a revised SEC reporting framework, as they
incorporate the Commission’s reporting requirements as they evolve. As a result, these
provisions would not be expected to require modification in connection with a transition
to semiannual reporting.




                                                                                 8|Page
Second, certain other indentures include more prescriptive covenants that require the
delivery of interim financial statements within a specified number of days following the
end of a fiscal period, often tied to a quarterly balance sheet date. These provisions would
not automatically conform to a semiannual reporting model and, accordingly, would
require an amendment to align with a revised reporting cadence. Updating these
agreements would entail an administrative effort, including legal analysis, coordination
with trustees, and, in some cases, execution of supplemental indentures or consents.

While we acknowledge that these revisions would involve time and cost, we believe such
efforts are manageable and do not outweigh the broader benefits of permitting
semiannual reporting. Further, if the Commission adopts our proposed Item 8.02, this may
provide issuers and creditors with an alternative mechanism to satisfy specific periodic
reporting requirements contained in the indentures without sacrificing progress on the
semiannual initiative. In our view, the flexibility and efficiency gains associated with a
semiannual reporting option would justify the necessary updates to legacy agreements,
particularly given that the affected provisions represent a discrete and addressable subset
of existing indenture requirements.

Advancing Semiannual Reporting Concurrent with a Path to Broader Modernization
We support the Commission’s continued advancement of its proposal to permit
semiannual reporting, while preserving flexibility for issuers to elect a reporting cadence
that aligns with their business models, investor needs, and cost-benefit considerations.

At the same time, we encourage the Commission to undertake a broader, holistic review
of existing Forms 10-K and 10-Q disclosure requirements to enhance relevance and
reduce unnecessary complexity, and to extend these to the new Form 10-S if adopted.
Modernization efforts should emphasize a principles-based, materiality-focused
framework that reduces reliance on prescriptive line-item requirements, which often result
in boilerplate disclosures rather than meaningful insight. This could include eliminating
duplicative disclosures across sections—such as overlap between the Business
description, Risk Factors, and MD&A—and refining MD&A to focus more clearly on
material drivers of performance, known trends, and forward-looking considerations
instead of repetitive, period-over-period narrative.

Additionally, the Commission could improve disclosure quality by removing or updating
low-utility/outdated requirements that no longer provide meaningful value to investors,
including detailed physical property listings, certain supplemental oil and gas schedules,
and legacy tabular disclosures that are costly to maintain but rarely decision-useful. A
thoughtful recalibration along these lines would sharpen the focus of periodic reporting
on information that is material and decision-useful, while reducing compliance burden and
improving readability. Taken together with a flexible approach to reporting frequency,
these changes would better align the SEC’s disclosure framework with the needs of
modern capital markets and investors.

Given the scope and complexity of a full Form 10-K and 10-Q modernization effort,
conditioning semiannual reporting on completion of broader reforms would delay

                                                                                  9|Page
meaningful near-term progress. The semiannual proposal is already well-developed, has
benefited from market engagement, and is positioned for timely implementation.
Accordingly, we support a sequenced approach: proceed with adoption of the semiannual
reporting option now, while continuing the Commission’s ongoing review of Regulation
S-K and undertaking further modernization initiatives on a parallel, but independent,
timeline.

Filing Deadline Consistent with Current Form 10-Q
We believe the filing deadline for a semiannual report should align with the current Form
10-Q deadlines—45 days after period-end for non-accelerated filers and 40 days after
period-end for accelerated and large accelerated filers. Consistent deadlines promote
comparability, reduce implementation complexity, and avoid pressuring issuers to modify
processes in ways that could compromise quality or internal controls while increasing
cost.

Expected Reduction in Compliance Costs and Regulatory Burdens
We believe providing issuers with the option to elect semiannual reporting offers a
meaningful opportunity to reduce compliance costs and allow for more efficient resource
deployment. While the precise magnitude of these efficiencies will vary by issuer, we
expect tangible reductions in the time and effort required from employees, management,
the Board of Directors, and our independent registered public accounting firm in
preparing, reviewing, and filing interim reports. Although certain foundational activities—
such as maintaining data integrity, executing disclosure controls, and sustaining effective
ICFR—will appropriately continue throughout the year, the overall cadence for required
SEC reporting would be significantly more efficient. As a result, we expect a net positive
impact, with reduced reporting frequency driving clearer prioritization, lower incremental
effort, and more efficient governance processes.

More broadly, semiannual reporting would further enable management to focus on core
business execution, long-term strategy, and shareholder value creation. Combined with
the continued use of quarterly earnings releases furnished on Form 8-K and disclosures
under Regulation FD, this approach can preserve timely and useful flow of information to
investors while reducing duplicative or low-value narrative. With appropriate
implementation and continued availability of key financial information, we believe issuers
can achieve meaningful efficiency gains without compromising transparency, investor
confidence, or access to capital markets.

Limited Impact on Insider Trading / Blackout Periods
We do not anticipate that semiannual reporting will result in meaningful changes or
impacts on our insider trading policy and guidelines, trading windows, or blackout periods
as these practices are structured around quarterly earnings releases rather than the
timing of Form 10-Q filings. As previously noted, if we elect semiannual reporting, we
expect to continue providing quarterly financial disclosures through earnings releases
furnished on Form 8-K. Consequently, we do not expect semiannual reporting to increase
insider trading risk or necessitate enhancements to our policies.

Transition Considerations
If the proposal is adopted, we believe no formal transition period is necessary, provided
issuers have a full calendar quarter to prepare. For example, if a final rule is published
and effective by September 30, an issuer should be able to determine—by the time it files
its Form 10-K for that year—whether it will elect semiannual reporting in the following
fiscal year. We also believe that such a timeline provides sufficient notice to investors of
the issuer’s intention.
