Subject: S7-2026-15: Webform Comments from Henggao Cai
From: Henggao Cai

I am submitting this comment as an individual investor and member of the public. I write in strong opposition to this proposal. While framed as a modernization of an outdated framework, this rule would, in practice, reduce the flow of standardized financial information that retail investors depend on to make decisions and it would do so for the primary benefit of corporate management teams, not the investors the Commission is charged with protecting.

The SEC's core mandate under the Securities Exchange Act is to protect investors, maintain fair and orderly markets, and facilitate capital formation. This proposal inverts that priority. It privileges corporate convenience over investor access, and it does so without credible evidence that quarterly reporting is actually causing the harms its critics claim.

The short-term argument does not hold up. The central justification for this rule is that quarterly reporting forces managers into short-term decision-making. This argument has been repeated for years by executives and lobbyists, but the empirical record does not support it. Academic research examining companies that shifted to semiannual reporting in the UK and EU found no meaningful improvement in long-term investment behavior. What it did find was increased stock price volatility, investor overreaction in the absence of own-firm disclosures, and overcorrection when semiannual reports finally arrived. The Commission is proposing to restructure a 50-year disclosure regime based on a premise that peer-reviewed research has already substantially undermined.

Retail investors will bear the cost of this rule. Institutional investors, hedge funds, and company insiders do not depend on quarterly 10-Q filings the way retail investors do. They have access to earnings calls, analyst relationships, roadshows, and proprietary data services. Retail investors, the people this agency exists to protect, rely on standardized, mandatory disclosures as their primary window into how a company is actually performing. Doubling the gap between those disclosures from three months to six creates a structural information advantage for sophisticated participants and leaves ordinary investors operating on assumptions for longer periods. This is not a theoretical concern. It is a predictable and documented outcome.

The Form 8-K backstop is not an adequate substitute. The proposal appears to rely on current-event 8-K filings to bridge the information gap between semiannual reports. But 8-K filings are management-discretionary, non-standardized, and cannot substitute for the systematic financial data, such as revenue, earnings, cash flow, balance sheet changes that Form 10-Q requires. A company that elects semiannual reporting could go six months without giving investors a standardized, auditable view of its financial condition, entirely within the rules as proposed.

The "optional" framing obscures the aggregate harm. Because this is presented as a voluntary election, proponents argue that market forces will discipline companies that reduce disclosure. That argument fails for several reasons. First, retail investors often cannot easily identify which companies have switched to semiannual reporting or understand the implications when they do. Second, the companies most likely to elect semiannual reporting are smaller, less-covered firms where information is already scarce, which is precisely where retail investors are most exposed. Third, once semiannual reporting becomes normalized, investor expectations will adjust downward and the transparency premium will erode.

I urge the Commission to withdraw this proposal. If the Commission is genuinely concerned about compliance costs for smaller issuers, there are targeted ways to address that and streamlining the content requirements of Form 10-Q, extending filing deadlines for non-accelerated filers, or providing technical assistance without eliminating the quarterly disclosure cadence that has underpinned market confidence for over half a century.

Flexibility is not a regulatory virtue when the costs fall on those with the least access to alternative information. I ask the Commission to reject this rule and reaffirm its commitment to investor protection as its primary obligation.

Thank you for considering this comment.
Henggao Cai
