Subject: File Number S7-2026-15 — Technical Assessment and Opposition to Proposed Semiannual
Reporting Framework
Dear Secretary Countryman,
I am writing to formally log my opposition to the Commission’s rule proposal to permit public issuers
the option to transition from quarterly financial reporting to a semiannual reporting framework on
Form 10-S. While acknowledging the Commission's intent to mitigate short-termism and lower
corporate compliance costs, empirical evidence and economic principles suggest this framework will
degrade market efficiency, elevate cost of capital, and exacerbate information asymmetry.
The introduction of a semiannual reporting cycle creates systemic risks across several critical
vectors:
●​ Erosion of the U.S. Market Gold Standard and Foreign Investment: The U.S. capital markets are
globally recognized as the gold standard precisely because of our rigorous, transparent, and
frequent disclosure regimes. Investors worldwide allocate capital to U.S. equities because they
trust the integrity and timeliness of the data. Lowering our standards to match the less
frequent reporting cycles of international markets will homogenize our ecosystem, strip away
our competitive advantage, and ultimately diminish the attractiveness of U.S. markets to vital
foreign direct investment.
●​ Compounding Operational and Supply Chain Risks: In a highly interconnected global economy,
operational disruptions—such as severe supply chain bottlenecks, inflationary pressures, or
sudden macroeconomic shifts—develop rapidly. A six-month reporting gap ensures that these
material risks will compound entirely hidden from public view. Instead of the market making
orderly, continuous price discoveries based on quarterly updates, investors will be exposed to
severe, delayed material shocks that disrupt portfolio risk management.
●​ Information Asymmetry and Bid-Ask Spreads: In the fields of financial economics and market
microstructure, reducing reporting frequency directly increases information asymmetry.

Institutional market participants possess the capital to acquire proprietary, alternative data
streams to bridge a six-month information vacuum, whereas individual retail investors do not.
Consequently, market makers will likely widen bid-ask spreads to protect against adverse
selection, increasing transaction costs and reducing liquidity for the broader market.
●​ Delayed Price Discovery and Volatility Clustering: Quarterly reporting facilitates continuous,
incremental price discovery. Extending the reporting interval to six months will cause material
structural data to compound behind a curtain of corporate confidentiality. Consequently, when
earnings are finally disclosed, the price adjustment process will be discrete rather than
continuous. This will result in heightened idiosyncratic volatility and severe price gaps upon
announcement, destabilizing secondary market trading.
●​ Insufficiency of Form 8-K as an Interim Substitute: The proposal relies on voluntary, interim
financial disclosures furnished via Form 8-K to maintain market communication. This
mechanism is structurally flawed. Disclosures under Item 2.02 or 7.01 lack the standardized
presentation, mandatory GAAP reconciliations, and strict disclosure controls and procedures
(DCP) inherent to Form 10-Q. Relying on fragmented, unstandardized updates diminishes
comparability across peers and increases the cognitive processing burden on market analysts.
●​ Agency Costs and Corporate Governance: Quarterly reporting acts as a vital commitment
mechanism that aligns manager and shareholder incentives. Extending the reporting window
increases the duration of unmonitored management discretion. This lag delays the market’s
ability to identify capital misallocation, operational drift, or deteriorating internal controls,
thereby elevating agency costs and eroding governance discipline.
The proposed reduction in regulatory costs is heavily outweighed by the negative externalities
imposed on capital allocation efficiency, market transparency, and global competitiveness. I strongly
encourage the Commission to preserve the current mandated quarterly disclosure regime under
Exchange Act Rules 13a-13 and 15d-13.
Thank you for analyzing these technical considerations.
Sincerely,
Anthony Langenstein
