To the Securities and Exchange Commission: 

I am writing as an individual retail investor to oppose the proposed shift from quarterly to semiannual financial reporting under Rule S7-2026-15. 

I rely on quarterly earnings reports to make informed decisions about the stocks and funds in my retirement portfolio. A six-month gap in financial disclosures would leave me — and millions of investors like me — flying blind while corporate insiders, institutional investors, and executives continue to have full visibility into their companies' financial health. That information asymmetry is fundamentally unfair and undermines the level playing field that the SEC exists to protect. 

The proposal is framed as voluntary, but I'm skeptical this will stay that way. Once large companies begin filing semiannually, competitive pressure will push smaller companies to follow, and reduced disclosure will quietly become the new norm. The supposed compliance savings accrue to companies and their executives — not to the shareholders whose retirement savings bear all the risk of being less informed. 

I'm also concerned about the fraud risk. Quarterly disclosures create regular checkpoints that make it harder to conceal deteriorating financials. Longer gaps give bad actors more runway. The collapses of Enron and WorldCom are cautionary tales about what happens when opacity grows. 

The SEC's mandate is to protect the investing public. I urge the Commission to keep quarterly reporting requirements in place. 

Respectfully, 
Theresa Condict 
Woodstock, CT
