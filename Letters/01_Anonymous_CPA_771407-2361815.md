# Letter 01 — Anonymous, CPA, Corporate Accounting, Retail Investor

- **Date:** 2026-05-08
- **Role/Affiliation:** CPA, retail investor; Big 4 + Fortune 500 background
- **Stance:** Oppose

Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Anonymous - Concerned retail investor and CPA

As a longtime moderately conservative retail investor who has consistently supported efforts to reduce regulatory burdens, eliminate wasteful "woke" mandates like expansive ESG disclosures, and restore free-market principles to American capital markets, I have submitted comments in the past urging the Commission to roll back overreach from prior administrations. I believe strongly that smarter, lighter-touch regulation would unleash innovation, encourage IPOs, and benefit companies, workers, and investors alike. However, I am strongly opposed to this specific proposal, which I view as a dangerous and misguided step.

Having started my career in the Big 4 and spent over a decade in various accounting and financial reporting roles across Fortune 500 companies, I have witnessed firsthand how rigorous, timely disclosures promote market trust to both financial institutions and analysts, as well as regular retail investors like myself. Yet this proposal leaves me profoundly disappointed, and increasingly jaded. What should have been a principled deregulatory measure has instead devolved into something far worse: blatant political cronyism that prioritizes executive convenience and presidential directives over core investor protections. After years of fighting against ideologically driven rules that politicized corporate disclosures, I never expected to see a Republican-led Commission deliver a gift-wrapped exemption that so clearly undermines market transparency and tilts the field against everyday retail investors. I fear this blatant favoritism will similarly disillusion the next generation of young professionals entering finance and accounting, who will see regulatory integrity sacrificed for political expediency.

The central problem remains unchanged: permitting companies to opt for semiannual reporting on Form 10-S inequality and invites hidden risks, earnings management, and delayed bad news — precisely the kind of opacity that harms the very Main Street investors conservatives claim to champion.

The timing and substance make the cronyism unmistakable. President Trump personally and repeatedly called for ending quarterly reporting. Mere weeks into Chairman Paul Atkins' tenure, the Commission has fast-tracked exactly that policy. The rhetoric of "flexibility" and "long-term thinking" cannot disguise what this is: regulatory sycophancy. An agency that once stood for investor protection has subordinated its statutory mandate to score political points. Having opposed the previous administration's weaponization of the SEC for climate activism and social agendas, I am no less troubled when the pendulum swings to favoritism toward corporate insiders who happen to align with the current White House. True conservatism demands consistent principles - not selective exemptions that serve connected interests.
