Re: File No. S7-2026-15 — Semiannual Reporting (Proposed Rule)


Dear Secretary Countryman:


I am a Certified Public Accountant with eleven years of practice specializing in
partnership taxation, complex syndicated structures, and high-net-worth client advisory
work. I submit these comments in my individual professional capacity, in response to
the Commission's invitation for public comment on the proposed amendments to allow
optional semiannual interim reporting on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reporting on
Form 10-Q.
I oppose the adoption of the proposed rule as presented. My opposition is not to the
concept of reducing unnecessary compliance burden on reporting companies. It is to
the specific mechanism chosen and to the predictable pattern of who will elect it, and at
whose expense.

I. The Framing of This Proposal as a Burden-Reduction Measure Obscures Its
Investor Protection Costs
The proposing release frames optional semiannual reporting primarily as a compliance
cost reduction for reporting companies. That framing is accurate as far as it goes. What
it does not adequately address is the asymmetric distribution of who absorbs the cost of
that reduction.
Quarterly reporting under Form 10-Q is not merely an accounting exercise. It is one of
the few remaining structural mechanisms through which the investing public, not
institutional analysts with direct management access, not sell-side desks with
proprietary data feeds, obtains standardized, filed, legally accountable financial
information about the companies whose securities they hold.
The Commission asks in its economic analysis whether semiannual reporting would
meaningfully affect the information environment for different classes of investors. The
answer, in my professional judgment, is yes, and not symmetrically. Institutional
investors have the resources, access, and analytical capacity to construct a reasonable
picture of a company's financial condition from earnings releases, management
guidance, 8-K filings, and alternative data sources. Retail investors largely do not. For
retail participants, the Form 10-Q is the disclosure, not a supplement to it.
Reducing the frequency of that disclosure from three times annually to once does not
eliminate the information gap between institutional and retail investors. It widens it.

II. The "Optional" Structure Will Not Produce Neutral Outcomes
The Commission has characterized the proposal as optional, with each company
electing the reporting frequency that best serves its circumstances and investors. In
theory, this is neutral. In practice, it is not.
The Commission's own analysis identifies smaller, development-stage, and speculative-
growth companies as the likely adopters of semiannual reporting, specifically because
quarterly reporting imposes relatively higher fixed costs on these companies relative to
their size, and because their investors are characterized as focused on non-financial
milestones rather than interim financial results.
This characterization should concern the Commission. Smaller and development-stage
companies are precisely the issuers in which retail investors are most concentrated and
least protected by alternative information sources. These are not the companies with
robust analyst coverage, active investor relations programs, or frequent management
access for large shareholders. They are the companies whose retail shareholders are
most dependent on filed financial disclosures as their primary source of material
information.
An optional rule that predictably concentrates in the issuer segment where retail
investors are most exposed is not a neutral flexibility measure. It is a structural
reduction in the information rights of the investors least positioned to compensate.

III. Earnings Releases Are Not an Adequate Substitute for Filed Quarterly Reports
The Commission acknowledges in the proposing release that companies electing
semiannual reporting may continue to issue quarterly earnings releases, and asks
whether this practice would mitigate investor information concerns. I submit that it would
not, and that this question reflects a conflation of furnished disclosure with filed
disclosure that warrants direct address.
Quarterly earnings releases are furnished, not filed. They are not subject to the same
liability framework as Form 10-Q. A company that furnishes an earnings release and
later files a Form 10-S that tells a materially different story has not violated the same set
of obligations as a company that files a deficient quarterly report. The accountant review
requirement, which currently applies to interim financial statements included in Form 10-
Q under PCAOB AS 4105, does not apply to furnished earnings releases.
Permitting a semiannual filer to satisfy its interim disclosure obligations through
voluntary, unreviewed, furnished releases, while characterizing the total disclosure
environment as substantially equivalent to quarterly reporting, overstates the investor
protection value of that arrangement. The legal accountability structure is not
equivalent. The quality assurance framework is not equivalent. Framing voluntary
practice as a substitute for mandatory obligation is not a sound basis for rule adoption.
There is a further consequence of reduced disclosure frequency that the proposing
release does not adequately address: the concentration of material nonpublic
information exposure into longer and fewer windows. Under the current quarterly
framework, the period during which insiders possess material information that has not
yet been publicly filed is bounded by a 40 or 45-day reporting deadline. Semiannual
reporting doubles that window by definition. For companies that also cease issuing
quarterly earnings releases upon electing semiannual reporting, the extension of that
window is not merely administrative, it creates a structurally longer period during which
the gap between what insiders know and what the market can price is at its maximum.
The practical consequences include longer mandatory blackout periods, increased
complexity in administering Rule 10b5-1 trading plans, and heightened Regulation FD
exposure at investor conferences and analyst meetings occurring in the months
between filed disclosures. These are not theoretical concerns for practitioners advising
reporting companies. They are the operational reality of a reduced-cadence disclosure
environment, and they fall asymmetrically on retail investors who have no alternative
channel through which to access the information insiders hold.

IV. The Foreign Private Issuer Comparison Does Not Support Generalization
Proponents of this proposal have cited the experience of foreign private issuers, which
are not subject to Form 10-Q requirements and operate on semiannual interim
reporting, as evidence that reduced reporting frequency is compatible with functional
capital markets.
This comparison has significant limitations. Foreign private issuers operate within home-
country regulatory frameworks that in many cases provide structural investor protections
not present in the U.S. system for the issuer categories most likely to elect semiannual
reporting here. The applicable disclosure regimes, liability standards, shareholder rights
structures, and alternative information environments differ materially. Drawing a direct
equivalence between the FPI experience and the likely experience of U.S. domestic
small-cap and development-stage issuers electing Form 10-S does not follow from the
comparative evidence.

V. The Public Trust Dimension
I want to raise a concern that falls outside the Commission's standard cost-benefit
framework but is, in my view, material to the policy judgment here.
Public markets function on the premise that participation is accessible to the public, not
just to participants with the resources, access, and analytical infrastructure to construct
their own information picture independent of mandatory disclosure. The Commission's
periodic reporting requirements are part of the structural infrastructure that supports that
premise. They are not merely compliance costs. They are part of what makes a public
market public.
At a moment when retail investor participation in public markets is at historically high
levels, and when public confidence in the fairness of those markets is not. The
Commission should be cautious about rule changes that structurally reduce the
information available to the investors least equipped to compensate through private
means. The compliance cost savings to issuers are real. The cost to the information
environment of retail investors is also real, and it falls on a different population than the
one that captures the benefit.
This is not an argument that compliance costs do not matter. It is an argument that the
Commission should weigh that trade-off explicitly, rather than treating investor
protection concerns as adequately addressed by the voluntary continuation of practices
that are structurally and legally inferior to the mandatory disclosures they would replace.

VI. Reduced Interim Controls Cadence in an Era of AI-Assisted Financial
Reporting
The Commission's proposal arrives at a moment when AI-assisted tools are being
deployed at scale across financial reporting functions: in reconciliations, variance
analysis, disclosure drafting, and internal control monitoring. This context is material to
the controls cadence question, and I submit that it weighs against reducing interim
reporting frequency.
Section 302 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act requires CEO and CFO certification of financial
statements and disclosure controls at each interim and annual filing. Under the current
quarterly framework, that certification cycle occurs three times annually before the
annual Form 10-K. It functions not only as a legal accountability mechanism but as a
forcing function: it requires management to affirmatively evaluate the integrity of
financial reporting processes on a regular, defined cadence. Reducing that cadence to
once annually, with a single interim checkpoint, does not eliminate the statutory
obligation. It materially reduces the frequency with which that obligation requires active
management attention.
This matters more, not less, as AI-assisted tools take on larger roles in financial
reporting workflows. The reliability of AI-assisted processes is not static. Model behavior
can degrade between evaluation points through updates, configuration changes, or drift
in the underlying data environment without generating the kind of visible failure signal
that would prompt unscheduled management review. A quarterly certification cadence
creates regular, mandatory human verification checkpoints in a process that is
otherwise increasingly automated. Semiannual reporting removes two of those
checkpoints per year.
This is not a theoretical concern. Documented failures in AI-assisted processes,
including in high-stakes professional contexts, share a common feature: the failure
accumulated between evaluation points, in the intervals where no mandatory human
review was required. Reducing the number of those intervals in financial reporting does
not reduce the risk of AI-assisted error. It extends the window in which such errors can
propagate undetected before a required certification forces review.
The Commission's proposal does not address this dynamic. The proposing release
does not analyze the interaction between reduced reporting frequency and the
expanding role of automated tools in financial reporting processes. I urge the
Commission to solicit comment specifically on this question before finalizing any rule,
and to consider whether companies electing semiannual reporting should be required to
disclose the extent to which AI-assisted tools are used in financial reporting functions,
and what human verification controls are in place in the absence of the quarterly
certification cadence.

VII. Disclosure Quality, the Transparency Premium, and Foreign Capital
Allocation
The Commission frames this proposal as a competitiveness measure, reducing
compliance burden to encourage more companies to go and remain public, thereby
strengthening U.S. capital markets relative to international peers. I submit that this
framing inverts the actual competitiveness dynamic and that the Commission has not
adequately analyzed the risk that reduced disclosure quality could impair, rather than
enhance, the ability of U.S. public markets to attract and retain foreign institutional
capital.
U.S. public equity markets have historically commanded a valuation premium relative to
comparable international markets. That premium is not solely a function of economic
size or liquidity. It reflects, in part, a disclosure infrastructure: quarterly GAAP financials,
accountant review, SOX certification, and EDGAR accessibility that provides foreign
institutional allocators with a standardized, legally accountable information environment
they cannot replicate through private channels. That infrastructure is part of what makes
U.S. equities a default allocation for global capital, particularly at the institutional level.
The proposal does not eliminate that infrastructure for quarterly filers. But it introduces a
class of domestic issuers for whom that infrastructure is materially reduced and it does
so without any disclosure requirement that would allow a foreign institutional allocator to
identify, at the portfolio level, which of their U.S. equity holdings operate under the
reduced framework. An allocator running a U.S. small-cap sleeve would need to check
the cover page of each issuer's most recent Form 10-K to determine whether that issuer
is subject to quarterly or semiannual reporting obligations. That is not a workable
disclosure environment for cross-border capital allocation at scale.
The Commission's proposing release does not analyze this dynamic. It does not
estimate the share of foreign institutional capital currently allocated to domestic issuers
in the size and stage categories most likely to elect semiannual reporting, nor does it
model the reallocation risk if those issuers are perceived as operating under a
structurally weaker disclosure standard than their international counterparts in
comparable markets. Treasury desks at reporting companies have already identified
this risk directly: the operative concern is whether investors will continue to purchase
securities from issuers whose most recent filed financial statements are six months old
at the time of a capital markets transaction. That concern is not hypothetical. It is the
question debt and equity underwriters will have to answer for every semiannual filer that
accesses the capital markets.
The Commission should not assume that reducing compliance burden on issuers
translates automatically into stronger markets. If the disclosure reduction causes even a
modest reallocation of foreign institutional capital away from the issuer categories most
likely to elect semiannual reporting, the net effect on capital formation in those
categories may be negative, not positive. That analysis is absent from the proposing
release, and the Commission should conduct it before finalizing any rule.

VIII. Index Standard Fragmentation as a Leading Indicator
The Commission's proposal arrives against a backdrop of accelerating fragmentation in
the standards applied by major index providers to listing eligibility and index inclusion:
fragmentation that is itself producing the kind of retail investor comparability failure the
Commission's disclosure rules exist to prevent. This fragmentation is directly relevant to
the question of what semiannual reporting will mean in practice for investors trying to
understand the composition of the index products they hold.
The recent SpaceX IPO process illustrates the dynamic. When SpaceX and its advisers
sought expedited inclusion in major U.S. equity indexes, index providers responded
differently. Nasdaq amended its Fast Entry rule to allow newly listed large-cap
companies to join the Nasdaq-100 within fifteen trading days of listing; a change that did
not exist before this transaction and was adopted specifically to accommodate the
listing. S&P Global declined to make an equivalent change to its S&P 500 inclusion
criteria. The result is that the same company will be present in some index products and
absent from others for an extended period, not because of any difference in the
company's underlying characteristics, but because the standards applied by competing
index providers are no longer uniform.
The relevance to this rulemaking is structural, not company-specific. Index providers are
already operating under divergent eligibility standards in response to commercial and
political pressure. The Commission's proposal would layer a second dimension of
divergence on top of that: some issuers in a given index will be quarterly filers and some
will be semiannual filers, with no standardized disclosure to index-fund investors about
which holdings operate under which framework. A retail investor holding an ETF that
tracks an index containing both quarterly and semiannual filers has no reliable basis for
assessing the currency of the financial information underlying their exposure to each
constituent. They are not equipped to investigate the cover page of each holding's most
recent Form 10-K to make that determination.
The Commission's disclosure regime is supposed to create a standardized information
environment that allows retail investors to participate in public markets on terms that do
not require the analytical infrastructure of institutional allocators. When index providers
fragment their eligibility standards and the Commission simultaneously fragments its
disclosure cadence requirements, the cumulative effect is a public market that is
increasingly legible only to participants with the resources to navigate the divergence.
That is not a stronger market. It is a less public one.
The Commission should consider whether the semiannual reporting proposal, in the
current index market environment, requires affirmative standardization measures,
including prominent, machine-readable disclosure of reporting frequency at the index
constituent level, before any rule reducing disclosure uniformity is finalized.

IX. Conclusion
I urge the Commission not to adopt the proposed rule in its current form. If the
Commission concludes that some form of reduced-frequency reporting is appropriate, I
recommend the following modifications be considered:
First, eligibility for semiannual reporting should be conditioned on the absence of retail
investor concentration: For example, limited to issuers with institutional ownership
above a defined threshold, or to issuers with market capitalizations above a level
consistent with robust analyst coverage.
Second, any earnings releases issued in lieu of Form 10-Q filings by semiannual filers
should be required to be filed rather than merely furnished, and should be subject to the
same accountant review requirements that currently apply to Form 10-Q interim
financial statements.
Third, the Commission should require disclosure in both the election mechanism on
Form 10-K and in any investor-facing communication that the company has elected
semiannual reporting and that interim financial information will not be subject to
mandatory quarterly filing requirements during the applicable fiscal year.
The Commission's mandate includes investor protection. That mandate is not satisfied
by characterizing reduced disclosure as flexibility, or by treating voluntary furnished
practice as equivalent to mandatory filed obligation. I respectfully ask the Commission
to address these concerns before proceeding to a final rule.
