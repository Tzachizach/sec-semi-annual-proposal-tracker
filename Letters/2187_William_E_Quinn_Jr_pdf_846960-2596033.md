RE: Comments on Proposed Rule and Form Amendments: Optional Semiannual
Reporting for Public Companies (File No. S7-2026-15)

Dear Ms. Countryman,

I am writing to as the Executive Director for the Ohio Association of Professional Fire
Fighters Deferred Compensation 457(b) Plan. On behalf of the 14,000+ firefighters,
paramedics and rescue professionals in Ohio, I’d like to express our strong opposition to
the Securities and Exchange Commission’s (the "Commission" or "SEC") proposed rule and
form amendments that would permit public companies to voluntarily opt out of traditional
quarterly reporting on Form 10-Q in favor of a semiannual framework via the proposed
Form 10-S.

While I appreciate the Commission’s desire to reduce regulatory burdens and inject
flexibility for small or unique issuers, a six-month reporting gap introduces severe
information asymmetry into the public capital markets. In particular, this shift undermines
the foundational fiduciary responsibilities of plan sponsors, trustees, and advisors
managing governmental defined contribution structures—such as the Ohio Association of
Professional Fire Fighters (OAPFF) Deferred Compensation 457 Plan—who rely on
consistent, reliable data to perform continuous due diligence for public servants and their
beneficiaries.

I urge the Commission to reject the proposal based on the following critical points:

1. Severe Impairment of Fiduciary Due Diligence and Monitoring

Governmental 457(b) plans operate to protect the retirement assets of public safety
professionals who dedicate their careers to serving the public. The fiduciaries and
investment advisors of the OAPFF 457 Plan have a strict duty to perform rigorous, ongoing
due diligence reviews of the plan's investment line-up.

   •   Under a quarterly reporting regime, advisors can monitor emerging warning signs
       within underlying mutual funds or collective trusts—such as deteriorating corporate
       balance sheets, structural revenue declines, margin compression, or rising debt
       levels—and take corrective action.

   •   In a fast-moving macroeconomic environment, six months is an eternity. Waiting
       half a year for verified, reviewed financial statements effectively blinds plan
       fiduciaries. It forces them to make asset-allocation and fund-retention decisions
       based on stale information or unverified public rumors, increasing risk for
       participants.

2. Disproportionate Impact on Public Servants and Beneficiaries

Unlike institutional hedge funds or high-frequency traders, the participants in the OAPFF
457 Plan—firefighters, paramedics, and their beneficiaries—are long-term retail investors
utilizing defined contribution vehicles to build a secure retirement. They do not have
access to costly alternative data streams or direct corporate access. They rely entirely on
the democratic democratization of information provided by mandatory SEC disclosures. By
allowing companies to skip Form 10-Q, the SEC effectively treats public safety
professionals as second-class market participants, exposing their hard-earned deferred
compensation to sudden, unmitigated market shocks.

3. Proliferation of "Blind Spots" and Delayed Problem Identification

Corporate mismanagement, shifts in competitive landscapes, and financial stress rarely
align with a six-month calendar. Historically, quarterly reporting has functioned as an early-
warning system that allows the market to correct incrementally. If companies elect
semiannual reporting, material weaknesses in internal controls or negative financial trends
could remain concealed for up to 180 days. For defined contribution plans holding these
equities, this increases the probability of catastrophic "gap-down" pricing events when a
company finally discloses six months of accumulated bad news.

4. The Illusion of Cost Savings vs. Increased Cost of Capital

Proponents of the rule argue it will mitigate "managerial short-termism" and cut
administrative compliance costs. However, institutional investors and underwriter
networks will likely demand private disclosures or continue to favor companies maintaining
a quarterly cadence. Companies electing Form 10-S may find themselves penalized with
higher borrowing costs and depressed valuations due to a lack of transparency.
Ultimately, the cost of this "flexibility" will be borne by investors, including the public
retirement plans that anchor the stability of our capital markets.

Conclusion

For over 50 years, mandatory quarterly interim reporting has been the bedrock of trust in
the U.S. financial system. Dismantling this framework in favor of semiannual optionality
erodes the transparency required for institutional due diligence and jeopardizes the
retirement security of public employees like Ohio's professional firefighters.

I strongly encourage the Commission to withdraw this proposal and maintain the robust,
quarterly disclosure framework that ensures a fair and orderly market for all.
