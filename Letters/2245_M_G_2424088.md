To SEC: 

Form 10-Qs were forged in the wake of the 1929 market crash and the Great Depression to dismantle a financial "Wild West" defined by corporate secrecy and rampant insider abuse. Before mandatory reporting, executives routinely exploited a massive information advantage, front-running the public on material news and colluding in manipulative "stock pools" to fleece retail investors. The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created the SEC to enforce transparency, and the 10-Q was later established to eliminate dangerous 12-month information gaps, ensuring that fresh financial data stripped insiders of their unfair advantage. There are valid reasons why these things were created...ignoring hostory does not sound smart to me. 

Today, keeping the 10-Q is vital because annual reports are backward-looking obituaries; quarterlies provide the real-time vital signs necessary to price risk accurately and prevent catastrophic capital misallocation. Abolishing them would instantly widen information asymmetry, drive up the cost of capital, and crush investor confidence. In a fast-moving economy, a year-long information vacuum invites speculation, fraud, and a return to insider dominance—proving that standardized quarterly disclosure is not administrative red tape, but the bedrock of market integrity.
