Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Ravi Shah

I am writing to oppose the Commissionâs proposal to allow public companies to replace quarterly reporting on Form 10-Q with semiannual reporting on new Form 10-S. I respectfully urge the Commission to retain mandatory quarterly reporting for public companies.

The U.S. public markets are the deepest and most trusted in the world largely because investors receive regular, standardized, comparable, and reliable information. Quarterly reporting is not just a compliance exercise but a core component of market transparency that allows investors, analysts, lenders, employees, regulators, and the public to evaluate business performance in a timely and consistent manner.

The proposed semiannual option would reduce transparency at the exact moments when investors most need information. Reporting on a semiannual basis is too long in modern markets. Business conditions can change rapidly because of interest rates, tariffs, supply-chain disruptions, consumer weakness, credit conditions, litigation, cybersecurity incidents, accounting issues, customer concentration, or management execution problems. Investors should not have to wait half a year to receive standardized financial statements and management discussion from companies whose securities trade daily in public markets.

The proposal also risks creating an uneven information environment. If some companies continue to report quarterly while others report semiannually, investors will have less ability to compare peer companies during the year. A company reporting only twice annually could appear less volatile or less scrutinized simply because less information is available. That does not make the company stronger; it only makes the market less informed. The SECâs own proposing release recognizes this comparability concern by asking whether investors can reasonably compare a quarterly filer to a semiannual filer when first- or third-quarter information is not available.  

A voluntary semiannual regime may also disadvantage retail investors. Large institutions, insiders, sophisticated funds, private lenders, and well-connected analysts may still obtain information through management access, industry data, alternative data, private conversations, or selective corporate updates. Ordinary investors are more dependent on formal SEC filings. Reducing required reporting could widen the information gap between professional and retail market participants.

The likely substitute for quarterly reports would not be silence; it would be less standardized disclosure. Companies would still face market pressure to provide updates, issue press releases, hold investor calls, or selectively discuss business trends. But those communications may not provide the same level of consistency, liability discipline, financial-statement structure, MD&A, risk-factor updates, or comparability as Form 10-Q. In practice, the proposal could shift the market from standardized reporting to more fragmented, company-controlled messaging.

I understand the concern that quarterly reporting may contribute to short-term thinking. But eliminating or weakening quarterly reporting is not the right solution. The better distinction is between quarterly reporting and quarterly earnings guidance. The SEC can discourage excessive short-term earnings guidance without reducing the required financial disclosures that investors rely on. Long-term business planning and quarterly transparency are not mutually exclusive. A well-run company should be able to pursue long-term strategy while still providing investors with timely quarterly financial information.

The compliance cost argument should also be weighed carefully. Public companies receive significant benefits from access to public capital markets, including liquidity, valuation, credibility, and broad investor participation. In return, they should meet a baseline level of public transparency. For many investors, quarterly reporting is part of the bargain of investing in public securities rather than private businesses.

If the Commission is concerned about smaller companies or newly public companies, it could consider targeted relief rather than a broad semiannual option available to all reporting companies. For example, the SEC could simplify certain disclosure requirements, reduce duplicative items, or provide scaled reporting relief for smaller issuers while preserving the quarterly cadence. That would address compliance burdens without materially weakening market transparency.

For these reasons, I respectfully request that the Commission retain mandatory quarterly reporting on Form 10-Q. At minimum, if the Commission adopts any semiannual reporting option, it should be limited, carefully phased, and subject to investor-protection safeguards, including enhanced notice, shareholder approval or opt-out rights, clear risk disclosure, and restrictions for companies with elevated risk profiles.

Public markets work best when information is timely, standardized, comparable, and broadly available to all investors. Quarterly reporting supports those goals. Semiannual reporting would move the market in the wrong direction.

Thank you!
