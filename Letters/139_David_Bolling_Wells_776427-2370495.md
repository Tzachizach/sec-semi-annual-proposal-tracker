From: David Bolling Wells

SEC Comment Letter on Optional 6 Month Reporting Rule

As a former VP of Financial Planning & Analysis and then CFO of high growth company Netflix, I went through more than 60 quarterly reporting cycles with high stakes calls on both the creation and stress testing of content accounting that was being created for a new industry and guidance setting for a high profile company in a dynamic marketplace. Up close and personal. Our stock saw dramatic volatility, some of which we had trouble explaining. I went on to serve on the pre-public Audit and Risk Committee and then Chair of the Board of fintech company Wise (formerly Transferwise) which was the largest public listing on the LSE. Wise just switched on 5/11/26 to being a Nasdaq US primary listing after reporting every 6 months as a UK company in the first roughly 5 years of our public life. I have seen the cons and merits of the traditional 4 quarter cycle and the 6 month formal cycle with a "trading update" interim release.

There are good arguments for an optional 6 month opt in so I understand why many frustrated CFO and finance teams would propose and argue for it.

I am here to comment that on balance, I think the 4 quarter cycle is the best compromise and underpins the United States' superior trust (and value) position in global capital markets. The four quarter model drives discipline and rigor, and provides long investors with a reasonable 4 times-a-year check in on a company's progress on its long term strategic and financial goals. It also is a foundational element of "democratic capital markets," attempting to even the playing field for individual investors versus large institutions that can command regular check-ins with management.

Yes, the 4 quarter cycle is grinding, demanding, and "can" lead to overly short term financial focus but that is up to a company's financial leadership and investor relations to shape and manage. If the 6 month reporting opt in is enacted, what will happen is that a small minority of companies in industries that do match a twice-a-year reporting will be overjoyed and adopt it. But then, another group will adopt and this will become a mixed bag of lesser financially disciplined companies and the market will be left to sift through this and apply discounted value or higher risk. Fraud, slower impairments, increased insider knowledge value, and bad strategy living longer will all be the bogies of the slower reporting cycle.

I urge the SEC to make decisions that keep our US capital markets the envy of the world and stay with the current model. Don't confuse the need for securities litigation reform (loser pays legal fees) with changes, even optional, to the reporting cycle.

Sincerely,

David Wells
Chair WSE, Lead Independent HIMS, former CFO NFLX
