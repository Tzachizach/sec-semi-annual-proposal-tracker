Executive Summary
This comment provides technical implementation guidance on the proposed amendments concerning
electronic filing requirements and Form 10-K disclosures. While the Commission’s objective to
standardize and modernize data ingestion is structurally sound, the proposed operational mechanics
introduce unnecessary friction into enterprise reporting workflows. The current draft inadvertently
shifts implementation costs rather than reducing them, primarily through aggressive transition
timelines and undefined technical thresholds. The recommendations below are intended to support
the Commission’s transparency, comparability, and market integrity objectives while reducing
avoidable implementation friction. Adopting the specific structural adjustments detailed below will
guarantee the Commission receives high-utility data while minimizing system disruption for
registrants.

Procedural & Statutory Alignment
To ensure the final rule functions predictably and avoids interpretive challenges during
implementation, the technical requirements should strictly track the explicit, unambiguous text of the
Securities Exchange Act. Relying on broad, gap-filling interpretations for new data structuring
mandates introduces downstream litigation risk and administrative ambiguity. By anchoring these
technical mandates precisely to existing statutory definitions, the Commission can finalize a more
durable administrative record aligned with prevailing judicial standards of statutory interpretation,
ensuring the rule’s longevity and functional authority.
Furthermore, the Regulatory Impact Analysis (RIA) must reflect a more comprehensive baseline of
implementation costs to satisfy the logical outgrowth test of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA).

The current cost-benefit analysis accurately models recurring variable costs but underestimates the
fixed integration costs necessary for initial IT architectural overhauls. Strengthening the RIA with the
ground-truth operational data provided below will insulate the rulemaking from procedural
vulnerabilities while satisfying the rigorous evidentiary requirements of OMB Circular A-4.

Section-by-Section Technical Analysis: Part 232 (Regulation S-T)
Quote/Reference: Proposed amendments to electronic filing specifications under Part 232.
Issue: The transition to new structured data requirements assumes immediate enterprise IT system
readiness, which conflicts with standard corporate upgrade cycles and third-party vendor software
availability. Mandating simultaneous compliance for all filer tiers creates an operational bottleneck at
filing agents, making systemic technical errors and late filings structurally inevitable.
Proposed Redline/Fix: Implement a tiered compliance schedule: "Large Accelerated Filers shall
comply for fiscal years beginning on or after [Date + 1 Year]; Accelerated Filers on or after [Date + 2
Years]; and Non-Accelerated Filers on or after [Date + 3 Years]."
Operational Safeguards & Why This Works: A phased rollout prevents resource exhaustion among
third-party filing software vendors. This operational sequencing allows the Commission’s EDGAR
system to ingest a smaller, more manageable dataset initially, enabling the SEC to iteratively refine its
internal validation architecture before the full registrant population comes online. As a result, the
Commission materially reduces the volume of expected waiver requests and maintains continuous
market operations.

Section-by-Section Technical Analysis: PRA Burden Estimates
Quote/Reference: "PRA Table 1. Estimated Paperwork Burden Effects of the Proposed
Amendments".
Issue: The Paperwork Reduction Act (PRA) estimates rely on a baseline that mathematically undercalculates the initial mapping hours for newly tagged data fields. The estimates presume that existing
workflows can simply absorb new tags; operationally, deploying a newly customized taxonomy
requires cross-functional reconciliation between legal, accounting, and IT departments, multiplying the
actual burden hours in year one.
Proposed Redline/Fix: Adjust the initial burden hours upward by 25% in the first year to account for
cross-departmental taxonomy mapping. Simultaneously, insert a provision into the final rule preamble:
"The Commission will treat formatting errors in newly required structured data tags during the first 12
months of compliance as correctable administrative errors rather than substantive disclosure failures,
provided they are made in good faith."

Operational Safeguards & Why This Works: Calibrating the initial burden estimates to reflect
ground-truth implementation costs fundamentally strengthens the administrative record's durability
against arbitrary and capricious challenges. The temporary safe harbor for formatting errors prevents
the chilling effect of strict liability, ultimately accelerating the market's transition to the accurate, highfidelity data reporting the Commission requires.

Section-by-Section Technical Analysis: Part 249 (Form 10-K General Instructions)
Quote/Reference: General Instructions for Form 10-K reporting requirements.
Issue: The proposed modifications to reporting thresholds introduce ambiguous aggregation
requirements across domestic and foreign subsidiaries. Without a definitive technical boundary, the
rule is operationally dissonant; registrants will inevitably over-report data out of an abundance of
caution, flooding the Commission with immaterial artifacts and defeating the modernization effort's
primary purpose.
Proposed Redline/Fix: Anchor the aggregation threshold directly to existing accounting standards:
"For the purposes of this specific disclosure, 'consolidated entity data' shall be calculated utilizing the
identical scope of consolidation applied in the registrant's audited financial statements under U.S.
GAAP or IFRS, as applicable."
Operational Safeguards & Why This Works: Harmonizing the aggregation threshold with existing
U.S. GAAP or IFRS consolidation scopes prevents the generation of parallel, bespoke reporting
structures. This regulatory alignment drastically reduces compliance overhead while guaranteeing the
Commission receives data that is structurally verifiable against the registrant's audited financials.

Operational Impact Assessment
In practice, failing to adopt these technical adjustments will result in widespread operational
dissonance. Registrants are fully capable of meeting the Commission's mandate, but they require
technical specifications that map realistically to enterprise resource planning (ERP) system
capabilities. Regulatory requirements that outpace the deployment cycles of enterprise software
inevitably force manual intervention, which directly degrades the data fidelity the Commission seeks to
achieve.
By implementing the specific structural redlines proposed above, the Commission can immediately
mitigate these friction points. These targeted solutions do not compromise the statutory intent or the
policy goals of the rulemaking; rather, they provide the exact operational mechanics necessary for
successful, frictionless market adoption.
The foregoing recommendations are offered to reduce ambiguity and litigation exposure by improving
administrative predictability. They are intended as implementation guidance rather than critiques of

agency authority or intent. They are intended to ensure the statutory framework functions precisely as
written.
