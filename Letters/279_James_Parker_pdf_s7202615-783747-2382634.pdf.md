Re: S7-2026-15
Dear Ms. Countryman,
I am writing to express my strong opposition to the Commission’s proposal to transition
mandatory financial reporting for public companies from a quarterly (3-month) to a
semiannual (6-month) cadence. While the stated goal of this change is to reduce "shorttermism" and compliance costs, the proposal fails to account for the severe
informational disadvantages it imposes on retail investors and the broader risks to
market stability. My opposition is based on the following points.
First, this rule change ignores the fundamental structural gap between institutional and
retail investors. Small investors rely heavily on standardized, frequent quarterly reports
because, unlike large institutional firms, they lack direct access to senior management.
Without the 90-day pulse of Form 10-Q filings, retail investors are essentially locked out
of the information loop for half the year, while large funds continue to leverage their size
to gain private insights.
Second, reducing the frequency of reporting provides management with an
unacceptable window to delay the disclosure of negative material news. The history of
the markets shows that when companies are allowed longer gaps between public
filings, bad news is often buried until it can no longer be hidden, resulting in "information
dumps" that trigger extreme price volatility. This undermines the SEC’s mission to
maintain orderly markets.
Third, the Commission has not adequately addressed the unknown impact this change
will have on derivative markets, particularly the options market. Options are vital tools
for investors seeking to hedge their positions and manage risk. Option pricing and

liquidity are deeply tied to the regular arrival of quarterly financial data. Extending the
reporting cycle could drastically increase the cost of hedging and reduce liquidity in
these essential markets.
Furthermore, I am deeply concerned that a 6-month cycle will exacerbate the exchange
of material non-public information. Management frequently holds private meetings with
large investors and sell-side analysts between public reporting periods. Doubling the
time between public disclosures creates a dangerous six-month "blackout" for the
public, during which management can provide private "color" or guidance to favored
parties. This creates an environment of asymmetrical information where retail investors
are systematically traded against by those with better access.
In conclusion, the proposed shift to semiannual reporting prioritizes corporate
convenience over investor protection. It erodes transparency, invites market volatility,
and tilts the playing field even further toward institutional players. I urge the
Commission to withdraw this proposal and maintain the quarterly reporting standards
that have made U.S. capital markets the most transparent in the world.
Sincerely,

James A Parker
Charlotte, North Carolina
