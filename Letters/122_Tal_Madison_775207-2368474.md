Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Tal Madison

Re: Comment on File No. S7-2026-15, Semiannual Reporting

To Whom It May Concern,

I write to comment on the Commission's proposal to replace the quarterly reporting requirement on Form 10-Q with a semiannual regime.

The proposal raises substantial questions under the Administrative Procedure Act and recent decisions of the Supreme Court that constrain how agencies may exercise statutory authority delegated to them by Congress. I focus on four problems with the proposal as drafted. First, the rule cannot rest on Chevron deference to the Commission's interpretation of Section 13(a) after Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo. Second, the question whether public companies should report quarterly or semiannually is an issue of sufficient economic and political significance that it implicates the major questions doctrine. Third, the Commission has not yet assembled an adequate record under State Farm and its progeny to justify abandoning the disclosure regime that has been in place since 1970. Fourth, the proposal's cost-benefit analysis, as previewed in the release, repeats analytical errors that the D.C. Circuit has previously held fatal to Commission rulemakings.

I. The Commission Now Bears the Full Interpretive Burden Under Section 13(a).

Until June 2024, the Commission could defend an exercise of its disclosure authority under Section 13(a) of the Exchange Act by pointing to the statute's general grant of power to require periodic reports "in such detail as the Commission may prescribe." Reviewing courts would ordinarily defer to a reasonable Commission interpretation of that authorizing language. That regime ended with Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, which held that courts must "exercise their independent judgment in deciding whether an agency has acted within its statutory authority" and may no longer defer to an agency's interpretation of an ambiguous statute simply because the agency has offered a permissible reading. Chief Justice Roberts wrote that the APA "specifies that courts, not agencies, will decide all relevant questions of law arising on review of agency action."

The practical consequence for this rulemaking is straightforward. Whatever final rule the Commission adopts will be tested in court against the best reading of Section 13(a), not against a reading that a court might once have accepted as merely permissible. The Commission, of course, may still adopt a rule that reflects its expert policy judgment, and reviewing courts will continue to apply ordinary arbitrary and capricious review under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A) to the Commission's factual findings and policy choices. What has changed is that the textual question, whether semiannual reporting is consistent with Congress's instruction that issuers file "such annual reports … and such quarterly reports … as the Commission may prescribe" under Section 13(a)(1) and (a)(2), will receive de novo treatment.

The release should engage that interpretive question directly rather than treating it as foreclosed by historical practice. Section 13(a)(2), as amended, refers to "quarterly reports" in the alternative, and there is a serious textual argument that the statute presupposes a quarterly cadence as the default mode of interim reporting even as it leaves implementation details to the Commission. A final rule that eliminates quarterly reporting altogether must explain why the statutory reference to "quarterly reports" does not require, at minimum, that quarterly reporting remain available to investors as the baseline disclosure regime. The Commission cannot now rely on the deference framework that supported earlier expansive readings of its Section 13(a) authority, and the release as currently drafted does not grapple with the textual problem.

II. The Proposal Implicates the Major Questions Doctrine.

In West Virginia v. EPA, the Court held that in "extraordinary cases" involving questions of "vast economic and political significance," an agency must point to "clear congressional authorization" for the power it claims to exercise. The doctrine was reaffirmed in Biden v. Nebraska, where the Court emphasized that the doctrine applies whenever an agency invokes a long-extant statute to assert sweeping authority over questions of "deep economic and political significance" that Congress has not clearly addressed.

Quarterly reporting has been a foundational feature of United States public capital markets for more than half a century. Form 10-Q was adopted in 1970, and the four-quarter reporting cadence has shaped how analysts model earnings, how institutional investors allocate capital, how auditors plan their interim review procedures, and how issuers communicate with the market. Roughly 4,000 domestic reporting companies file Form 10-Q each quarter, and the universe of investors who rely on those filings extends to retail brokerage account holders, pension beneficiaries, mutual fund participants, and the broader research and analyst community.

A regulatory action that dismantles that regime is not a routine technical adjustment to disclosure forms. It is a structural change to the information environment of the United States equity markets. Whether one believes the change is wise or unwise, it presents the kind of question that West Virginia and Nebraska instruct courts to scrutinize for clear congressional authorization. The Commission cannot answer that question by citing only the general delegation in Section 13(a). The release should identify with specificity the statutory text that, in the Commission's view, clearly authorizes the elimination of mandatory quarterly disclosure, and it should acknowledge the major questions framework as the standard against which that authorization will be measured.

III. The Record Does Not Yet Support a Reasoned Departure from Existing Policy.

An agency changing course must "provide a reasoned explanation" for disregarding facts and circumstances that underlay its prior policy. Under State Farm, an agency acts arbitrarily and capriciously if it "entirely failed to consider an important aspect of the problem," if it "offered an explanation for its decision that runs counter to the evidence before the agency," or if its decision "is so implausible that it could not be ascribed to a difference in view or the product of agency expertise." In Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California, the Court reiterated that an agency must "assess whether there were reliance interests, determine whether they were significant, and weigh any such interests against competing policy concerns."

The release identifies short-termism as the principal concern motivating the move to semiannual reporting. The academic literature on that question is genuinely contested. Some studies suggest that quarterly reporting pressures managers toward myopic decisions, while other work finds that more frequent reporting reduces information asymmetry between issuers and investors and lowers the cost of equity capital. The Commission has not yet identified which body of evidence it credits, why, and how it weighs the offsetting findings. A final rule that asserts a settled empirical foundation for the short-termism rationale, in the face of substantial countervailing literature, will be vulnerable on review.

Reliance interests in this rulemaking are unusually concentrated and quantifiable. Equity research analysts have built coverage models around the quarterly reporting calendar. Mutual funds and ETFs that mark to market on a daily basis depend on quarterly issuer data to validate the prices generated by their pricing services between annual filings. Credit committees at lenders, insurance underwriters, rating agencies, and counterparty risk groups all incorporate Form 10-Q data into their periodic monitoring. The Commission should catalog these reliance interests and explain how the proposed semiannual cadence accommodates them, particularly during the two financial quarters in which no public information would otherwise enter the market under the proposal.

Public statements by the executive branch in support of the proposal do not relieve the Commission of its independent obligation to develop a reasoned record. The Commission is an independent agency, and a rulemaking record that appears to rest on extra-record political direction rather than substantive evidence will not satisfy State Farm review.

IV. The Cost-Benefit Analysis Must Meet the D.C. Circuit's Established Standard.

Section 3(f) of the Exchange Act directs the Commission, when engaged in rulemaking that requires it to consider whether an action is necessary or appropriate in the public interest, to consider whether the action will promote efficiency, competition, and capital formation. The D.C. Circuit has held that the Commission acts arbitrarily and capriciously when it fails to "apprise itself, and hence the public and the Congress, of the economic consequences of a proposed regulation." In Business Roundtable v. SEC, the court vacated a proxy access rule because the Commission "inconsistently and opportunistically framed the costs and benefits of the rule" and "failed adequately to quantify the certain costs or to explain why those costs could not be quantified."

A defensible cost-benefit analysis for this rulemaking must do at least the following. It must quantify, or explain why it cannot quantify, the savings to issuers from eliminating two interim filings per year. It must quantify, or explain why it cannot quantify, the costs to investors from receiving only two interim updates per year rather than four, including the cost of impaired price discovery and the cost of information asymmetry between insiders and outside investors during the resulting reporting gaps. It must address whether the proposed regime is likely to widen bid-ask spreads, increase the cost of equity capital, or shift trading volume toward derivative or alternative venues that do not depend on Commission-mandated disclosure. And it must consider less restrictive alternatives, including retention of Form 10-Q with streamlined content requirements, voluntary semiannual reporting at the issuer's election, or scaled relief for emerging growth companies and smaller reporting companies that does not affect the broader market.

The release should also address the interaction between the proposed change and existing Management's Discussion and Analysis requirements under Item 303 of Regulation S-K. Much of the information that flows to the market through Form 10-Q is qualitative MD&A content rather than the quarterly financial statements themselves. If the Commission intends to preserve interim MD&A while eliminating quarterly financial statements, it should say so. If it intends to eliminate both, it should explain why the elimination of qualitative interim disclosure is consistent with the materiality framework articulated in Basic Inc. v. Levinson.

V. Recommendations.

Taken together, these defects are not merely procedural. The proposed rulemaking does not survive the post-Loper Bright framework because the Commission must now defend the rule as the best reading of Section 13(a), not merely as a permissible construction of ambiguous statutory text. Nor does the rule comfortably survive major-questions scrutiny, because a Commission rule that substantially alters the cadence of mandatory public-company disclosure implicates the structure of the national securities markets and requires clear congressional authorization. Even apart from those threshold statutory concerns, the proposal is vulnerable under State Farm because the release does not yet provide a reasoned explanation for departing from the settled quarterly-reporting regime or adequately account for investor reliance, market-efficiency costs, and less disruptive alternatives. On the current record, the Commission has not carried the interpretive, statutory, or administrative-law burden required to finalize the rule as proposed.

Respectfully submitted.
