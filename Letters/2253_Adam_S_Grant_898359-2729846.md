Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Adam S. Grant

I am a semi-retired investor with IRAs, SEP and personal accounts. I rely on QUARTERLY reporting to assess the health of my investments.

I write in opposition to proposed Rule S7-2026-15, which would allow public companies to substitute semiannual reports on new Form 10-S for the existing quarterly Form 10-Q filings. If adopted, this would represent the biggest rollback of investor disclosure requirements in more than 50 years. I urge the Commission to reject it.

Quarterly reporting is not a burden on healthy, well-run companies - it is a basic expectation of transparency that public markets were built on. When I invest in a public company, I give up liquidity and take on risk. Quarterly disclosures are part of what I get in return.

Moving to semiannual reporting would leave investors like me without timely information for six months at a stretch. A lot can change in six months: revenue trajectories shift, debt levels change, management makes capital allocation decisions that affect shareholders. Delaying that disclosure doesn't reduce short-termism - it just reduces accountability.

The argument that quarterly reporting pressures companies to focus on short-term results is not an argument for less transparency. It is an argument for better management. If a CEO cannot explain quarterly results without sacrificing long-term strategy, the problem is not the reporting requirement.

Semiannual reporting benefits insiders and institutions who have access to private management briefings and industry data that retail investors do not. Retail investors depend on mandatory public disclosures precisely because we do not have those back channels.

I urge the Commission to retain the quarterly reporting requirement. Reducing disclosure frequency would shift the information advantage further toward sophisticated market participants at the direct expense of ordinary investors.
