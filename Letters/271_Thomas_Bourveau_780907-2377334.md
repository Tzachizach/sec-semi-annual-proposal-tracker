Re: Proposal to Allow Optional Semiannual Reporting - File S7-2026-15
Dear Chairman Atkins,
I submit this letter in response to the Commission proposed amendments to Exchange Act
Rules 13a-13 and 15d-13, which would permit issuers registered with the SEC to elect to
file semiannual reports on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q. I am a
Professor of Accounting at the University of Oxford. I write as a scholar who has extensively
studied mandatory and voluntary disclosure and auditing in U.S. capital markets. In this
letter, I offer insights drawn from academic research to help inform the economic analysis of
the proposed amendment.
Unlike many comments in the public debate, the question at stake is not whether investors
in public companies should receive financial statements every quarter, but rather whether
the Commission should impose a fixed reporting frequency on issuers. There is a longstanding scholarly debate concerning the necessity of mandatory disclosure in capital markets.
Although this debate extends beyond the specific questions raised by the proposed amendment to the Exchange Act Rules, it remains relevant to the present inquiry. The central
argument to justify a disclosure mandate is the presence of a market failure. Two types of
market failure are often cited: (1) market forces may be insufficient to resolve the information
asymmetry between public issuers and investors; and (2) information disclosure generates
externalities, such that the amount of disclosure produced through market forces falls below
the level that would be optimal for the market (or society) as a whole.
Below, I provide some observations about the limited evidence to support these two sources
of market failure and discuss them in the context of the proposed amendments. I conclude
my letter with some comments on the potential benefits of this proposed amendment to
make quarterly reporting optional.

(1) Disclosure mandates are necessary to resolve the information asymmetry
between the issuers and their investors so the Commission should maintain the
universal quarterly reporting frequency for issuers registered with the SEC.
This argument rests on two pillars: (i) insiders possess private information about a company’s economic performance that is relevant to investors; and (ii) insiders fail to disclose
this information voluntarily and in a timely manner to investors. While the first point is
widely accepted, the second is far more debatable. In fact, several theoretical studies in information economics from the 1980s suggest that, under certain assumptions, economic agents
will eventually reveal their private information.1 The intuition is straightforward: if an agent
withholds information, market participants may interpret this nondisclosure as a negative
signal, leading them to revise their beliefs about the agent’s prospects downward. This process leads all but the worst agents to disclose their information. This theory is difficult to
test in the real world because the disclosure of financial statements has been mandated at
the federal level for nearly a century in U.S. capital markets. Moreover, state-level “blue sky
laws” predated federal regulation and often required financial disclosure from issuers seeking
to sell securities to the public in order to protect investors. The pervasiveness of disclosure
mandates in capital markets should not be interpreted as evidence that unregulated markets
are unable to resolve information asymmetry between issuers and investors.
In a recent paper, my co-authors and I test whether information unravel in capital markets.2
To do so, we leverage the technological disruption caused by the electrification boom in the
late 19th century that allowed the emergence of streetcar companies as the default urban
transportation mode. Newly established streetcar companies required a large amount of
capital to finance miles of tracks and cars, which created significant investment opportunities. To satisfy investor demand for information on these opportunities, business newspapers
started issuing regular supplements dedicated to reporting company-specific information on
almost all existing streetcar companies. Using a comprehensive sample of streetcar companies in the North East of the United States, we find that 72% of companies disclose their
financial statements voluntarily in the first specialized supplement of a prominent business
media, and that this rate increases rapidly to reach 95% within two years. Our findings
suggest that an almost full disclosure equilibrium is reached over a short period of time.
Evidence from the 1890s streetcar industry shows that, even without mandatory disclosure
requirements, public issuers facing investor demand voluntarily disclosed their financial performance. In other words, market forces can often resolve information asymmetry between
issuers and investors through voluntary disclosure. Applied to the current policy debate,
these findings suggest that if quarterly reporting were made optional, issuers would likely
continue to provide quarterly earnings information if investors demand it, arguing that it
constitutes first-order sources of information for their capital allocation decisions.

(2) Earnings information generate externalities in capital markets so the Commission should maintain quarterly reporting for all SEC-registered issuers
The argument holds that market forces lead companies to provide investors with the optimal amount of information on an individual basis. However, the total amount of disclosure
produced by market forces may be insufficient, because information disclosure generates
externalities that companies do not internalize. As a result, the overall level of financial
disclosure is likely to be too low.
This argument appears quite weak for several reasons:
• It is admittedly very challenging to provide rigorous evidence that disclosure mandates
correct a market failure due to the presence of market-wide externalities. However,
studies based on European private companies fails to find evidence that reporting
mandates unambiguously improve the efficiency of resource allocation in the economy.3
• The market-wide information loss will be a function of the portion of issuers that will
switch to semi-annual reporting along with their relative importance for systemic risk
in capital markets. This information loss will likely remain limited:
– Disclosure choices are known to be sticky, suggesting that most issuers will continue to file 10-Q filings or become hybrid reporters that provide some voluntary
financial information during the first and third quarter.4
– Issuers that would choose to report semi-annually are likely those for whom the
benefits of quarterly reporting are minimal. Capital market benefits tend to be
lower for issuers operating mature businesses with stable economic performance,
where more frequent updates provide little new information to market participants. As a result, the market-wide information loss from these issuers would
likely be limited.
– Issuers that would choose to report semi-annually are likely those for whom the
costs of quarterly reporting are highest. Preparation costs are proportionally
greater for smaller issuers, and the opportunity costs of preparing reports are
higher for high-growth companies.5 However, smaller high-growth issuers represent only a small portion of the value-weighted capital market, further limiting
any potential market-wide information loss.

(3) The potential benefits of a flexible reporting regime
I would like to highlight two potential benefits from the proposed amendment to make
quarterly reporting optional:
• Carefully quantifying the compliance costs of reporting and auditing regulations for
SEC-registered public issuers has proven challenging. By introducing a flexible reporting regime, the Commission would allow issuers to reveal their preferences, making
it possible to observe how many view the switch to semi-annual reporting as beneficial (i.e., when the costs of quarterly reporting outweigh the benefits). Tracking the
number of issuers that switch and analyzing their economic characteristics could provide valuable insights for the Commission in considering further efforts to simplify the
reporting regime for SEC registrants.
• The Commission must balance investor protection with the goal of facilitating capital
formation. The economic analysis of the proposed amendment indicates that, relative
to the overall size of U.S. capital markets, the aggregate cost savings from reduced
reporting frequency would be modest, suggesting only a limited impact on capital
formation at the market level. However, these cost savings could be much more significant for smaller, high-growth issuers, for whom compliance costs represent a larger
burden. Allowing such firms to opt out of quarterly reporting may enable them to
better focus on their core business activities. A reduction in reporting frequency for
some issuers does not necessarily mean diminished investor protection. Investor protection is achieved through both reporting requirements and the SEC’s direct oversight
activities. To maintain robust investor safeguards while easing the burden on issuers
for whom quarterly reporting is especially costly, the Commission could reallocate its
oversight resources to focus more closely on those issuers that choose to report less frequently. In other words, the Commission could incorporate issuers’ chosen reporting
frequency into its risk-based oversight framework, ensuring that limited resources are
targeted where the potential risks to investors are greatest.6
