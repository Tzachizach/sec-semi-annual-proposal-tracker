Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Ramesh Doraiswamy

To the Securities and Exchange Commission,

I am writing as an individual retail investor based in Carlsbad, California, USA, with over two decades of experience managing my own portfolio using publicly available SEC filings, earnings reports, and options strategies. I am opposed to the proposed amendments under File No. S7-2026-15.

The quarterly reporting cadence is not a burden on investors; it is valuable and vital information for them. Retail investors like me rely entirely on public filings to make informed decisions. Institutional investors, corporate insiders, and large asset managers have continuous access to operational data through analyst relationships, private briefings, and internal channels. Extending the interim reporting window from 90 days to 180 days does not level the playing field; it widens it in favor of those who already have the least need for public disclosure.

For investors who use options strategies tied to earnings cycles like I do, a 180-day blind spot materially increases risk. Options pricing, implied volatility, and position sizing all depend on predictable disclosure timelines. Halving the frequency of interim reports is not a modernization; it is a structural disadvantage imposed on retail participants.

The cost-savings rationale also does not hold up. The largest public companies, the ones most likely to influence market behavior have automated their reporting pipelines. With the advent of AI and modern technology tools, the marginal cost of producing a quarterly 10-Q for a large-cap issuer is no longer the burden it was in 1970. If the Commission's concern is genuinely about smaller companies, I propose a targeted approach: make semiannual reporting optional only for companies below a defined float threshold (e.g., under $75 million), while keeping quarterly reporting mandatory for large- and mid-cap issuers.

I respectfully urge the Commission to reject or substantially narrow this proposal. Transparency is the foundation of fair markets. This rule moves in the wrong direction.

Respectfully submitted,
Ramesh Doraiswamy
Carlsbad, California
