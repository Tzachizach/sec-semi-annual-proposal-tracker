Dear Members of the Commission,

I unequivocally oppose any proposal to weaken mandatory quarterly reporting by replacing it with semiannual reporting. Such a change would weaken the U.S. disclosure regime, undermining investor protection, market transparency, and corporate accountability. Timely, recurring financial reporting is a cornerstone of capital market integrity-not an administrative burden.

As a professor of accounting holding bachelorâs, masterâs, and doctoral degrees with a concentration in accounting, and as a Certified Fraud Examiner with experience in financial reporting, forensic accounting, and corporate governance, I believe this proposal would materially increase systemic risk. It would impair the ability of investors and regulators to detect performance deterioration, liquidity stress, and fraudulent activity in a timely manner.

Quarterly reporting ensures that capital allocation decisions are based on current, reliable information. Extending reporting intervals to six months would expand informational blind spots, heighten information asymmetry, and allow misstatements and governance failures to persist longer before detection.

From a fraud and governance perspective, reduced reporting frequency weakens scrutiny, diminishes deterrence, and increases the opportunity to conceal adverse financial conditions.

 Reducing transparency does not ease reporting pressure-it simply delays when problems are uncovered and allows the consequences to become more severe.

Public companies benefit from access to capital markets; in return, they owe timely and transparent disclosure. Weakening this obligation shifts risk to investors and erodes trust in market integrity.
