# Letter 72 — Peter Ford

- **Date:** 2026-05-05
- **Role/Affiliation:** Investment professional / advisor
- **Stance:** Oppose
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/S7-2026-15/s7202615-768488-2357114.html

---

Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Peter Ford

1. Information gaps create pricing inefficiencies and risk. Quarterly earnings have been the bedrock of fundamental analysis for decades. Cutting reporting to twice a year means investors go six months without an auditor-reviewed financial picture of a company. For individual investors, that's blind flying. For advisors managing client portfolios, it means extended periods where you simply don't know if the thesis on a position has changed. Professional advisors who rely on 10-Q data for position monitoring, valuation updates, and risk flagging would face a material degradation in signal quality.

2. "Optional" won't stay optional for long and the wrong companies will opt in. The framing as optional makes this sound benign. But in practice, companies with something to hide between periods. Those with deteriorating margins, rising debt, operational problems are the most motivated to elect semiannual filing. High-quality, stable businesses have little to gain. This creates adverse selection: the universe of semiannual filers will be skewed toward companies with elevated opacity risk, right at the moment investors have less visibility.

3. It undermines earnings-based risk management. Professional advisors use quarterly filings for far more than earnings: they track working capital trends, debt covenant headroom, revenue seasonality, segment-level performance, and management guidance credibility. A six-month gap makes all of this harder. Sector-sensitive portfolios where conditions shift materially quarter to quarter would be particularly exposed.

4. The market efficiency argument cuts the other way. Proponents (echoing Chairman Atkins' language about companies determining "the interim reporting frequency that best serves their business needs") frame this as reducing burden. But capital markets are efficient because information flows continuously and uniformly. Allowing a two-tier system fragments that. Stocks of semiannual filers would likely trade at a discount — a permanent information-risk premium, which ironically hurts the very companies that elect the option.
