Re: Proposed Rule on Semiannual Reporting (File No: S7-2026-15)

Dear Ms. Countryman:

       On behalf of the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees
(AFSCME), I am writing to provide comments on the U.S. Securities and Exchange
Commission’s (the SEC or Commission) proposed rulemaking entitled Proposed Rule on
Semiannual Reporting (File No: S7-2026-15) (the Proposed Rule) to allow companies to file
semiannual reports on the new Form 10S in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q.

        AFSCME's 1.4 million members provide the vital services that make America
happen. With members in communities across the nation, serving in hundreds of different
occupations — from nurses to corrections officers, child care providers to sanitation workers
— AFSCME advocates for fairness in the workplace, excellence in public services and
freedom and opportunity for all working families. AFSCME members participate in over
150 public pension systems, in private sector single-employer and multiemployer pension
plans and public- and private-sector defined contribution retirement plans.

        AFSCME appreciates the opportunity to submit comments. We urge the
Commission to withdraw the Proposed Rule and keep current quarterly reporting disclosure
as is, rather than making quarterly disclosure optional. Public companies have been
disclosing quarterly reports on Form 10-Q since the passage of the Securities Exchange Act
of 1934 and have been required to do so since 1970. The efficient and orderly operation of
our markets depends on investors being able to make informed decisions. Quarterly reporting
of timely, comparable and decision-useful financial disclosure is essential to the efficient
functioning of our capital markets. Therefore, we believe the Proposed Rule is a solution in
search of a problem and should be withdrawn.

What Gets Measured Gets Managed

        Transparency is the foundation of our capital markets. Investors rely on quarterly
reports to make sound investment, voting and engagement decisions, including by
monitoring how companies are performing and implementing their strategies. Mandatory
quarterly disclosure ensures material financial information reaches all investors at the same
time. Investors rely on quarterly data for risk monitoring, whereas semiannual reporting

would allow a company to delay information reaching the company’s owners — the shareholders who
are providing the capital. Quarterly reporting is an important element of transparency for investors, as
well as a positive discipline for management. We agree with a recent editorial by Charles Elson, who
notes that quarterly reporting aligns with basic corporate financial accounting, where boards measure
management performance by meeting quarterly and reviewing financials that are quarterly based.1
Quarterly reporting is essential for providing investors with prompt and reliable information on how
companies are actually performing and for providing management with discipline to provide regular
reporting for measuring.

Arguments Have Not Changed Since the SEC’s 2018–2019 Consultation

         Arguments supporting the Proposed Rule — such as reducing burdens on issuers or promoting
capital formation and long-termism — are largely unchanged from those previously made during the
SEC’s 2018–2019 Request for Comments on Earnings Releases and Quarterly Reports (File No: S7-
2026-15). At that time, the CFA Institute found there was almost no support for changing quarterly
reporting among investment professionals around the globe, who rely on financial reporting to manage
assets.2 Fast forward to the Proposed Rule, and the views of investors have not changed: CFA’s latest
survey confirms investors globally, and not just in the U.S., continue to view quarterly reporting as an
essential feature of transparent, efficient and trustworthy capital markets.3

         From the previous consultation, we agree with the Council of Institutional Investors comment
that the requirement to report historical earnings on a quarterly basis is a key element of the timely and
accurate information flow that underpins the quality and efficiency of our capital markets. 4 We also
support the position of the AFL-CIO that rather than eliminating quarterly reporting requirements, the
Commission should instead be focusing on ways to enhance the quality of information provided by
public companies to investors, including environmental, social and governance (ESG), human capital
management and political spending disclosures.5

The Need to Expand Private Market Disclosure

        We also believe the SEC should focus on expanding disclosure rules related to private equity.
A big reason for the decline in IPOs and public companies is the deregulation and disclosure
exemptions for private financing, like private equity and venture capital, rather than public company
disclosure requirements like quarterly reporting. AFSCME has regularly urged the SEC to strengthen
oversight and enforcement of rules related to private equity,6 especially in the wake of the 2020 SEC

         The need for increased private markets disclosure has become only more urgent given the U.S.
Department of Labor’s proposed rule to encourage private equity access to 401(k) plans with $13.8
trillion11 in assets. Allowing private offerings to retail investors, including through self-directed
retirement plans, while failing to pair this access with required public disclosures, will leave plan
fiduciaries and vulnerable investors without the disclosures necessary to distinguish between good and
bad investments.

        We appreciate the opportunity to share our views on the importance of keeping quarterly
reporting rules. We urge the Commission to protect investors’ access to information and keep current
quarterly reporting disclosure requirements so as to meet the evolving needs of the investing
community. The efficient and orderly operation of our markets depends on investors being able to
make informed decisions, including receiving material information in a uniform and timely manner. If
you have any questions, or need additional information, please do not hesitate to contact John Keenan
at jkeenan@afscme.org.
