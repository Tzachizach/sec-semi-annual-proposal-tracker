To the Commissioners:

I appreciate the opportunity to comment on the SEC’s proposed amendments related to
optional semiannual reporting.
As a finance and governance executive with experience in public-company reporting, IPO
readiness, SOX/internal controls, Audit Committee engagement, and operational finance
leadership, I believe the proposal raises important considerations around balancing
regulatory burden, investor transparency, and long-term value creation.
I support the Commission’s effort to provide flexibility in reporting cadence rather than
mandating a single reporting structure for all issuers. Public companies operate with very
different business models, investor bases, operational cycles, and resource constraints,
and a more principles-based framework may better align reporting obligations with
company-specific circumstances.
From an operational perspective, quarterly reporting can create significant organizational
burden beyond the direct filing process itself. Management attention, finance resources,
auditor coordination, and board governance activities often become heavily concentrated
around quarterly cycles. In some organizations, this cadence can unintentionally reinforce
short-term decision-making and near-term performance management at the expense of
longer-term strategic execution and investment.
At the same time, I do not believe the conversation should simply be framed as “less
reporting.” Financial statements are inherently backward-looking, and reducing reporting
frequency may further widen the information gap between institutional and noninstitutional investors.
Large institutional investors often have greater access to management teams, industry
data, research resources, and alternative information channels outside of standardized
SEC reporting. By contrast, many retail and non-institutional investors rely more heavily on
periodic SEC disclosures as a primary source of transparent, comparable, and decisionuseful information.
For that reason, quarterly reporting serves an important governance and market-discipline
function beyond simply communicating financial results. It creates regular accountability,
standardized disclosure cadence, and broader market transparency that may be more
difficult to replicate through voluntary or less structured communications.
One area I believe deserves particular consideration is the increasing importance of
enterprise risk integration and governance visibility. Boards today are overseeing

significantly more interconnected operational, technology, cybersecurity, AI, compliance,
workforce, and financial risks than they were when quarterly reporting requirements were
originally established decades ago. In many organizations, the issue is not necessarily lack
of information, but fragmented information and difficulty connecting operational
developments back to enterprise strategy, risk oversight, and measurable value creation.
For that reason, I believe optional semiannual reporting should be limited to issuers that
demonstrate sustained governance and reporting maturity. Reporting flexibility should be
earned through demonstrated controls discipline, transparency, and governance reliability.
Potential eligibility criteria could include objective governance and reporting indicators
such as:
•
•
•
•
•

no material weaknesses in internal control over financial reporting for a specified
multi-year period
no significant financial restatements within a specified multi-year period
consistent timely SEC filing history
independent Audit Committee oversight, including designated financial expertise
consistent with existing SEC requirements
absence of recurring significant deficiencies or disclosure-related enforcement
matters

In my view, companies with established governance maturity and strong controls
environments may be better positioned to operate effectively with less frequent interim
reporting while still maintaining investor confidence and transparency. Conversely,
companies with weaker controls environments or recurring deficiencies may benefit from
maintaining the discipline and accountability created through quarterly reporting cycles.
I also believe the Commission should carefully consider potential unintended
consequences related to:
•
•
•
•
•

reduced comparability between issuers
varying voluntary disclosure practices
impacts on smaller investors who rely more heavily on standardized SEC reporting
potential increases in information asymmetry
implications for internal controls and disclosure governance processes

In practice, many larger or more mature issuers may continue quarterly reporting due to
investor expectations, debt covenants, analyst coverage, or market discipline. However,
providing conditional optionality may particularly benefit issuers with demonstrated
governance maturity and stable reporting environments.

Finally, I would encourage the Commission to continue emphasizing that governance
quality, operational accountability, and long-term value creation are not solely functions of
reporting frequency. Strong boards and management teams should remain focused on
transparency, risk oversight, capital allocation discipline, and sustainable enterprise
performance regardless of whether interim reporting occurs quarterly or semiannually.
Thank you for the opportunity to comment on the proposal.

Respectfully submitted,
Colleen Wold
