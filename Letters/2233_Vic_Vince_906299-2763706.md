Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Vic Vince

This proposal will lead to:

-Less investor insight
-Less retail engagement
-Less accountability for public companies
-Less transparency into company performance
-Less visibility into debt, dilution, cash burn, and operational progress
-Less ability for investors to make informed decisions

I strongly oppose the proposed amendments that would allow public companies to move from quarterly reporting to semiannual reporting.

Public companies receive the benefit of access to public capital markets. In exchange, investors should receive regular, standardized, and timely information about the companies they own. Quarterly reporting is one of the few tools that allows both retail and institutional investors to evaluate company performance on a consistent basis.

Reducing required interim reporting from quarterly to semiannual would create longer periods where investors are left without updated financial information. This is especially harmful for retail investors, who do not have the same access to management, private channels, analysts, or institutional research as large market participants.

Less frequent reporting would reduce visibility into important matters such as revenue trends, cash burn, debt levels, dilution, working capital, risk factors, and whether management is following through on prior statements and guidance. These are not minor details. They are core pieces of information investors need in order to make informed decisions.

The proposal suggests that companies could choose the reporting frequency that best serves the company and its investors. I believe this creates the wrong incentive. A company may prefer less frequent reporting because it reduces scrutiny, reduces administrative burden, or gives management more time before having to disclose poor performance. That does not necessarily serve investors.

Investors benefit from more transparency, not less.

In modern markets, where information moves instantly and technology has made reporting and analysis easier than ever, reducing reporting frequency feels like a step backward. If anything, regulators should be encouraging greater transparency, better disclosure, and more frequent access to standardized information.

Semiannual reporting would not create a fairer market. It would widen the information gap between companies and investors, and between institutional investors and retail investors.

For these reasons, I respectfully urge the Commission not to adopt this proposal. Public companies should continue to provide quarterly reports so investors can make informed decisions based on timely, comparable, and legally filed information.
