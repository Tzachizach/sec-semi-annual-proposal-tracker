Dear Ms. Countryman:
I write in support of the Commission's proposal to permit Exchange Act reporting companies to
satisfy their interim reporting obligations on a new semiannual Form 10-S in lieu of the quarterly
Form 10-Q.1 I am the founder and financial advisor at Treveri Capital LLC, and I have spent
more than twenty years in the financial markets as a floor broker, as a vice president of sales at a
major wirehouse, and now as an independent fiduciary serving individual investors. I review
issuer disclosures regularly in the course of that work. My comments reflect that practitioner's
vantage point rather than the perspective of any single issuer.

The proposal is, at its core, a modest and sensible expansion of issuer choice, and it serves a goal
I share as a fiduciary, encouraging more companies to enter the public markets and to remain in
them so that ordinary investors can participate in their growth. It does not abolish interim
reporting, and it does not weaken the antifraud regime, the Form 8-K current reporting
framework, or Regulation FD. It simply allows a company and its investors to choose the interim
reporting cadence that fits the company's size, stage, and shareholder base. I believe that choice
is overdue, that the benefits are tangible and well documented, and that the principal objection, a

I. Choice, Not Mandate: Let the Market Set the Cadence
The central virtue of the proposal is that it returns a decision to the parties best positioned to
make it. A pre-revenue biotechnology firm whose value turns on clinical milestones does not
communicate the same way as a mature consumer bank, yet both are bound today to an identical
quarterly rhythm. Allowing each issuer to elect its frequency annually lets the cadence follow the
business rather than the other way around. This is not a novel concept in our markets. Companies
already decide whether to split their stock, and a celebrated few, Berkshire Hathaway among
them, have famously declined to do so for decades without any loss of investor confidence. The
market absorbs that variation easily and prices the security accordingly.

Critics worry that issuers will rush to report less. The available evidence points the other way.
When the United Kingdom removed its quarterly reporting mandate in 2014, only about nine
percent of firms actually stopped reporting quarterly.2 The overwhelming majority kept the
quarterly cadence because their investors and analysts expected it, and the firms judged the
expense worthwhile. That is precisely the outcome a choice-based regime should produce.
Companies whose shareholders value frequent data will continue to supply it, because trading
interest, analyst coverage, and access to capital reward them for doing so. Companies whose
shareholders do not need it can redirect the savings to the business. The daily reality of trading
volume and investor demand will discipline the decision far more efficiently than a uniform
federal rule.

Lowering that fixed cost matters beyond any single balance sheet, though it should be kept in
proportion. The number of U.S. public companies has fallen markedly since its mid-1990s peak,
and capable firms now stay private far longer than they once did. The largest driver of that shift
is the abundance of late-stage private capital, which lets a company raise enormous sums without
ever facing public-market discipline, and no reporting reform will change that by itself. What the
Commission can address is the part within its control. The recurring cost of being public does not
scale down for a smaller issuer the way its revenue does, so the same obligation that is trivial for
a large company is a real constraint for a small one. A semiannual option will not pull a company
public on its own, and I do not suggest otherwise. It removes one disincentive at the margin, and
it removes it precisely where the burden bites hardest. For the retail investors a fiduciary like me
serves, anything that widens the universe of investable public companies, even modestly, helps
counter a market in which the fastest growth is increasingly captured in private hands before
ordinary savers can reach it.

short-term earnings target.5 Theoretical work reaches a compatible conclusion, showing that

This is, after all, a principal reason so many founders prefer to remain private. They want to run

judgment than a one-size rule written for every issuer at once.

IV. A Measured Step on the Cost of Being Public
There is a broader policy dimension as well, and it is one of proportion as much as principle.
Regulatory cost is not free, and it is not evenly borne. A fixed compliance obligation falls hardest
on the smallest and youngest issuers, which have the fewest people to absorb it, because the cost
does not shrink as the company does. Over time, costs of that kind tilt the decision at the margin
toward staying private rather than going public. This proposal will not reverse that tide on its
own, and I do not claim that it will. It is one sensible step, the removal of a single recurring cost
that the modern disclosure system has, in large part, already made redundant. The Commission
has framed the proposal in just those terms, as part of the Chairman's stated agenda to make
initial public offerings attractive again and to encourage companies to go and stay public. 7
Properly understood, the change is not a loosening of investor protection. The substantive
protections remain fully intact. It is the pruning of a procedural redundancy, undertaken where its
cost is least justified and most keenly felt.

That last point deserves emphasis because it answers the instinct that less reporting must mean
less protection. Since semiannual reporting was last permitted in 1970, the Commission has built
an entire architecture of timely disclosure around it. Form 8-K now compels disclosure of
material events within four business days, Regulation FD bars selective disclosure, and earnings

V. A Fair Word on the Principal Concern
Less frequent reporting can widen the information gap between disclosures. Research finds that
information asymmetry tends to grow across a reporting period until the next filing resets it, and
that semiannual cadences are associated with somewhat greater asymmetry than quarterly ones.8
In practice this can mean larger price gaps and longer price drift, because news that would have
been priced in gradually instead arrives in fewer, larger steps. A related concern is that
accountability is delayed, since investors receive fewer interim signals with which to monitor
management. Both concerns are genuine, and the Commission is right to study them.

They are, however, bounded and largely manageable. The asymmetry concern is itself a function
of the same information gap, not a separate harm, and it is mitigated wherever alternative data,
analyst coverage, and active trading interest exist, which is to say in the very names where it
would otherwise matter most. Just as important, the regime is elective. An issuer whose
shareholders would punish a wider information gap will simply keep reporting quarterly, exactly
as the UK experience predicts. The companies likely to elect semiannual reporting are
disproportionately those whose value is not revealed quarter to quarter in the first place. The
concern is real at the margin, but it does not outweigh the benefits, and the choice architecture of
the proposal channels issuers toward the cadence their own investors demand.

VI. Conclusion
Taken together, the case for the proposal is strong. It restores a sensible choice to issuers and
their investors, it lowers a fixed cost that bears most heavily on the smaller and newer companies
our markets need, it relieves a quarterly pressure that demonstrably distorts management
behavior, and it does all of this without disturbing the timely, antifraud-backed disclosures that
genuinely protect investors. The chief objection, a wider information gap, is real but limited, and
the elective design of the rule lets the market correct for it issuer by issuer. For these reasons I
respectfully urge the Commission to adopt the proposed amendments. I thank the staff for the
care evident in the release and appreciate the opportunity to comment.
