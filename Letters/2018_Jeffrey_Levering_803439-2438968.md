Commissioners:
I strongly oppose this rule for the following reasons:
1. Owners of a company deserve regular, accurate, and unfiltered financial
information from the companies in which they invest. Management has access
to this information as part of operating the business; if it is important for
management to know, it is equally important for investors to know.
2. Any cost savings from eliminating quarterly reporting requirements would be
insignificant. Companies already collect financial information at least monthly, and
management routinely has access to that information. The costs of gathering and
maintaining these data are already reflected in company operations and financial
reporting processes. Any potential savings are outweighed by the reduction in
transparency that this rule would permit.
3. Eliminating quarterly reporting will reduce market liquidity and slow the flow of
capital to investors and companies alike. Investors will be less willing to commit
capital to more speculative investments—such as smaller companies, emerging
technologies, and companies with limited operating histories—if updated financial
information may not be available for as long as six months. Reduced transparency
increases uncertainty, which in turn increases the cost of capital and discourages
investment.
My perspective is informed by managing my own investments since the 1980s and by
decades of involvement with the investment processes and technologies that support
professional investment management. Timely, accurate financial information is essential
to sound security valuation and informed investment decisions.
In summary, this proposal provides no meaningful benefit to investors, reduces
transparency, meaningfully increases investment risk, and should be rejected
summarily by the Commission.
Jeffrey Levering
Worcester, Mass.
