 Comments of the Auditing Standards Committee of the Auditing Section of the American
        Accounting Association on the SEC’s Semiannual Reporting Proposal

SUMMARY: On May 5, 2026, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) issued a request
for comment on its proposal to allow its registrants the option to reduce the frequency of interim
reporting from quarterly to semiannually (SEC 2026). This comment letter summarizes the views
of the participating members of the Auditing Standards Committee of the Auditing Section of the
American Accounting Association. We raise concerns about the proposal to provide issuers with
the option to reduce the frequency of financial reporting from quarterly to semiannually.

Generative AI technology has not been used in the preparation of this manuscript.

                                    I.      INTRODUCTION

       In May 2026, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC or the Commission) issued

a proposal to provide registrants with the option to file their interim financial information

semiannually rather than quarterly (SEC 2026). We appreciate the opportunity to provide

feedback on this proposal. This comment letter summarizes the participating committee

members’ observations related to the proposal. Overall, we have concerns that the proposal could

be harmful to investors, particularly retail investors who rely on SEC filings to obtain

information used for decisions to buy, hold, or sell securities.

                              II.     OVERALL OBSERVATIONS

Quarterly Reporting is Useful to Investors

       Prior accounting research suggests that investors find quarterly financial statement

information decision-useful, as evidenced by the strong market reaction to quarterly earnings

announcements (Landsman and Maydew 2002). In fact, market reactions to quarterly earnings

announcements have increased over time, particularly in the post-SOX era (Beaver, McNichols,

and Wang 2018). This research suggests that investors place greater value on quarterly financial

information today than in prior decades.




                                                  1
        Quarterly earnings announcements also elicit a stronger market reaction than other firm

news events, indicating that financial statement users view these disclosures as an important

source of information about firm performance (Beaver et al. 2018). A likely explanation for this

difference is that quarterly earnings announcements are accompanied by auditor-reviewed

financial statements, increasing the perceived reliability of the disclosure relative to other firm

communications (Schroeder 2016). Moreover, improvements in financial reporting quality and

internal controls following the SOX legislation may have strengthened investor confidence in

quarterly financial information, increasing its usefulness to capital markets.

Timeliness of Financial Information Impacts its Usefulness

       The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) Conceptual Framework suggests that

the proposed change could diminish the usefulness of financial information:

       Timeliness means having information available to decision makers in time to be
       capable of influencing their decisions. Generally, the older the information is, the
       less useful it is. (FASB 2024, 44).

       Information that is provided semiannually is less timely than information provided

quarterly. Generally accepted accounting principles recognize the importance of interim financial

reporting in providing users with timely information regarding changes in a firm’s financial

position and operating results between annual reporting periods. According to ASC 270-10-45-1,

“Interim financial information is essential to provide investors and others with timely

information as to the progress of the entity” (FASB 2026).

Comparability and Consistency of Financial Information Impact its Usefulness

       The FASB’s conceptual framework emphasizes the importance of comparability as an

enhancing qualitative characteristic of accounting information:

       Users’ decisions involve choosing between alternatives, for example, selling or
       holding an investment, or investing in one reporting entity or another.

                                                  2
       Consequently, information about a reporting entity is more useful if it can be
       compared with similar information about other entities and with similar information
       about the same entity for another period or another date (FASB 2024, 43).

       The proposal could impair both intertemporal (within-firm) and cross-sectional (across-

firms) comparability. In addition, the proposal's annual election mechanism may reduce

consistency in reporting practices over time. Under the proposed framework, a firm could report

quarterly in one year, elect semiannual reporting in the next, and subsequently return to quarterly

reporting in a later period. Collectively, reductions in timeliness, comparability, and consistency

may make it more difficult for financial statement users to evaluate performance trends,

benchmark firms against peers, and interpret changes in reported results, thereby reducing the

decision-usefulness of financial information.

Potential Adverse Impacts on Investors and Investees

       While quarterly reporting imposes regulatory costs on firms, reducing reporting

frequency could place investors at a disadvantage by providing fewer timely updates on firm

performance, increasing information asymmetry and uncertainty. This reduction in the

availability and reliability of information may contribute to greater market volatility, reduced

liquidity, and a higher cost of capital as investors demand additional compensation for bearing

increased risk. In addition, quarterly earnings releases without reviewed financial statements may

be viewed as less reliable, potentially reducing investor confidence in the disclosed information.

Accordingly, any proposal to reduce reporting frequency should be evaluated not only in the

context of the loss of information timeliness but also in the context of the potential loss of

information credibility associated with reviewed interim financial statements. Collectively, less

frequent information could decrease its decision usefulness.




                                                  3
       The proposal may also create unintended signaling effects in capital markets. Although it

is ultimately an empirical question which firms would elect to report semiannually, investors

may interpret reporting frequency as a signal of firm quality, transparency, or governance. This

concern is consistent with prior research showing that corporate disclosure reduces information

asymmetry between managers and outside investors and plays an important role in capital-

market functioning (Healy and Palepu 2001). For example, larger firms may continue to report

quarterly, while smaller firms disproportionately elect semiannual reporting. Over time, quarterly

reporting could become a de facto best practice, causing investors to view firms that elect

semiannual reporting less favorably, regardless of their underlying economic performance. Such

signaling effects could harm investors by introducing new uncertainty into the market – and may,

at the same time, harm firms seeking to raise additional capital. Relatedly, prior research finds

that more frequent financial reporting is associated with lower information asymmetry and a

lower cost of equity capital (e.g., Fu, Kraft, and Zhang 2012).

       Beyond these concerns, the proposed rule may also reduce the availability of

standardized, machine-readable financial information. Although many firms may continue to

issue quarterly earnings releases, a shift away from quarterly SEC filings could substantially

reduce the amount of XBRL-tagged financial data available to investors and other market

participants. Unlike SEC filings, earnings releases are not subject to the same level of

standardization, making them more difficult to aggregate, compare, and analyze across firms.

The loss of XBRL-tagged quarterly information may increase information-processing costs for

investors, analysts, researchers, and automated trading systems while reducing the accessibility

and comparability of financial information. Consequently, the proposal may impose unintended

costs on financial statement users even if it reduces reporting costs for certain firms.

Structural Costs, Constraints, and Market Frictions

       The proposed rule may create significant transition costs for firms that elect semiannual

reporting and later return to quarterly reporting. This concern is particularly relevant because the

proposal contemplates an annual election, allowing firms to change reporting frequencies from

one year to the next (SEC 2026). Currently, SEC Regulation S-X requires interim financial

statements included in quarterly reports on Form 10-Q to be reviewed by the auditor. The

proposed changes would maintain the interim review requirement, clarifying that the interim

financial statements may be “included in quarterly reports on Form 10-Q or semiannual reports

on Form 10-S” (SEC 2026, 175). Since the proposed rule would not require reviews of quarterly

information included in Form 8-K for issuers electing semiannual reporting, the first and third

quarter financial information for companies electing semiannual reporting may not have been

subject to review by the independent auditor. Consequently, if a firm subsequently returns to

quarterly reporting, auditors would be required to perform additional procedures to obtain

sufficient support for comparative quarterly information presented in future filings. This

reporting pattern would require reconstructing and reviewing previously unreviewed quarterly

information. As a result, any short-term compliance savings associated with semiannual

reporting may be partially offset by substantial transition costs when firms change reporting

frequencies across reporting periods.

       The proposed rule change may provide fewer benefits than anticipated because a

significant portion of firms may be functionally unable to implement semiannual reporting due to

existing regulatory, contractual, or governance requirements. For example, many financial

institutions and insurance companies are already subject to separate quarterly regulatory

reporting requirements. In addition, many private debt agreements and lending arrangements



                                                 5
require borrowers to provide creditors with quarterly financial information. Firms may also

continue preparing quarterly financial information to satisfy the information needs of boards of

directors, audit committees, investors, and other stakeholders. As a result, many firms may be

required to continue preparing quarterly financial information regardless of SEC reporting

requirements, thereby limiting the proposal's anticipated cost savings.

Potential Audit Consequences

       Quarterly reviews provide recurring auditor involvement that may facilitate the earlier

identification and resolution of accounting issues before they affect annual financial statements.

This role is consistent with PCAOB interim review standards, which describe interim reviews as

involving analytical procedures and inquiries of financial and accounting personnel and requiring

auditors to update their knowledge of the company’s business and internal control as they relate

to interim financial reporting (PCAOB AS 4105). Because reviews occur throughout the year,

they create multiple opportunities for auditors to identify accounting errors, challenge

management estimates, evaluate unusual transactions, and address emerging financial reporting

issues before they become entrenched. Eliminating two review periods may increase the time

during which material weaknesses go undetected, potentially increasing the costs to remediate

when eventually discovered. This concern is particularly important because PCAOB standards

recognize that a material weakness can exist even when the financial statements are not

materially misstated and that the ICFR audit is designed to support the auditor’s opinion on

internal control effectiveness as of year-end (PCAOB AS 2201). In some cases, issuers may not

have sufficient time to remediate material weaknesses before the reporting period ends, leading

to more adverse internal control reports and opinions (and the related capital market

consequences). Consistent with this concern, Barr-Pulliam, Brown-Liburd, and Sanderson (2022)



                                                 6
find that internal control opinions affect perceptions of audit quality, assurance, and auditor

negligence, highlighting that adverse ICFR reporting outcomes, in some settings, can have

consequences beyond the disclosure itself. In addition, auditors currently identify and resolve

many issues during quarterly reviews before they materially affect annual financial reporting.

Delays in identifying and resolving financial reporting issues may increase the magnitude of any

required year-end corrections or adjustments (making these adjustments more difficult for

auditors to negotiate).

       Quarterly reviews also allow auditors to maintain a current understanding of client risks

throughout the year. Recurring interactions with management provide opportunities to identify

changes in operations, personnel, information systems, internal controls, and economic

conditions that may affect the risk of financial reporting. Less frequent auditor involvement may

reduce awareness of these developments and impair the timely identification of emerging

accounting issues. As a result, risk assessments may become less timely, requiring auditors to

modify planned procedures later in the engagement. This may reduce audit efficiency and

increase audit effort as issues that could have been identified and addressed earlier are instead

resolved closer to year-end.

       In addition, reducing the number of quarterly reviews may not eliminate audit work;

instead, it may shift a portion of that work to periods when audit firms already face significant

resource constraints. Prior audit research finds that workload compression and high auditor

workloads are associated with lower audit quality, including through increased pressure on audit

teams and reduced capacity to perform and review audit procedures effectively (e.g., López and

Peters 2012; Persellin, Schmidt, Vandervelde, and Wilkins 2019). To the extent that accounting

issues, material weaknesses, or unusual transactions are identified later in the review or audit



                                                  7
process, auditors may require additional time to investigate and resolve those matters under

tighter deadlines. Such workload compression may negatively affect audit quality and harm

investors. In some cases, the additional time required to investigate and resolve issues identified

later in the process could delay the issuance of audited financial statements, thereby creating

other compliance costs for firms and further reducing the timeliness of financial information

available to investors.

        If companies continue issuing quarterly earnings releases after quarterly reviews are no

longer required, the perceived reliability of those disclosures may decline as auditor involvement

becomes optional. Under the proposed framework, firms may continue providing quarterly

information to investors while no longer obtaining auditor reviews of the underlying financial

information. This concern is consistent with evidence that preliminary earnings releases issued

before audit completion are more likely to be revised, suggesting that auditor involvement can

improve the reliability of earnings information released to capital markets (e.g., Bronson, Hogan,

Johnson, and Ramesh 2011). Companies facing pressure to meet earnings targets may have

greater discretion over unreviewed quarterly disclosures, potentially increasing uncertainty

regarding the credibility of reported results. As a result, investors may place undue reliance on

risky and unreviewed interim disclosures. Prior research finds that lower financial reporting

frequency can increase investors’ reliance on alternative sources of earnings news, which may

create excessive information spillovers from peer firms (e.g., Arif and De George 2020). Over

time, this could lead to investor losses, greater market volatility, and higher costs of capital for

registrants.

Potential Unintended Consequences for the Audit Market

       The proposal may also have unintended consequences for the audit services market.

Some audit firms may view firms that elect semiannual reporting as presenting greater

engagement risk (e.g., Johnstone 2000). First, electing semiannual reporting will reveal valuable

information about registrants’ control environment. Doing so could easily become an important

risk factor for audit firms to consider in their engagement acceptance/continuance decisions, risk

assessments, and pricing. Prior research finds that internal control weaknesses are associated

with audit fee premiums and that audit fees may remain elevated for some firms even after

material weaknesses are remediated (e.g., Hogan and Wilkins 2008; Munsif, Raghunandan,

Rama, and Singhvi 2011). Additionally, auditors will inevitably interact less with management

under a semiannual reporting routine, relative to a quarterly reporting regime. This will result in

fewer opportunities to evaluate emerging accounting issues and identify potential financial

reporting problems before year-end. As a result, certain audit firms may become less willing to

serve firms that elect semiannual reporting or may require higher fees to compensate for the

additional risk. To the extent this occurs, affected firms may face reduced auditor choice and

potentially higher audit costs. In addition, if firms electing semiannual reporting are concentrated

among a smaller subset of audit firms, the proposal could have unintended consequences for

audit quality and competition within the audit market.

                                  III.    ECONOMIC ANALYSIS

Critique of the Economic Analysis and Short-termism Assumptions

       The Commission’s economic analysis suggests a causal link between quarterly reporting

and managerial short-termism and there is some evidence consistent with this view (Kajuter,

Lessenich, Nienhaus, and Van Gemmern 2022). For example, Kraft, Vashishtha, and



                                                 9
Venkatachalam (2018) find that companies decreased investments in fixed assets after the United

States increased the frequency of interim reporting to quarterly. However, there is also evidence

that contradicts the view that increased reporting frequency leads to higher levels of managerial

myopia. For example, Nallareddy, Pozen, and Rajgopal (2021) find that changing the frequency

of reporting requirements has essentially no impact on firm investment decisions.

       In its proposal, the Commission argues that “it is difficult to empirically estimate the

counterfactual value of semiannual reporting” with respect to “the issue of what reporting

frequency would be optimal for investors and companies” (SEC 2026, 98). However, research

confirms that higher reporting frequency reduces information asymmetry and financing frictions,

with higher frequency associated with a lower cost of equity capital (Fu, Kraft, and Zhang 2012).

Moving to a semiannual cadence doubles the length of the inter-announcement window, leading

to a broader buildup of adverse selection risk and a potentially higher net cost of equity capital.

Adverse Impact on Continuous Monitoring Framework

       The Commission acknowledges in the proposal that “less frequent reporting could delay

the identification and resolution” of misstatements and control deficiencies (SEC 2026, 119).

Less timely involvement of the auditor in significant unusual transactions and new types of

transactions will delay the detection and correction of misstatements as well as the identification

and remediation of internal control deficiencies. This may lead to higher audit fees that offset

any savings from eliminating reviews of the first and third quarters. For example, unremediated

control deficiencies will increase the risk of material misstatement and the extent of required

audit procedures necessary to address the heightened risk.




                                                 10
Potential Impact on Market Competition for Audit Services

        The Commission notes that firm size and operating complexity are likely to affect issuers’

reporting frequency decisions (SEC 2026, 122). We suspect that smaller issuers will be more

likely to choose less frequent reporting. These issuers are more likely to be served by smaller

audit firms. Thus, the estimated 12% reduction in audit fees resulting from this proposal may fall

disproportionately on smaller audit firms and could lead to further concentration of the audit

market, a systemic risk that is not currently addressed in the proposal. 1

                        IV.      RESPONSES TO SELECTED QUESTIONS


Question 20: In connection with any adoption of the proposal, should there be a new
requirement for semiannual filers that announce or release earnings for the first or third
quarters of their fiscal year (i.e., the periods that would later be subsumed in Forms 10-S
and 10-K but for which there would be no quarterly report filed with the Commission)—
that financial information in any first or third quarter earnings releases be reviewed by an
independent public accountant? If so, would any changes to current auditing standards
(e.g., governing reviews) be required?

        Semiannual filers that choose to announce or release quarterly earnings should be

required to have their financial statements reviewed by their external auditor. At the time the

Commission adopted the rule to require interim reviews, it cited the need for more discipline in

the financial reporting process to address increasing concerns about inappropriate earnings

management (SEC 1999). Ettredge, Simon, Smith, and Stone (2000) find that quarterly reviews

result in more adjustments in the quarters and fewer adjustments at year-end. Moreover, allowing

companies to choose semiannual reporting to avoid interim reviews while still reporting

quarterly earnings does not seem appropriate.

Question 24: Would the nature and extent of procedures that an independent public
accountant performs during a review change depending upon whether the independent
public accountant is performing a review over a fiscal semiannual period or a fiscal
quarterly period? Would independent public accountants conducting reviews do the same
amount of work for a fiscal semiannual period as they currently do for two quarterly fiscal
periods on a combined basis? Would an independent public accountant experience any
impact on efficiency or economies of scale when conducting reviews and annual audits
under semiannual reporting versus under quarterly reporting for the same company?
Would any changes to independent public accountants’ review or audit procedures or any
impact on efficiency or economies of scale result in changes in costs to companies? If so,
describe the impact and whether the impact could vary depending upon the size of the
registrant subject to the review.

       Interim reviews consist primarily of inquiry and analytical procedures. However, auditors

typically perform audit procedures on significant unusual transactions in the period in which

those transactions occur. Reducing the number of interim reviews from three to one will

adversely affect the auditor's ability to identify and perform relevant audit procedures, delaying

those procedures until later in the year. Moving audit work to busier times has the potential to

significantly adversely impact audit quality (Heo, Kwon, and Tan 2020; Lopez and Peters 2012).


Question 25: Would companies that elect semiannual reporting retain their independent
public accountant to perform a review of their financial statements at the end of each
quarter either to: (1) support financial information that is used for purposes of a quarterly
earnings release (notwithstanding that, as noted above, there is no Commission
requirement for a quarterly earnings release to be reviewed by an independent public
accountant), or (2) guard against the possible need for a quarterly review to be performed
should the company decide to change back to quarterly reporting in a future period (where
that period would require comparative quarterly data for the prior year)?

       We do not know whether semiannual filers would voluntarily elect to have their quarterly

financial statements reviewed by their external auditors. We therefore recommend that the

                                                12
Commission require semiannual filers who choose to announce or release quarterly earnings to

engage their auditor to perform interim reviews prior to release.


Question 26: For semiannual filers, what impact would a shift to semiannual reporting
have on: (1) companies’ disclosure controls and procedures, (2) companies’ internal control
over financial reporting, and (3) independent public accountants’ strategy and approach
for the annual audit of companies’ internal control over financial reporting or financial
statements? With semiannual reporting, is there a potential for a material increase in the
risk that material misstatements (either due to error or fraud) or control deficiencies are
not timely detected by or communicated to the independent public accountant thereby
limiting potential remediation of these issues by the issuer? Please provide any data related
to these questions.

       The shift to semiannual reporting will result in less frequent evaluation of the

effectiveness of disclosure controls and internal controls over financial reporting. As a result,

management and auditors may detect internal control deficiencies less timely, increasing the risk

of material misstatement in interim and annual periods.
