My name is Laura Bowen. I’m an accountant and a retail investor, and I’m writing to oppose SEC proposed rule S7-2026-15. This proposal would meaningfully harm ordinary investors while creating significant advantages for corporate insiders.

The modern market moves fast. Six months is a long time for material developments like failed product lines, ballooning debt, and litigation exposure to go undisclosed. Right now, companies are required to surface that information every quarter. Under this proposal, they could sit on it for six months while executives, who always have a clear picture of how the business is performing, have plenty of time to act before retail investors ever find out. That information gap is not a minor inconvenience. It’s a structural disadvantage for anyone who doesn’t have a seat at the table.

As an accountant, the fraud implications are what concern me most. Quarterly filings require companies to regularly reconcile, disclose, and certify their financials under penalty of law. That reporting cadence is one of the most important safeguards in public markets. Enron, WorldCom, and the conditions that led to the 2008 financial crisis were all made possible, in part, by insufficient transparency in financial reporting. Quarterly reporting doesn’t prevent fraud, but it significantly limits how long fraudulent activity can go undetected before someone outside the company sees the numbers. Moving to semiannual reporting doubles that window. It gives bad actors more time, more cover, and more opportunity to cause serious harm before anyone on the outside has a chance to catch it. And when it finally surfaces, ordinary investors absorb the loss, not the executives who signed off on the filings.

The SEC’s core mandate is to protect the investing public. This proposal undermines that mandate directly. I urge the Commission to reject Rule S7-2026-15 and maintain the current quarterly reporting standard.

Thank you for your time and consideration,
Laura Bowen
