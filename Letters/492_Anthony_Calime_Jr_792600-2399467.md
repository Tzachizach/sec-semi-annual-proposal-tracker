Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Anthony Calime Jr.

I am an individual, self-directed investor who manages my own retirement and education investment accounts, including two 401(k) accounts and a 529 college savings account. I am concerned that allowing public companies to move from quarterly reporting to semiannual reporting would negatively affect my ability to make informed investment decisions.

As an individual investor, I rely on timely, standardized, and comparable company information to evaluate risks, track business performance, rebalance my accounts, and make responsible long-term investment choices. Reducing required reporting from quarterly to semiannual would create longer periods where investors like me have less reliable information about a companyâs financial condition.

Background
Quarterly reports help individual investors monitor company performance throughout the year. These reports provide important updates on revenue, earnings, cash flow, debt, margins, business risks, management discussion, and other financial information. While large institutions may have access to professional analysts, private research, management meetings, and advanced data tools, individual investors typically rely heavily on public filings.

My investment decisions affect long-term family goals, including retirement security and education funding. Because I self-manage my accounts, I need consistent and timely public information to evaluate whether companies remain suitable for my portfolio.

Semiannual reporting may reduce administrative burden for companies, but it may also reduce transparency for investors. A six-month reporting gap can allow material changes in business performance, debt levels, liquidity, competitive position, or operational risk to build up before ordinary investors receive a complete update.

Assessment
Moving to semiannual reporting would likely disadvantage individual investors in several ways.

First, it would reduce transparency. A lot can change in six months, especially in volatile sectors such as technology, banking, healthcare, energy, consumer discretionary, and small or mid-cap companies. Without quarterly reports, I would have fewer reliable checkpoints to assess whether a companyâs fundamentals are improving or deteriorating.

Second, it may increase information inequality. Large institutional investors often have more resources to interpret market signals, access expert research, and react quickly to partial information. Individual investors may be left relying on rumors, headlines, analyst opinions, or stock price movements instead of standardized public filings.

Third, it could increase market volatility. If investors receive fewer formal updates, each semiannual report could carry more surprise risk. That could lead to sharper stock price reactions when information is finally released, making it harder for individual investors to manage risk.

Fourth, it could make portfolio management harder. I use company information to decide whether to hold, buy, reduce, or sell positions across retirement and education accounts. Semiannual reporting would make it more difficult to identify changes in earnings quality, cash flow, debt, business outlook, and risk exposure in a timely manner.

Fifth, it could reduce accountability. Quarterly reporting creates regular discipline for management teams and boards. Less frequent reporting may make it easier for problems to remain hidden longer, which could harm shareholders who depend on timely disclosure.

I understand the concern that quarterly reporting can encourage short-term thinking. However, I believe the better solution is to discourage excessive short-term earnings guidance, not reduce the frequency of historical financial reporting. Investors need actual financial results more often than twice per year.

Recommendation
I recommend that regulators preserve quarterly reporting requirements for public companies. If semiannual reporting is allowed, it should include strong investor protections, such as:

Companies should still be required to provide timely quarterly financial updates or key performance data. 

Any company choosing semiannual reporting should clearly explain why the change benefits shareholders.

Companies should be required to disclose material changes promptly and in a format that is easy for retail investors to understand.

Investor protections should prioritize equal access to information for individual investors, not just institutions.

Regulators should distinguish between quarterly earnings guidance and quarterly financial reporting. Reducing unnecessary short-term guidance may be reasonable, but reducing historical financial reporting would harm transparency.

As a self-directed individual investor managing retirement and education accounts, I believe quarterly reporting is essential to fair, informed, and responsible investing. Moving to semiannual reporting would reduce the quality and timeliness of information available to ordinary investors and would make it harder for me to protect my familyâs long-term financial goals.
