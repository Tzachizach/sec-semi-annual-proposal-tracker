Re: File No. S7-2026-15 — Proposed Amendments Permitting Optional Semiannual Reporting

To the Securities and Exchange Commission:

I appreciate the opportunity to provide comments on the proposed rule permitting optional semiannual reporting for eligible public companies.

This comment responds specifically to:

Request for Comment 18: "Would this practice create any new or heightened investor protection concerns?"

and

Request for Comment 26: "For semiannual filers, what impact would a shift to semiannual reporting have on: (1) companies' disclosure controls and procedures, (2) companies' internal control over financial reporting, and (3) independent public accountants' strategy and approach for the annual audit of companies' internal control over financial reporting or financial statements? With semiannual reporting, is there a potential for a material increase in the risk that material misstatements (either due to error or fraud) or control deficiencies are not timely detected or communicated to the independent public accountant thereby limiting potential remediation of these issues by the issuer? Please provide any data related to these questions."

There are conditions that based on my experience when present generate serious concern for a material increase in the risk that material misstatements (either due to error or fraud) or control deficiencies are not timely detected by or communicated to the independent public accountant thereby limiting potential remediation of these issues by the issuer. These conditions also indicate new or heightened investor protection concerns when coupled with semiannual reporting.

A shift to semi-annual reporting under the current disclosure requirements for public companies with recent material revisions, material misstatements, and material period-end financial reporting control failures especially when coupled with material significant deficiencies in ICFR at year-ends presents serious risks when:

1) The compounding effect of material extraordinary risks to financial reporting from fraud or operational problems that present themselves as material control failures to period end financial reporting, occurring often alongside near-material failures in ICFRs such as operational controls for material line items at the entity level that are sometimes even remediated in the last quarter or classified as significant deficiencies at year-end and avoid public disclosure at all. The effect of current disclosure practices results in companies avoiding public reporting of significant deficiencies, including some longstanding issues, that often occur alongside recent period-end financial reporting control failures under current disclosure law through classification as a significant deficiency at year end (not explicitly required to be disclosed at all sometimes, but disclosure might occur under current materiality principles). Significant deficiencies can be widespread in both ICFR operational and period end financial reporting controls after an ICFR control failure is removed from public disclosure. While the condition alone of an ICFR material failure in period end financial reporting in my view should prevent semiannual reporting, a significant deficiency in an operational ICFR can linger after the period-end financial reporting control has been removed from public view in disclosures in the 10-K. This information is decision useful because conditions listed above may lead to unexpected costs to investors in material line items like those that could be directly used to value the company in the event of going concern risk that may appear in the future. Late filing slightly complicates the picture and rules should also be considered to reduce the burden for these companies in the short rather than medium-term which semi-annual reporting might accomplish.

2) There are longer lead times until corrective action will be taken in practice towards most now undisclosed revisions due to the current frequency of management quarterly reviews and inquiry from auditors as compared to semiannual reporting. Semiannual reporting would give companies much less chances over time to put into practice corrective action which sometimes may take a couple years to get correct. Quarterly reporting entails more frequent account reconciliations that often help discover errors in practice, management reviews, and important conversations quarterly about material balances that prevent surprises with unnecessary costs/overruns at year end that are common in emerging growth companies with these conditions present.

These risks are hard for retail investors to assess fully under the current disclosure requirements, especially the disclosures made in practice. Coupled with semiannual reporting, the current disclosure requirements seem very inadequate to protect all investors' interests.

I think that narrowing the scope of who will be eligible for semiannual reporting to not include public companies with these conditions present for at least three years after they are present will increase capital markets efficiency and decrease the administrative burden on companies with proper corporate reporting and governance priorities. A negative effect to information provided would be from decreased comparability and consistency in information provided by companies and in between years. A forced change would probably be problematic for these reasons but adjustments could possibly be provided by the companies if possible.

Important Considerations for Semi-Annual Reporting

1. Revisions when in total material over a three year period because of the compounding effect through quarters of most minor errors seen in practice. Materiality should be considered over at least a three year rolling period to prevent intentional one year shifts and one-off operational errors leading to an increased administrative burden that is unproductive to protect investor interests. Disclosures should be made considering qualitative factors like those listed in SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin No. 99 (SAB 99). Disclosures about the scope of total revisions to retail investors are often not provided in practice since some may be disclosed exclusively to institutional investors. Total quantitatively would be decision-useful information provided to retail investors about the company's progress toward improving corporate governance.

2. Material Restatements: Important to mention with consideration of qualitative factors like those listed in SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin No. 99 (SAB 99).

3. Period End Financial reporting material control failures in the past three years and otherwise undisclosed ICFR operational control failure remediation affecting material line items when occurring in the last quarter or still classified as a significant deficiency at year end for those following three years. These disclosures would also need to address steps taken to improve information technology when appropriate. Material control remediation in the last quarter of the year that is not subject to proper risk assessment, when combined with recent failures in period end financial reporting, present fraud and operational risks that should be disclosed to shareholders. Both problems indicate semiannual reporting will not adequately protect investor interests due to compounding effects of material fraud and operational risk.

Excluding companies that exhibit risky characteristics from semiannual reporting is key to address concerns that semiannual reporting will make fraud or operational problems harder to detect quickly based on practical experience with following control failures and resulting impacts to retail investors. Narrowing the rules will also provide additional incentives for companies to avoid improperly taking advantage of these conditions that are common in emerging growth companies and promote proper governance practices.

Impact on Public Companies

What percentage of emerging growth companies have control failures in ICFR? As of 2017, among the 1,862 EGC filers, 1,355 provided a management report on internal control over financial reporting in their most recent annual filing. Of those 1,355 companies, approximately 46% reported material weaknesses.

- Accelerated Filers: 17.7%
- Non-Accelerated Filers: 38.0%
