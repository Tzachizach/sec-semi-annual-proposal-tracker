I am Abigail Rider, a recently retired CFO of a flagship state university. The plurality of the US stock market, about $49 trillion, is owned by retirement funds (such as 401(k)s, IRAs, and defined-benefit pension plans) managing retail investors’ assets. Retirement funds need to be able to evaluate their holdings on a timely basis in order to maintain the confidence of the millions of investors they represent. The loss of quarterly information would make the entire system more vulnerable to panics since the funds couldn’t evaluate their holdings frequently and relate their performance to the way the market is performing. Especially today when insider dealing has risen to a shocking degree, people need to know that their retirement assets are being continuously and rigorously evaluated and kept safe. Quarterly reporting ensures that this is the case. The argument that quarterly reporting makes companies avoid long term capital investment because it drags quarterly earnings down is specious - capital spending does not run through the P&L except as a provision after operating profit, and therefore does not depress operating earnings. It only reduces only the amount available for dividends to the shareholders and presumably the shareholders who hold the stock are on board with management’s growth plans. It’s the corporate officers who don’t want to have to worry about quarterly operating earnings, but that’s their job and they are paid huge salaries to do so. It is critical to maintain retail investor confidence in the market. Do not remove the quarterly reporting requirement. Thank you.
