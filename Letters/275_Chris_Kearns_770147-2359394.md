Subject: Public Comment on File No. S7-03-26 – Opposition to Semi-Annual
Reporting Transition
To the Securities and Exchange Commission:
I am writing as a self-directed retail investor and software developer who builds custom
analytical tools for portfolio management. I strongly oppose the proposal to shift from
quarterly (10-Q) to semi-annual (6-month) reporting requirements.
Moving to a semi-annual schedule does not "modernize" our markets. Instead, it
obscures them. Specifically, I would like the Commission to consider the following
points:


Institutional Information Asymmetry: In a 2026 market defined by highfrequency trading and real-time data, a six-month reporting gap is an eternity.
Large institutional firms have the resources to "proxy" a company's health
through alternative data methods such as satellite imagery, credit card scrapes,
and supply chain tracking, et. al. The individual investor relies on the SEC’s
periodic filings as the only verified "level playing field." Removing two of these
checks per year hands a permanent, unfair advantage to institutions and hedge
funds.



The "Cost Savings" Fallacy: The argument that this change saves significant
capital for issuers is mathematically flawed. These companies maintain the same
accounting systems, the same internal audit teams, and the same compliance
staff regardless of filing frequency. The cost of "preparing the materials" is a
decimal point on the balance sheet of a mid-to-large cap firm. Trading market
transparency for negligible administrative savings is a poor bargain for the
American retail investor.



The Risk of "Information Shocks": When reporting is frequent, the market can
digest data in smaller increments. Moving to 180-day windows will lead to
massive, destabilizing "gap-downs" or "gap-ups" when the reports finally drop.
For those of us who manage risk using a 13-week performance window, a sixmonth vacuum makes disciplined risk management nearly impossible.

I urge the Commission to maintain the current quarterly reporting standards.
Transparency should not be sacrificed for the sake of corporate convenience.
Sincerely,
/s/
Christopher G. Kearns
RigidBody Tech LLC
