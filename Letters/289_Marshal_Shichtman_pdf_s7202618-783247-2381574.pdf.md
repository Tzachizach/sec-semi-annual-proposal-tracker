Gentlepersons:
I would like to make a few comments on the proposal for proposed Form 10-S.

In an age when everything is moving faster, I am stunned that the Commission is
proposing rules to slow down the process. We have virtually real time trading and next day
settlements. Today’s market operates practically in real time. However, effectively all
transactions are premised upon information. Information the issuer disseminates primarily.
I fail to see how that makes the markets more efficient, more orderly, or builds investor
confidence. Those are the foundational items upon which the American capital markets are
built and upon which it’s reputation stands. I believe this proposal may have the opposite
effect. Moreover, it gives foreign markets a competitive advantage, at a time when new U.S.
issuers are at a low point. That is the overview.
I am skeptical that issuers will see an aggregate material savings by reducing two
Form 10-Qs. All the PCAOB auditors are still going to go through the same amount of
material for the year. However, they have two less looks by not reviewing the Form 10-Qs
allowing items to take root or fester. It is the old proverb of an ounce of prevention is equal
to a pound of cure. Or in modern parlance, maintenance is cheaper than breakdowns.
One of the subline benefits of all issuers doing quarterly reports, is all issuers are
doing quarterly reports. The uniformity creates an apples-to-apples effect. It is not simply
an issue of dividing by two, it is the regularity of the information provided. All issuers
reporting quarterly creates a momentum of consumer confidence.
I also have concerns with market mechanics and collateral effects. Quarterly
reporting is baked into a variety of market mechanics such as: provisions in financing
agreements for defaults based upon regular reporting (and I have doubts that creditors are
going to enjoy getting financial information less regularly, which creates an asymmetric nonpublic information issue); Rule 144 adequate public information (although entitlement
holders are availing themselves less frequently of Rule 144 exemptions, I believe the

I fully understand the need to be thorough and the magnitude of the Commission’s

than more clear due to the indigestibility of the magnitude of pages.
Please consider reducing the page count.
