Dear Chair and Commissioners:
To give investors adequate time to comment on such an extensive, far-reaching, and
dramatic change after more than half a century of practice, Better Markets 1 requests that the
Commission extend the comment period by an additional sixty days on the above-referenced
proposed rule change. 2 The proposed rule change would allow public companies to file reports
containing material information about their financial performance semiannually instead of
quarterly – as they have done since 1970. The current comment period of sixty days is not
enough time for the public to comment on a proposed change of this magnitude.
Better Markets looks forward to providing its own substantive comments on the proposed
rule change. At this time, however, it simply wants to ensure that all investors have the
opportunity to provide comments too. The proposed rule change affects all investors, yet it will
be difficult for investors—especially retail investors—to digest the proposal and provide
comments within 60 days. The proposal spans 279 pages and includes 58 requests for comment,
each with multiple associated questions. 3 Investors need more time to have a meaningfully
opportunity to comment.

The Commission needs to provide enough time to hear from investors for whom
quarterly reports matter. Quarterly reports provide investors with timely information about public
companies. They “are designed to increase transparency to investors by providing unaudited
financial statements and continuing insights into the company’s financial health throughout the
year.” 5 Quarterly reports make it possible for investors to more easily compare companies and
their financial performance and future profitability. In this way, quarterly reports provide
investors with the material information that they need to make informed investment decisions. 6
Quarterly reporting is especially important to retail investors. That is because most retail
investors lack any other way to obtain information about the public companies in which they
may want to invest. “Absent disclosure of information regarding the issuer of securities, average
retail investors have no mechanism for distinguishing between high and low quality enterprises,
hampering efficient allocation of capital.” 7 Retail investors therefore greatly rely on and “use the
information disclosed in the company’s periodic reports.” 8 Indeed, “the most important source of
information for their investment decisions is the company’s mandatory disclosure documents.” 9
In light of the importance of quarterly disclosures, the Commission must ensure that retail
investors have sufficient time to become aware of the proposal, review the proposal, and provide
comments.
Indeed, it is particularly important that the Commission hear from retail investors because
the proposal will disproportionately impact them. Institutional investors “can presumably obtain
from public companies whatever information they need to make informed investment
decisions.” 10 They have the resources and relationships to obtain information on a continuous
