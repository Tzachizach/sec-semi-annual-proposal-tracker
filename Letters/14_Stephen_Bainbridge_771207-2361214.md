selection dynamic would itself be valuable information for the Commission to observe as part of the natural 
experiment  discussed below Accordingly, the possible signaling effect is insufficient to cause me to favor a universal 
mandate of semi -annual reporting over an optionality regime.  
 3 A. The Theoretical Foundation for Mandatory Quarterly Reporting Is Weak  
The quarterly earnings reporting requirement traces its origins to the SEC's 1970 overhaul of the 
periodic disclosure system, itself a product of the Wheat Report's dissatisfaction with the then -existing 
Exchange Act disclosure regime. That regime rests on  the broader mandatory disclosure system enacted 
in the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, premised on Congress's view that "[t]here cannot be honest 
markets without honest publicity."4 
No one disputes that investors benefit from a steady flow of accurate information. The question is 
whether that flow requires a government mandate or whether market forces would produce adequate 
voluntary disclosure. This is the central question in the lon gstanding academic debate over mandatory 
disclosure, of course, but i t is directly relevant to evaluating the quarterly reporting requirement.  
The standard justification for mandatory disclosure rests on the identification of a market failure —
some reason why competitive market pressures would lead firms to disclose sub -optimally in the absence 
of a legal mandate. Three categories of market failure have been advanced  in defense of the mandatory 
disclosure system in general and the periodic disclosure reporting system in particular : (1) information is a 
public good, (ii) externalities, and (iii) the information asymmetry between issuers and investor s. 
Each of these alleged market failures remains highly contested  in this connection . In order for it to 
make sense, for example, t he public goods argument  requires not merely that information ha ve public 
goods characteristics, but also both that firms will in fact fail to disclose optimally and that government 
mandates will produce efficient disclosure levels. None of those  propositions are self-evident. As for the 
whether information —especially securities data and research —is a public good, information is no t subject 
to non -rivalrous consumption in the securities markets. Because US capital markets are efficient, at least 
in the weak - and semi -strong forms, i nformation is consumed by investors trading in response to it as 
soon as it is disclosed, thereby impounding it into the market price, and thereby becoming less valuable —
indeed worthless —to those that fail to immediately trade upon it. T  
The externalities argument posits  that “because the issuer does not share in the benefits of the 
externalities conferred on third parties and actually may suffer losses (e.g., by enlightening a competitor 
that is suddenly better able to compete), marginal private benefits from disclosure will f all short of its 
marginal social benefits and therefore issuer disclosure is likely to be sub -optimal.”5 But this line of 
argument proves too much, as it would justify mandating disclosures far beyond what the current system 
requires.  
Admittedly, the information asymmetry argument  is intuitively powerful . Insiders obviously 
possess considerabl y greater information than outsider investors. But this line of argument fails to take 
into account an issuer management's strong incentives to voluntarily disclose information . Issuer 
management frequently holds a substantial portion of their monetary net worth in issuer stock. Insiders 
also have made considerable investments in non -diversifiable, firm -specific human capital. Providing 
levels of disclosure dem anded by investors thus benefits insiders by lowering the issuer’s cost of 
capital —making firm failure less likely —and raising the value of the issuer’s stock . 
Turning from theory to empirical data, there is indirect evidence that there is no persistent market 
failure in US capital markets. Issuers making private placements —free from the mandatory disclosure 
regime —routinely provide disclosures comparable to those in registered public offerings, in response to 
investor demand and the pressure of reputational intermediaries such as underwriters.6 Many foreign

B. As With t he Mandatory Disclosure System  in General, the Quarterly Reporting 
Requirement  Functions Like an Ill -Calibrated Price Control  
A particularly trenchant objection to the current regime is that mandatory disclosure functions in 
ways not unlike price controls. A regulatory mandate is unlikely to peg required disclosure at the 
equilibrium point at which marginal cost equals marginal b enefit. As with any legal rule, the quarterly 
reporting mandate is likely to be both under - and over -inclusive. Under -inclusive disclosure rules harm 
investors by denying them needed information; over -inclusive rules harm investors by requiring firms to 
spend money on unnecessary disclosures —costs that ultimately come out of shareholders' pockets. 
Investors do not want management spending a dollar on disclosure unless that expenditure produces at 
least a dollar's worth of benefit.  
Different firms achieve disclosure equilibrium at different levels, and a uniform quarterly cadence 
is by definition over -inclusive for firms whose investors would be adequately served by semi -annual  
reporting and under -inclusive for firms whose investors might benefit from more frequent updates. The 
Commission's proposed optional system would allow companies and their investors to self -select into the 
reporting frequency that best reflects their part icular circumstances. Under a voluntary regime, 
managemen t has an incentive to provide disclosure until it achieves an efficient trade -off between 
lowering the cost of capital and the costs of disclosure preparation. The government has no comparable 
incentive and , accordingly,  a mandatory quarterly rule cannot be calibrated to the efficient level for each 
company.

The dramatic decline in publicly traded U.S. corporations has had serious consequences for the American

private equity funds, and their wealthy limited partners. By the time a company does reach public markets 
(if it ever does), much of the appreciation has already occurred. This dynamic has contributed to widening 
wealth inequality, since participation in private markets is largely limited to accredited investors who 
already meet high income or net -worth thresholds.  The shrinkage of the public markets thus represents 
not just an economic efficiency loss but a structural barrier that prevents ordinary Americans from sharing 
in the gains of entrepreneurial capitalism.

B. Promoting the Quality of Disclosure  
The Commission notes (at 114) that “[r]educed reporting frequency may also affect . . . reporting 
quality.” In fact, the impact may be positive rather than negative. Quarterly reports can become repetitive, 
boilerplate, and compliance -driven. Risk factors, MD&A, legal proceedings, and controls language may 
change little from quarter to quarter. A semiannual cadence might allow companies to provide more 
thoughtful analy sis of trends rather than mechanically updating marginal changes every three months.  
Put another way, more frequent disclosure is not always better disclosure. Fewer mandatory 
reports could lead to reports that are more substantive, better integrated with management’s actual view 
of the business, and less focused on immaterial quarter -to-quarter fluctuations.  
IV. The Arguments In Favor of Retaining Quarterly Disclosures Are Weak  
A. More Frequent Reporting Improves Market Efficiency and Price Accuracy  
Some argue that quarterly 10 -Qs help securities prices more quickly reflect issuer -specific 
information. In turn, or so they argue, improved pricing  improve s capital allocation, reduce s adverse 
selection, and may lower issuers’ cost of capital.  
This argument ignores the multiple ways in which information is conveyed to the marketplace . It 
also ignores the risk that qu arterly data can also add noise. Three -month periods may be too short to 
evaluate strategy, cyclicality, seasonality, investment payoffs, or one -time events. In addition, markets 
may overreact to small quarterly deviations, especially earnings misses, producing short -term volatility 
rather than better valuation.  
B. Assisting Investors with Trading Decisions  
According to a May 5, 2026, Bloomberg report, “[a] dvocates of quarterly reporting say 
transparency helps investors make crucial buy -and-sell decisions. ”11 If that consideration deserved much 
weight, however, it would argue for increasing the frequency of corporate reporting. After all, investors 
routinely make intra -quarterly trading decisions. If assisting with trading decisions matters as much as the 
advocates of the current system claim, the SEC should mandate daily reporting. But the SEC wisely has 
concluded that the costs of more frequent disclosure outweigh the claimed benefits.  
 In addition, nothing in the SEC proposal contemplates eliminating intra -quarterly disclosures on 
Form 8 -K. Companies will still be required to inform investors of critical news in real -time disclosures. If 
experience with semi -annual reporting proves that there are some categories of information investors do

C. Preventing Insider Trading  
The May 5th Bloomberg report posited that:  
Less frequent reports could also increase the risk of insider trading, as it would lengthen 
the time insiders could hold on to confidential information and potentially make illicit stock 
trades.  
This concern is reflected in Question 32 of the SEC proposing release:  
Would there be an increased risk of insider trading at companies that elect to report on a 
semiannual basis?13 
I do not believe that a shift from mandatory to optional quarterly reporting would significantly 
affect insider trading incentives. We can get at this issue by considering the argument that we need to 
regulate insider trading so as to prevent insiders from  delaying disclosure of corporate information.  As I 
have observed previously:  
[U]nwarranted delays in disclosing corporate information result in social costs. Despite 
the strong bias in the federal securities laws towards prompt disclosure of material 
developments, there are many circumstances in which the firm is permitted to delay  
disclosure, such as when it could be preempted by competitors or otherwise would be 
unable to take advantage of the development. This type of delay reduces market 
efficiency, but is justified because prompt disclosure in such circumstances would reduce 
the firm's incentive to produce socially valuable information. The resulting loss of market 
efficiency, however, requires that the delay be no longer than necessary for the firm to 
capture the development's value.

D. Promoting Comparability  
The Commission notes (at 66) that switching to semi -annual reporting “could result,” inter alia , in 
“reduced comparability.”  But s emi-annual reporting would still permit year -over-year and first -half-to-first-
half comparisons. Companies that benefit from providing quarterly information could voluntarily continue 
doing so. Investors could punish firms that withhold useful interim data.  
In any event, what really drives comparability is standardization. To be sure,  standardized 
disclosures have their own concerns. They tend towards boilerplate and discourage innovative 
presentations, to cite but two.  
Nevertheless, if insuring comparability across firms and across time periods is a concern what 
matters is the form of disclosure not its frequency. Comparability is assured by requiring financial 
statements to be prepared in accord with GAAP. It is ensured  by requiring all firms to disclose the same 
basic package of information. It is ensured by providing templates within which disclosures are made.  
E. Promoting Liquidity  
There is no doubt that m arkets are more liquid when traders trust that public information is 
reasonably current. But liquidity benefits likely are  concentrated in larger firms where analyst coverage 
and market data already exist. For smaller issuers, compliance costs likely  outweigh any liquidity gain. 
And, in any case,  investors can demand a liquidity discount from companies that voluntarily choose less 
frequent disclosure.

Before addressing the types of companies likely to elect semi -annual  reporting, it is important to

than switching to some alternative state, even when that stickiness cannot be explained on efficiency

reporting is undesirable . Rather, if take up of semi -annual reporting is slow initially, that fact  will simply

B. Smaller Companies with Higher Compliance Costs  
The most natural candidates for electing semi -annual  reporting are smaller reporting companies 
for whom the compliance burden of quarterly reporting is highest relative to the informational benefit 
investors derive from it. As noted above, t he quarterly preparation of Form 10 -Q—including financial 
statement preparation, auditor review, MD&A drafting, and officer certification —imposes fixed costs that 
fall disproportionately on companies with smaller finance and legal functions. For such companies, the 
margi nal cost of quarterly disclosure likely exceeds its marginal benefit to investors, and the optional 
semi -annual  system would permit them to reach the efficient equilibrium that the current mandatory 
regime prevents : 
Quarterly reporting can be burdensome, especially for smaller firms. For public 
companies that aren’t yet generating significant revenue, time -consuming 10 -Qs provide 
limited new information to investors. As such, capital -constrained firms must divert scar ce 
resources to preparing reports investors are likely to ignore. Directing those resources 
toward managing business operations or capital investments would better serve these 
firms and their investors.16 
Put another way, mandatory quarterly reporting imposes a one -size-fits-all disclosure cadence. Optional 
quarterly reporting would introduce some issuer -level tailoring.  
C. Companies with Longer -Horizon Investor Bases  
Companies whose investor bases have longer investment horizons are also more likely to elect 
semi -annual  reporting successfully. Companies with significant founder or family ownership, companies 
backed by patient institutional capital, and companies in industries where current -period earnings are 
poor proxies for long -run value are natural candidates.  
Conversely, companies with large active institutional ownership bases —pension funds, hedge 
funds, and other sophisticated investors who routinely demand more information rather than less —will 
face the strongest market pressure to remain on a quarterly schedule regardless of what the 
Commission's rules permit.

D. Industry Considerations  
Certain industries present a natural fit for semi -annual  reporting. Companies engaged in long -
gestation projects —biotech and pharmaceutical firms awaiting regulatory approvals, capital -intensive 
infrastructure and energy companies, and early -stage technology firms where current losses are priced 
against multi -year growth expectations —may find that quarterly disclosures add little decision -relevant 
information for investors already pricing future cash flows over extended horizons. The IPO evidence 
discussed above supports this intuition: investors in such companies are demonstrably capable of 
thinking far beyond the current quarter.  
Conversely, industries in which quarterly results are highly material to market valuations —
financial services firms, retailers with pronounced seasonal patterns, and cyclical businesses whose 
financial condition can change materially within a three -month window —are least likely to elect semi -
annual  reporting because investor demand for frequent updates in those sectors is strongest.  
VI. The Empirical Evidence Does Not Support the Claim That the Frequency of  
Reporting Impacts Management Decision -Making Timelines  
As the SEC notes at several points, there is a debate about the extent to which the frequency of 
periodic disclosure reporting promotes or discourages managers to make decisions that maximize short -
term performance at the expense of long -term performance. This debate seemingly drives the inquiry in

What impact would the proposed option to file semiannual reports on Form 10 -S 
have on Exchange Act reporting companies’ ability to focus on: (1) business operations, 
(2) growth, or (3) long -term business strategies?  
This question and the commentary in the report appears to assume that a short -term bias on the part of 
managers is inherently undesirable, which is a contestable proposition.19 More importantly, it also 
assumes that changing the frequency of reporting would change management incentives. I am skeptical 
of this argument for several reasons.  
First, the conventional wisdom that quarterly reporting drives short -termism is empirically 
contested. Economists Sanjai Bhagat and Glenn Hubbard have documented a considerable body of 
finance research consistent with managers and investors focusing on lon g-term shareholder value. Their 
observation that more than 80 percent of U.S. IPOs have negative earnings in the twelve months prior to 
going public —yet are valued at hundreds of millions or even billions of dollars —is powerful evidence that

Second, and more directly, the U.K. natural experiment provides a useful reality check. The 
United Kingdom introduced mandatory quarterly reporting in 2008 and then eliminated it in 2015 in favor 
of voluntary semi -annual  reporting. A careful study by Nallareddy and colleagues found that neither

proposed SEC rule extending the reporting period from three to six months should  not significantly affect

Third, while there are behavioral economics arguments —most notably the endowment effect —
that might support the view that shorter reporting periods exacerbate short -term biases, these arguments 
counsel for flexibility rather than a uniform mandate. Companies and investors differ in their susceptibility 
to such biases . A voluntary system would permit market participants to self -select into the reporting 
frequency that best serves their needs.  
Finally, to the extent managerial short -termism is a genuine problem, its sources likely run deeper 
than quarterly disclosure requirements. Executive compensation structures, activist investor pressures, 
and the fundamental mismatch between managerial care er horizons and corporate lifespans are more 
plausible causal factors.  
Accordingly, the SEC should not let concerns about management time horizons drive the decision  
either in favor of retaining the current quarterly reporting mandate or making quarterly reporting optional . 
Instead, that decision should rest on other considerations.  
VII. Miscellaneous Comments  
A. The Form 10 -K Check -the-Box Approach  
As proposed, the new rules contemplate that companies choosing semi -annual would opt in to 
that cadence by checking a box on the cover page of their annual Form 10 -K. Having done so, that 
election applies to  the following fiscal year  and cannot be changed except to correct an inadvertent error .  
This approach poses several issues:  
1. Companies will need to re -select the option annually.  This seems unobjectionable.  
2. For a non -accelerated filer Form 10 -K can be filed as few as 40 days before the first 
quarterly report would be due . A company that previously used quarterly reporting thus 
could switch to semi -annual reporting to avoid disclosing poor first -quarter results.

box was an inadvertent omission  when in fact the switch to semi -annual reporting 
is intended to delay disclosure of bad news.

delay disclosure of bad news.

quarterly to semi -annual reporting and explaining why it is making that switch. The press release should 
be filed with a Form 8 -K. If the company claims that i t inadvertently failed to check the box and now seeks

B. The Real Problem is Not the Cadence of Reporting But the Accompanying 
Requirements  
At least for larger established companies, t he costs of quarterly reporting likely do not consist primarily of 
collecting the requisite financial data . Many companies are continuously collecting financial data in real 
time. Automation and AI agents will continue to enhance their ability to have real time data readily 
available.  
Instead, the bulk of t he cost of periodic disclosure likely is incurred in the formal compliance process that 
accompanies  each filing . It is the need for SOX certifications, obtaining review of draft disclosures  by 
counsel , obtaining independent auditor reviews, holding disclosure committee meetings , and audit 
committee and board of director time reviewing the drafts. Going  from three Form 10 -Qs to one Form 10 -
S eliminates two full cycles of that process.  Yet, the SEC should not stop there. The SEC should consider 
not just the frequency  of disclosure, but also whether all of the compliance rigamarole that accompanies 
periodic disclosure is necessary.  
VIII. The Optional System Presents a Valuable Natural Experiment  
The proposed optional system would, if adopted, constitute a valuable natural experiment in the 
economics of voluntary disclosure. If companies given the option to report semi -annual ly nonetheless 
continue to report quarterly —whether in response to investor demand, institutional norms, or status quo 
bias—that evidence would tend to confirm that market forces are sufficient to produce optimal disclosure 
without a quarterly mandate, strengthening the case for reconsidering the mandatory disclosure regime 
more  broadly.  
Conversely, if companies migrate to semi -annual  reporting in significant numbers, the 
Commission and researchers would be able to observe whether investor welfare is adversely affected 
and whether analyst forecast accuracy declines. The U.K. experience suggests it would not: when the 
U.K. shifted to vo luntary semi -annual  reporting, analyst coverage declined modestly but forecast accuracy 
did not change significantly, suggesting that information continued to reach the market through alternative 
channels. The  Commission should monitor these outcomes carefully and use the resulting evidence to 
inform any future consideration of the periodic reporting system.  
Conclusion  
For the foregoing reasons, I support the Commission's proposed amendments permitting 
companies to elect semi -annual  reporting in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10 -Q. The theoretical 
foundation for mandatory quarterly reporting is weaker than is commonly assumed . The empirical 
evidence that quarterly reporting prevents managerial short -termism is not compelling . Market forces are 
likely to produce adequate disclosure in a voluntary system.  
Mandatory quarterly reporting imposes a uniform disclosure cadence on firms with different 
business cycles, investor bases, and information needs. That cadence may increase compliance costs, 
amplify short -term market pressure, encourage earnings management , and focus investors on noisy 
three -month results. A semiannual reporting requirement, paired with an option to continue quarterly 
reporting, would preserve a mandatory public disclosure floor while allowing firms and investors to 
determine whether quarte rly reporting is valuable in particular cases. Firms that benefit from quarterly 
transparency could continue it . Firms for which quarterly reporting is costly, repetitive, or misleading could 
shift to a less burdensome semiannual model, subject to market discipline and continuing event -based 
disclosure obligations.  
Chairman Atkins has correctly noted that the SEC has drifted away from its core mission of 
ensuring that issuers disclose material information. In recent years, the SEC has expanded or proposed 
expanding the scope of information required to include such ma tters as ESG factors (such as GHG 
emissions) that are of doubtful materiality to the typical investor. Observing what information firms supply

assess what information investors truly need. (I note in passing that the continually growing bulk of proxy

Lastly, t he optional system will also generate valuable empirical evidence about the merits of 
mandatory disclosure that the Commission could use to evaluate the periodic reporting regime more 
broadly.  The Commission should avail itself of this opportunity to evaluate not just the optimal cadence of

disclosure template has the advantage of promoting cros s-firm and cross -time comparability. Yet, it 
seems like ly that the burden of preparing periodic disclosure reports and the related compliance 
obligations falls more heavily on emerging growth companies, newly public companies, and micro - and 
nano -cap non -accelerated filers. Observing how those companies respon d to the change may give the 
Commission new insight into whether those firms would benefit from further regulatory relief.
