Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Skyler Mathis

From a leadership and cost-management standpoint, the initial concept behind this proposal appears well-intentioned and, at a high level, may seem attractive. However, when viewed through the lens of long-term operational effectiveness, reporting quality, and investor confidence, there are significant risks that warrant careful consideration.

One key concern is the likely reduction in dedicated external and SEC reporting teams within organizations. While this may appear to lower fixed costs, it could ultimately lead many companies to rely more heavily on recurring contractor support. From a leadership perspective, this model introduces inefficiencies and risk. A revolving cast of contractors often lacks deep institutional knowledge, which is critical for producing high-quality, consistent, and accurate external reporting. Over time, this could negatively impact the reliability of disclosures and increase risk for the company and its participants. Leadership teams rely on frequent and consistent reporting not only to meet regulatory requirements, but also to reinforce transparency and trust with investors. Reducing reporting frequency may unintentionally weaken that relationship and limit investors' ability to make informed decisions.

Additionally, reporting less frequently would necessitate a significant overhaul of interim reporting requirements. To compensate for reduced reporting frequency, companies would likely need to disclose more detailed information during interim periods. This could increase complexity rather than reduce burden, potentially undermining the proposal's objective. The SEC would also need to develop a much more robust interim reporting standard to ensure investors remain adequately informed about a company's financial and operational performance on a semi-annual basis.

Ultimately, while the proposal reflects thoughtful intent, leadership must evaluate whether it truly aligns with the broader responsibilities that come with being a public company. Although quarterly reporting may limit flexibility, it is an understood and accepted aspect of accessing public capital. Increased reporting, monitoring, and scrutiny are fundamental to protecting investors and maintaining confidence in the capital markets. From a leadership perspective, those expectations should not only be accepted — but embraced — as part of responsible corporate governance.
