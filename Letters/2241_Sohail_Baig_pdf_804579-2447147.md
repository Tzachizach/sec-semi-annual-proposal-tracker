Summary of Comment
The undersigned supports the Commission's effort to give domestic registrants the option to file on
Form 10-S in lieu of Form 10-Q under the proposed Semiannual Reporting framework, subject to the
qualifications set out below. The substantive point of the comment is that the success or failure of any
change in reporting cadence is determined, at the operating-company level, by the integrity of the
underlying receivables, cash, bank, reconciliation, and internal-controls discipline that produces the
reported numbers. Adjusting the cadence without strengthening the operating-layer financial-controls
infrastructure that feeds it would shift the timing of disclosure without improving its quality. The
Commission should pair the cadence option with explicit guidance that supports operating-layer
financial-controls modernization at mid-market and smaller registrants.

1. The Operating-Layer Financial-Controls Foundation Is the Binding Constraint
At the operating-company level, period-end reporting quality is a function of three connected systems:
(a) the accounts-receivable sub-ledger and its reconciliation to the general ledger; (b) the cash-
management module within the enterprise resource planning (ERP) platform, including bank-statement
automation, multi-bank and multi-currency reconciliation, and treasury workstation integration; and (c)
the internal-controls and audit-liaison work that produces audit-grade trace evidence at month-end and
quarter-end. These three systems are the binding constraint on the quality of any interim disclosure.
Adjusting the disclosure cadence to semiannual does not change the systems; it changes only the
frequency at which their output reaches investors.

The commenter's twenty-plus years of experience in this layer, including three completed ERP
migrations (Oracle E-Business Suite R11 in 2010, Oracle R12 in 2012, and SAP S/4HANA in 2022) at a
high-volume beverage manufacturer, indicates that the costs of period-end close are concentrated at
month-end six and month-end twelve of the fiscal year regardless of whether the registrant files a Form
10-Q at quarter-end three and quarter-end nine. The peer-reviewed empirical work the commenter co-
authored in Migration Letters (2024, Vol. 21, No. S11) corroborates this, with accounts-receivable
efficiency and quality standardization producing the highest empirical loadings on sustainable enterprise
performance among the four drivers tested.

2. Semiannual Cadence Will Benefit Some Registrants and Disadvantage Others
For smaller domestic registrants with stable revenue, low receivables volatility, and predictable working-
capital cycles, semiannual interim reporting on Form 10-S will reduce direct compliance cost and
management distraction without materially reducing the quality of the information reaching investors.
For registrants with seasonally volatile receivables, lumpy revenue recognition, or active working-capital
programs financed against the receivables base, semiannual cadence will leave significant performance
variance undisclosed for longer periods, which the credit and lending markets will price in through
tighter covenants, higher pricing, or reduced facility size.

The Commission's framework should accordingly: (a) confirm that election of semiannual reporting is
reversible without prejudice, so registrants can return to quarterly reporting if the operating profile
changes; (b) clarify the audit-readiness and internal-controls testing expectations for registrants that
elect semiannual reporting; and (c) preserve the existing Section 404 internal-controls attestation
regime in unchanged form, with no inference that semiannual cadence reduces the substantive Section
404 obligation.

3. Recommended Companion Guidance on Operating-Layer Modernization
The undersigned respectfully recommends that the Commission accompany any final rule with
companion staff guidance addressing the following operating-layer questions, which are the questions
that determine whether semiannual reporting works in practice:

First, expectations regarding mid-period reconciliation discipline. Registrants electing semiannual
reporting should be expected to maintain monthly bank and receivables reconciliations internally even
where quarterly external disclosure is not required, because the audit-readiness and internal-controls
infrastructure depends on month-end discipline at every month, not only at the months reported
externally.

Second, guidance on the Form 10-S MD&A discussion of mid-period variances. The Form should require
discussion of any material variance that occurred mid-period but resolved before the semiannual
reporting date, so that investors can understand the underlying volatility profile even when the period-
end snapshot is stable.

Third, clarification of the receivables and cash-flow disclosure expectations on Form 10-S. The proposed
amendments to Regulation S-X should be paired with explicit guidance that the receivables aging
analysis, the days-sales-outstanding trend, and the cash-conversion-cycle trend should be presented on

a comparable basis to the prior-period Form 10-Q disclosure, so that investors can construct continuous
time series across the cadence change.

4. The Cadence Change Should Not Reduce Investment in Operating-Layer Modernization
A risk in any move toward less frequent disclosure is that operating companies treat the reduced
cadence as license to underinvest in the operating-layer infrastructure. The Commission should make
explicit that the Form 10-S option does not change the substantive obligation to maintain receivables,
cash, bank, reconciliation, and internal-controls discipline at the same standard previously required to
support Form 10-Q. Mid-market registrants in particular face documented constraints in the cross-
platform ERP-financial-controls expertise needed to maintain that standard, as documented in the U.S.
Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook entry for Accountants and Auditors at
bls.gov/ooh/business-and-financial/accountants-and-auditors.htm and in the American Institute of
Certified Public Accountants pipeline reports at aicpa-cima.com. Strengthening that operating-layer
capacity is, in the commenter's view, the larger policy question of which the cadence question is one
part.

5. Closing
The undersigned supports the Commission's exercise of optionality on interim reporting cadence and
respectfully recommends that the final rule pair the Form 10-S option with the companion operating-
layer guidance described above. The Commission's attention to the integrity of operating-layer financial-
controls infrastructure, on which both quarterly and semiannual reporting ultimately rests, is the
foundation of investor confidence and capital-formation efficiency in the U.S. mid-market segment that
the proposed rule most directly affects.
