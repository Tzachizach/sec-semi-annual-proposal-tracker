The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) utilizes a standard 30- to 60-day notice-and-comment process to evaluate its rules. While the SEC occasionally explores altering these timeframes for specific rulemakings, advocates, industry panels, and lawmakers consistently push back against proposals that cut or shorten this period for several key reasons: 



 Inadequate Time for Deep Analysis: Complex financial regulations require significant time for institutional investors, analysts, and public interest groups to model economic impacts, compliance burdens, and risks. Shortened periods hamstring the SEC from gathering the expert feedback needed to craft surgical, effective proposals. 


 Disenfranchisement of Retail Investors: Shorter comment windows disproportionately harm individual and retail investors who lack dedicated legal and regulatory teams. They require additional time to review proposals and draft their perspectives. 


 Skewed Holiday Timelines: Comment periods that are hastily scheduled or overlap with major holiday seasons (such as Thanksgiving or Christmas) make it difficult for working professionals, nonprofits, and smaller entities to participate meaningfully. 


 Increased Risk of Unintended Consequences: Rushed rulemaking—whether it involves rescinding disclosures or changing reporting frequencies—can lead to poor oversight, "information asymmetry," and increased susceptibility to corporate fraud. 


 Loss of Diverse Perspectives: When comment periods are cut, rulemaking often becomes dominated by heavily resourced corporate lobbyists, which can erode trust in the perceived fairness of the regulatory process.
