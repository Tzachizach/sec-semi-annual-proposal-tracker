Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Dave

I strongly oppose reducing public company reporting requirements from quarterly to semi-annual disclosures. Public markets function on transparency, accountability, and equal access to information. Reducing the frequency of required reporting weakens all three.

Quarterly reporting provides investors with regular insight into a company's financial condition, operational performance, risks, and management decisions. Moving from disclosures every ~90 days to every ~180 days would cut formal transparency in half and leave investors operating with significantly less verified information for longer periods of time.

This change would disproportionately harm individual investors. Large institutional firms can rely on analyst networks, proprietary research, and direct management access to fill information gaps. Retail investors depend far more heavily on public filings to make informed decisions. Fewer required disclosures would widen the informational advantage already held by institutions.

Reduced oversight also increases the risk of problems remaining hidden longer â including deteriorating finances, governance failures, aggressive accounting practices, or material business risks. Strong reporting standards are not merely administrative burdens; they are a core investor protection mechanism.

Public companies benefit enormously from access to public capital markets. In exchange, they should accept robust and frequent disclosure obligations. Accountability should increase with public ownership, not decrease.

If the concern is excessive short-term market pressure, the solution is not less transparency. The solution is encouraging long-term corporate governance while maintaining strong disclosure standards that protect investors and preserve confidence in public markets.
