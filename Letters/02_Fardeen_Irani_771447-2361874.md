To the Securities and Exchange Commission:

I am writing to provide my strong views on the proposal to permit optional semiannual reporting on Form 10-S. This initiative represents a critical opportunity for the Commission to fulfill its dual mandate: protecting investors while facilitating capital formation.

The Economic Necessity of Reducing the Compliance Burden
The current quarterly reporting regime has evolved into a "compliance tax" that disproportionately penalizes innovation. With the SEC's own estimates suggesting a single Form 10-Q requires over 1,000 burden hours and significant financial outlays, the cumulative annual drain on management is staggering. For a smaller issuer, reclaiming 2,000 to 3,000 hours of executive focus annually by moving to Form 10-S is not just a "cost saving" — it is a strategic reallocation of human capital toward R&D and long-term growth. Reducing this friction is consistent with international standards, such as those in the UK and EU.

Balancing Efficiency with Market Integrity
While the reduction in burden hours is a clear victory for corporate efficiency, I recognize the valid concerns regarding "information gaps." To ensure this proposal succeeds without harming the public:

Strengthen Materiality: The Commission should emphasize that the shift to Form 10-S must be paired with rigorous, real-time reporting of material events on Form 8-K.

Mitigate Volatility: By allowing companies to opt-in, the market can naturally "price" transparency. Companies that provide less frequent data may face a higher cost of capital, allowing the market — rather than a rigid mandate — to determine the optimal reporting frequency.

Conclusion
The 90-day reporting cycle often fuels a "short-termism" culture that rewards quarterly optics over decadal value. By adopting the Form 10-S framework, the Commission provides a necessary "off-ramp" for companies that prioritize long-term strategic execution over quarterly earnings management. I urge the Commission to finalize this proposal as a vital step in modernizing the American capital markets.

Respectfully submitted,
Fardeen Irani
