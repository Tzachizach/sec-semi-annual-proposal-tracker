I do not understand the logic of this proposed regulation change. Companies would still be required to keep their books, and would still be required to keep their boards apprised. It would
 only be the rest of us who would be in the dark. 

But perhaps that’s the point. Third class passage on the Titanic seemed like a good deal for the little guys,  until they found they were locked in on a literally fast-sinking ship. If the object is
 to save money, the savings seem minimal compared to the potential damage done to investor confidence. The actual savings from two less public filings per year would not be commensorate with such potential damage, with small individual investors and funds bearing
 the brunt of any market fallout as the perverbial "last to know". Like the Titanic passengers, what little guys stand to lose is a disproportionally greater percentage of their life savings and overall net worth than the big investors at the top. The cost
 of a company's decreased transparency falls dispropionately hardest on these little guys whose 401(k), IRA, or colllege funds hold the stock. And like the Titanic there assuredly won't be a lifeboat for these funds and small investors, potentially costing
 them dearly. The market is grounded in its "open book" policy. It is foundational. Bedrock. 

Such a short-sighted move would wreak havoc and eventually destroy the free trading system from which we enjoy and all prosper. 

It is only a relative
 few select elites, corporate insiders (and, alas, members of congress) who would gain through this regulation. 

It would become the purest definition of "insider trading" and would result in a skewed market, with flights of fund capital moving into
 companies that maintain a commitment to tranparency through quarterly reporting. Their fiduciary obligations would likely require them to do so. 

Fiduciaries would give way to a rise of unscrupulous investment managers claiming to have inside sources
 and information, as little guys look for an edge -- any light in the market information blackout handed them by the SEC through this regulation. Ask yourself what the market would look like if 401(k)s, etc., chose to only invest in companies that maintained
 their quarterly reporting voluntarily? And if that meant higher percentages of capital invested in European and possibly other markets? Shame on the SEC for even pondering such a move, much less proposing it. 

If you don't remember the history lessons
 from October 1929, we can only hope you still remember the ones from the Titanic. 

Shame.
