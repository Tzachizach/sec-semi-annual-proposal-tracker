We respectfully submit this comment in response to the Commission’s proposed
amendments that would allow Exchange Act reporting companies to file semiannual
reports on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q.

We oppose the proposal because it would reduce the timeliness and relevance of
public-company financial reporting, increase information risk for investors, and
weaken the periodic reporting discipline that supports investor protection and market
confidence.
                                                       ~Page 1~
GAAP and the FASB Conceptual Framework emphasize decision-
usefulness, relevance, and timeliness.
Accordingly, the appropriate reference for “relevance” and “timeliness” is the FASB
Conceptual Framework, not PCAOB auditing standards.

FASB Concepts Statement No. 8, Chapter 3, paragraph QC4, states that financial
information must be relevant and faithfully represent what it purports to represent to be
useful. Paragraph QC5 identifies relevance and faithful representation as the fundamental
qualitative characteristics of useful financial information. Paragraph QC6 states that
relevant financial information can make a diƯerence in users’ decisions. Paragraph QC7
explains that financial information can make a diƯerence if it has predictive value,
confirmatory value, or both.

Timeliness is also directly relevant. FASB Concepts Statement No. 8, Chapter 3, paragraph
QC29, states that timeliness means having information available to decision makers in
time to be capable of influencing their decisions, and that, generally, the older information
is, the less useful it is.

The proposal would move financial reporting in the opposite direction. By permitting
issuers to replace quarterly Form 10-Q reports with semiannual Form 10-S reporting, the
Commission would reduce the frequency with which investors receive structured,
comparable, GAAP-based interim financial information. The information may still
eventually be disclosed, but it will be less timely, less capable of influencing current
investment and voting decisions, and therefore less relevant.

The proposal increases information risk to investors.
The concept of information risk is especially important here. Information risk arises
because investors are outside the internal operations of management and must rely on
financial reports and related disclosures to assess the issuer’s financial condition,
performance, liquidity, and risks.

This concern is consistent with FASB Concepts Statement No. 8, Chapter 1, paragraph
OB5, which recognizes that many existing and potential investors, lenders, and other
creditors cannot require reporting entities to provide information directly to them and
therefore must rely on general purpose financial reports for much of the financial
information they need.

That is the core investor-protection issue. Management has direct access to the issuer’s
books, records, accounting judgments, internal forecasts, controls, and operating trends.
Public investors do not. The periodic reporting system reduces that distance by requiring
                                         ~Page 2~
recurring, standardized, GAAP-based disclosure. Moving from quarterly reporting to
semiannual reporting increases the time during which investors must make decisions
without current GAAP-based interim financial statements.

The proposal would hurt investors, who are the central stakeholders of
the SEC’s disclosure regime.
The proposal would harm investors by increasing the gap between what management
knows and what shareholders know. Investors would receive fewer mandatory interim
financial reports, less frequent MD&A, less frequent updates about liquidity and capital
resources, and fewer periodic disclosures regarding material trends and uncertainties.

This would particularly disadvantage ordinary investors. Large institutional investors may
be able to obtain or infer information through alternative data, private research, analyst
models, supplier and customer checks, or other market intelligence. Ordinary investors
generally cannot. Reducing the frequency of mandatory public reporting therefore risks
shifting informational advantage away from the public disclosure system and toward those
with greater private research capacity.

That result is contrary to the investor-protection purpose of the federal securities
laws.

The proposal is counterintuitive to the financial-reporting movement
toward relevance.
Financial reporting has long involved a pendulum between reliability, now generally
expressed through faithful representation, and relevance. In recent decades, standard
setters and regulators have repeatedly emphasized that financial information must be
useful to investors in making decisions. But usefulness requires more than eventual
disclosure. It requires disclosure soon enough to matter.

The proposal is counterintuitive because it weakens the very quality it purports not to
disturb: relevance. Under the FASB Conceptual Framework, information is relevant when it
can make a diƯerence in users’ decisions. Delayed information is less capable of making a
diƯerence, especially in public markets where investors must make decisions
continuously.

Semiannual reporting would therefore reduce the relevance of interim financial reporting
by making it less timely.

PCAOB auditing standards should be considered separately, as audit and
review standards, not GAAP.
Although PCAOB standards are not GAAP, they remain relevant to the investor-protection
consequences of the proposal. Quarterly Form 10-Q reporting is accompanied by quarterly
interim financial information and related review procedures. Reducing the number of
required interim reports would reduce the frequency of the reporting and review discipline
imposed on management, audit committees, and auditors.

That discipline matters because financial reporting is not merely a year-end event. Interim
reporting helps identify accounting issues, disclosure issues, liquidity concerns, internal-
control concerns, and emerging risks on a more current basis. Reducing that cadence
increases the risk that investors will learn of important developments later than they
should.

Recommendation.
The Commission should not adopt the proposal in its current form. The better course is to
retain mandatory quarterly reporting. Quarterly Form 10-Q reporting supports relevance,
timeliness, comparability, investor confidence, and market eƯiciency.

If the Commission nevertheless proceeds, it should at minimum preserve quarterly
reporting for accelerated filers and large accelerated filers, require clear investor approval
before an issuer may elect semiannual reporting, require prominent disclosure that the
issuer has reduced the frequency of GAAP-based interim reporting, and require quarterly
disclosure of core financial information, liquidity, capital resources, material trends, and
changes in internal control over financial reporting.

Investors are the principal intended beneficiaries of the public-company disclosure
system. A proposal that reduces the frequency of mandatory public reporting increases
information risk, widens the distance between shareholders and management, and
diminishes the timeliness and relevance of financial information. For those reasons, we
respectfully urge the Commission not to adopt the proposed semiannual reporting
framework.
