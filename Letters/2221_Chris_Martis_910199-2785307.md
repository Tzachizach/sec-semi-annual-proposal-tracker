Dear Secretary,
I am writing to express my strong support for the Commissionâs proposal to allow public companies the option to transition from quarterly reporting (Form 10-Q) to a semiannual reporting framework via the new Form 10-S. This amendment represents a much-needed modernization of the U.S. capital market, one that balances investor protection with structural efficiency.
While critics may reflexively view this proposal as a reduction in transparency, it is instead a pragmatic shift that enhances the quality of corporate governance and market health. Below are the primary reasons why this proposal is a significant step forward.

1. Fostering a True Long-Term Investment View
The current 90-day reporting cycle forces an artificial, short-term focus on corporate America. Management teams routinely sacrifice long-term research and development (R&D), capital investments, and strategic planning just to meet or beat arbitrary quarterly consensus estimates.

Shifting the mandatory baseline to six months allows executive teams the operational breathing room to execute complex strategies without the distraction of 90-day market noise.
 
It realigns corporate reporting with realistic business and product development cycles, which naturally span years, not quarters.

2. Meaningful Cost and Time Reductions
Preparing, auditing, and legally vetting a Form 10-Q three times a year is an incredibly resource-intensive burden. The SECâs own economic analysis indicates substantial cost savings per year for companies that transition.
For Small and Mid-Cap Issuers:These compliance costs are disproportionately heavy relative to revenue. Saving hundreds of thousands of dollars in accounting and legal overhead allows these companies to redirect vital capital toward innovation, job creation, and growth initiatives.
 
For Management: The preparation of a Form 10-Q requires weeks of internal distraction. Reducing this frequency allows leadership to focus on running the day-to-day business.

3. Materiality Guardrails: Key Events Are Always Reported
A common misconception is that semiannual reporting creates a six-month "dark period" for investors. This is fundamentally untrue. The U.S. disclosure ecosystem already has robust mechanisms to ensure investors receive timely, price-sensitive information:
 
Form 8-K Requirements: Any material corporate event such as executive changes, major mergers, bankruptcies, or material financial obligation must still be disclosed to the public within four business days.
 
Voluntary Updates: As seen in European markets (which successfully moved to a semiannual baseline), companies frequently choose to issue voluntary quarterly trading updates or earnings releases via Form 8-K to satisfy market demand, without the rigid overhead of a full 10-Q filing.

Why This is a Positive Progress, Not a Step Backward
Giving companies the *flexibility* to choose their reporting cadence does not inherently decrease transparency, it optimizes it. Data from global markets shows no statistical valuation penalty for companies that report semiannually.
Rather than a "one-size-fits-all" mandate, this rule creates a competitive mechanism. Highly volatile or capital-intensive companies can choose to stick with quarterly filings to maintain investor confidence, while mature or R&D-heavy companies can protect their long-term strategies by opting for semiannual filings.

Conclusion
By adopting this proposal, the SEC will lower the barriers to being a public company, encourage more private companies to enter the public markets (promoting capital formation), and shift the American market mindset back toward sustainable, long-term wealth creation.
I strongly urge the Commission to finalize and adopt this rule.
