As an individual investor, this concerns me deeply. Quarterly earnings reports are not just paperwork—they are one of the few tools everyday investors have to evaluate whether a company is healthy, growing, or heading in the wrong direction. Transparency creates accountability. 
If companies only report twice a year, investors could go six months without knowing about declining revenue, rising debt, layoffs, failed projects, or other material risks. That information gap benefits insiders and large institutions far more than ordinary shareholders trying to protect their retirement accounts, college funds, and life savings. 
History has already shown us what happens when transparency is reduced: lower valuations, less market confidence, and poorer outcomes for investors. Trust is built through timely disclosure, not less disclosure. 
The cost of quarterly reporting is small compared to the cost of investors making decisions in the dark. Markets function best when information is available, accurate, and timely. 
Truth and transparency are not burdens—they are the foundation of fair markets. If we want to protect individual investors and maintain confidence in our financial system, we should be demanding more transparency, not less. The greater good depends on it. 

Concerned American Investors
