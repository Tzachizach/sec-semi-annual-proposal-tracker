Subject: File Number S7-2026-15 - Opposition to Proposed Rule Change for Semiannual Reporting
Dear Secretary Countryman,
I am writing to express my strong opposition to the Securities and Exchange Commission's proposal to permit public companies to transition from quarterly financial reporting to semiannual reporting. While I understand the stated goal is to reduce corporate compliance costs and alleviate short-term market pressures, I believe this change will severely harm individual investors, degrade market transparency, and increase financial volatility.
Quarterly reporting is a cornerstone of the modern, transparent U.S. capital markets. Moving to a six-month reporting cycle introduces several significant risks:
Increased Information Asymmetry: Individual retail investors rely heavily on timely, standardized financial disclosures to make informed investment decisions. Extending the reporting window to six months creates a massive information vacuum. This gap will disproportionately benefit institutional investors who have the resources to utilize alternative data streams, leaving retail investors at a severe disadvantage.
Heightened Market Volatility: Waiting six months to receive audited financial updates means that unexpected operational shifts, supply chain disruptions, or macroeconomic impacts will build up behind closed doors. When these results are finally released, the market reactions are likely to be far more volatile and disruptive than the gradual adjustments seen under a quarterly system.
Reduced Management Accountability: Quarterly oversight forces corporate executives to maintain strict financial discipline and operational accountability throughout the fiscal year. A semiannual framework reduces this regular check-and-balance, potentially allowing corporate inefficiencies or strategic missteps to go unnoticed by the public for too long.
Ineffectiveness of Voluntary Updates: Allowing companies to provide informal interim updates via Form 8-K is an inadequate substitute for standardized Form 10-Q filings. Fragmented, non-standardized disclosures make it difficult for investors to accurately compare performance across companies or sectors.
For these reasons, I urge the Commission to withdraw this proposal and maintain the current quarterly reporting framework. Protecting the integrity, transparency, and fairness of our markets for all participants must remain the SEC's highest priority.
Thank you for your time and consideration of my comments.
Sincerely,
 Lance Graven
