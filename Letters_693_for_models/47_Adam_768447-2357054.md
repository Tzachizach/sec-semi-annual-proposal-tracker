# Letter 47 — Adam

- **Date:** 2026-05-05
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/s7-2026-15/s7202615-768447-2357054.html

---

I am submitting this comment in strong opposition to the proposed amendments that would allow public companies to file semiannual reports in lieu of quarterly reports on Form 10-Q.

The SEC's mandate is investor protection. This proposal moves in the opposite direction.
Reduced transparency increases investor risk. Quarterly reporting gives investors, analysts, and the market timely signals to price risk accurately.

Moving to semiannual reporting creates a six-month information vacuum. Academic research has documented that reduced reporting frequency leads investors to overreact to peer-company earnings, overcorrect when reports do arrive, and experience greater price volatility — not less. Less transparency does not produce more efficient markets. It produces less efficient ones.

The 2008 financial crisis is not ancient history. The systemic failures that nearly collapsed the global economy were enabled by opacity, inadequate disclosure, and insufficient oversight of corporate financial conditions. The regulatory response to that crisis was built on the premise that investors need more information, more frequently — not less. We have not resolved the structural vulnerabilities that made that crisis possible. Loosening disclosure requirements now is not reform. It is regression.

"Optional" frameworks erode in practice. Framing this as voluntary flexibility understates the real-world dynamics. When large-cap companies adopt semiannual reporting, smaller firms face pressure to follow to reduce compliance costs. What begins as an option becomes the floor. Investor protections are weakened for the entire market, not just the companies that elect the new cadence.

Compliance cost arguments do not justify the tradeoff. The burden-reduction case rests on the costs to companies. The SEC's obligation runs to investors, not issuers. Shifting that cost onto investors — in the form of information asymmetry, increased risk, and reduced ability to make informed decisions — is not a net regulatory benefit. It is a transfer of risk from corporations to the public.

The SEC should be strengthening oversight, not contracting it. Bad actors exist in public markets. They always have. Quarterly reporting does not prevent fraud, but it shortens the window in which fraud can go undetected. Extending that window by six months is a gift to those who exploit information gaps.

I urge the Commission to reject this proposal and reaffirm its commitment to the transparency standards that have distinguished U.S. capital markets for over five decades.
