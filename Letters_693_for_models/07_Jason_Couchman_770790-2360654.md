# Letter 07 — Jason Couchman

- **Date:** 2026-05-07
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/S7-2026-15/s7202615-770790-2360654.html

---

Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Jason Couchman

As a longtime IT professional working for public companies in information security, audit, and assurance, I object to any change that reduces PCAOB reporting requirements. The companies subject to quarterly reporting are generally large enough to sustain the compliance costs required for quarterly reporting. In the IT space at least, the controls being tested are not onerous by any stretch; instead, they are commonsense controls like making sure people who have left the company's systems access have been end-dated and closed, that application changes have been tested before being deployed to production environments, etc. These controls make organizations function better, and the cost to test them effectively usually amount to no more than 1-3% of company profit. Doing so on a quarterly basis allows company management to keep a finger on the pulse of activity at the staff level, and identify anomalies early so that internal management can prevent those issues from erupting into serious problems.

Companies are always under pressure to cut costs in administrative areas due to natural competition. The rules coming from regulatory entities are applied across all public companies and sets the "low bar" — if two public companies compete in the market, and the SEC shifts to a semi-annual model, neither company has any incentive to do "more work" on compliance and thus incur higher costs than its competitor, and so standards will degrade market-wide. It's the wrong signal for the SEC to send to public companies that it's "OK" to put off checking that pervasive IT controls for corporate governance are functioning well, which may lead to situations where company management discovers only too late in the annual reporting cycle that issues exist. The testing cycle is tightly correlated to the reporting cycle, so a likely outcome is that company management will find out its organizational controls are failing later than they otherwise would, leading to a loss of investor confidence at the time the issue is reported by the company's external auditor.

Moreover, in the age of cloud computing, companies on "Main Street" have become significantly more reliant on services provided by other companies who provide so-called "cloud computing" services, which are delivered by some of the largest and most profitable companies on earth — Google, Microsoft, Oracle, etc. These companies can well afford the 1-3% hit on their profits in order to demonstrate to their other client businesses that their operations are well-controlled. If these rules are relaxed for those companies only to discover later that companies in those industries relaxed their standards in order to keep pace with competition which led to systematic issues in corporate governance, then the issue will quickly snowball throughout capital markets in ways that are difficult to predict. Tech fraud is at an all-time high, and getting worse by the day. The IT controls tested as part of quarterly reporting also represent a low bar — if anything, the SEC should be pushing for higher standards tested at least quarterly, not the same governance standards tested semi-annually.
