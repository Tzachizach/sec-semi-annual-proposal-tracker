Dear Secretary,

I am writing to express my strong opposition to the Commissionâs proposal to allow public registrants the option to switch from a quarterly to a semi-annual reporting framework (Form 10-S). As an active market participant who relies on timely data for risk management and financial evaluation, I believe extending the disclosure cycle to six months will severely degrade the structural integrity, transparency, and efficiency of our capital markets.

My primary concerns regarding the transition to semi-annual reporting include:

Amplification of Gap Risk and Idiosyncratic Volatility: A six-month reporting cycle introduces a dangerous information vacuum. Under a semi-annual model, material changes in operational health, supply chain stability, and inventory velocity will remain obscured from the public for up to half a year. This lack of continuous disclosure will inevitably lead to massive, violent asset price gapping on earnings release dates. The market will see an increase in overnight capital destruction as investors are forced to absorb six months of accumulated operational surprises in a single trading session.

Delayed Price Discovery and Stale Trends: Efficient markets require continuous, incremental data to price assets accurately. Allowing companies to report semi-annually means equity prices will trend for months on entirely obsolete narratives. This creates an unlevel playing field where asymmetric information risk thrives, directly disadvantaging retail investors who do not have access to alternative data pipelines.

The Critical Function of Frequent Cadences: Regular, predictable, quarterly touchpoints ensure that public companies behave with consistent transparency. While a three-month cadence requires discipline, it ensures that management remains accountable to its baseline operating targets in real-time. In contrast, a six-month cycle represents an eternity in modern, algorithmic markets, completely breaking the historical baseline of corporate accountability.

While proponents argue that quarterly reporting places an administrative burden on companies, the infrastructure for modern corporate accounting is highly automated. The compliance cost of compiling quarterly data is a minor, necessary premium paid to maintain public investor trust and market liquidity.

Frequent, structured transparency is fundamental to a well-functioning market. I strongly urge the Commission to reject the proposed Form 10-S and maintain the current mandatory quarterly reporting framework to protect investors and maintain orderly, transparent markets.

Sincerely,

Ryan Schaefer
Individual Investor
