Public Comment on Proposed Rule S7-2026-15 - Semiannual Reporting

To the Securities and Exchange Commission,

I am submitting this comment in opposition to the proposed amendments under File Number S7-2026-15, which would allow public companies to file semiannual reports in lieu of quarterly reports.

I am a software developer building financial transparency infrastructure that tracks institutional investment activity using public SEC filings, on-chain blockchain data, and real-time disclosure feeds. My perspective is technical and practical: the cost savings argument underpinning this proposal is obsolete, and the proposal moves in the wrong direction at precisely the wrong moment.

The compliance cost burden cited in support of this rule assumes a world of manual accounting, physical filings, and costly reconciliation processes. That world no longer exists for the vast majority of public companies. Modern financial systems are digital, automated, and continuously maintained. The marginal cost of packaging already-maintained books into a quarterly filing is minimal. The heavy lifting -auditing, internal controls, financial statement preparation - happens on a rolling basis regardless of reporting frequency. Reducing filings from four to two per year does not meaningfully reduce that burden.

More importantly, this proposal ignores where financial markets are heading. Blockchain-based financial infrastructure - already live and operational on networks including XRP Ledger and Stellar - enables continuous, real-time, permissionless disclosure. Institutional transactions are already being recorded on public ledgers with millisecond timestamps. The trajectory of modern capital markets is toward greater transparency and higher frequency disclosure, not less. Proposing to halve reporting frequency in 2026 is a step backward relative to where the technology already is.

Reducing disclosure frequency does not harm all market participants equally. Institutional insiders, large asset managers, and company executives maintain continuous access to operational data regardless of what is filed publicly. Retail investors and independent analysts depend entirely on public filings. Extending the blind spot from 90 days to 180 days widens an already significant information asymmetry - and does so in a way that primarily benefits those who already have the least need for public disclosures.

Academic research supports this concern. Studies of European markets, which moved to semiannual reporting before partially reversing course, found that reduced reporting frequency created information vacuums that caused investors to overreact to peer-firm disclosures and produced more volatile, less efficient stock prices. The U.S. should not repeat that experiment.

I urge the Commission to reject this proposal and instead direct its attention toward modernizing disclosure infrastructure - leveraging existing and emerging technology to make reporting more frequent, more accessible, and more useful to all market participants, not less.

Respectfully submitted,
[Morten Hansen]
[Tampere, Finland]
