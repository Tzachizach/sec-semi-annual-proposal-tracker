I am writing in opposition to the proposed Form 10-S rule that would allow public companies to reduce quarterly reporting to semi-annual reporting.

Quarterly disclosure requirements exist to protect ordinary investors, not corporate management. Executives, board members, and large institutional investors have continuous access to internal financial data. Retail investors do not. Quarterly filings are one of the few mechanisms that partially close that information gap. Extending the reporting window to six months doubles the period during which insiders can act on material non-public information while the public remains uninformed.

The “voluntary” framing does not hold up to scrutiny. Competitive and market dynamics will pressure companies toward semi-annual filing once larger peers adopt it. Voluntary standards in disclosure contexts have historically become de facto floors, not ceilings.

The fraud prevention argument also deserves serious weight. Major accounting scandals share a common feature: extended opacity. Quarterly reporting shortens the window in which material misstatements can compound undetected. Lengthening that window is a structural regression, regardless of intent.

The compliance savings cited in support of this proposal accrue to companies and their executives. The cost — degraded information quality — falls on the retirement accounts, IRAs, and college savings funds of ordinary investors. That is not a reasonable trade.

I urge the Commission to reject Form 10-S and maintain quarterly reporting requirements
