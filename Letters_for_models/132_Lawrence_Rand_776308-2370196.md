## Abstract

Quarterly reporting has been a central component of the U.S. equity markets since 1970. Recent statements by President Trump and an ongoing inquiry by the SEC have highlighted potential grievances with the current system. Critics believe that more frequent reporting incentivizes short-term thinking among management and investors. Similar criticism have been applied to the practice of earnings guidance. Yet, current reporting requirements have been attributed to greater transparency of information and a lower cost of capital for companies seeking to raise funds. This paper seeks to understand the sources of this disagreement. Using interviews of key actors from across the private and public sector, along with primary and secondary research, this paper finds that **a bifurcated reporting system, whereby smaller companies would be allowed to report on a semiannual basis, is the best system to enhance long-term shareholder value.** We also recommend that companies that issue earnings guidance should consider ending the practice, if appropriate.

## Structure of the paper

**Chapter 1 — Introduction.** Sets historical context: the SEC moved from semiannual to quarterly in 1970; SOX (2002) layered on management certification and internal-controls requirements. Notes bipartisan support for revisiting frequency (Trump 2018, Obama 2015, Clinton 2016) and that the methodology rests on interviews with investors, investment bankers, C-level executives, regulators, and PR experts.

**Chapter 2 — Benefits of the Quarterly Reporting System.**
- *Greater Transparency:* Stanford GSB (Beaver, McNichols, Wang 2015) on information content; Indiana University (Arif & De George 2018) finding that returns of semiannual announcers are "almost twice as sensitive" to U.S. bellwethers' earnings during nonreporting periods (information spillover from less frequent disclosure). Reg FD (2000) cemented the 10-Q as the main source of information. Notes Madoff as a counterexample (transparency is about quality, not just quantity).
- *Lower Cost of Equity Capital:* Fu, Kraft & Zhang (2012) — U.S. cost of equity dropped 1955–1970 as the SEC moved from semiannual to quarterly. S&P 500 trades at 22.23x trailing earnings vs. 14.6x for STOXX Europe 600 (Oct 2018).
- *Insider trading risk:* If disclosure cadence falls, analyst coverage drops and insider abnormal returns rise (Dou, Ji, Reibstein & Wu 2018).

**Chapter 3 — Issues with the Quarterly Reporting System.**
- *Fosters Short-Term Thinking:* Musk (Tesla privatization email), Ron Shaich (Panera/JAB buyout), Kathryn Cearns (ICAEW). Private equity dealflow up 568% since 2000.
- *Cost of Compliance:* Out-of-pocket cash ~$100K per reporting period for large issuers, plus Q4/annual costs that can be double. Audit Analytics for WSJ: large accelerated filers paid $541 per $1M revenue in 2016 audit fees (0.05%) vs. $3,345 per $1M for smaller firms (0.33%). Matt Kreps (Darrow Associates): "dollar cost [from quarterly reporting] can be a disproportionate amount of OPEX." Small cap media-company executive quoted: CEO spends 2% of annual time on quarterly filings, CFO 5%. PwC 2017: 4.5 days to close vs 6 days in 2009.

**Chapter 4 — Benefits of Quarterly Guidance.**
- *Wall Street Expectations:* Bob Pisani (CNBC): "if companies don't set goals, Wall Street will." Epstein WSJ: study of 27,000 guidance reports found management forecasts beat analyst forecasts.
- *Publicity:* Houston/Lev/Tucker — firms that don't guide get less analyst coverage and weaker profits.

**Chapter 5 — Issues with Quarterly Guidance.**
- *Fosters Short-Termism:* Larry Fink ("quarterly earnings hysteria"), Bob Lutz ("father of many, many bad product decisions" — channel-stuffing). Cheng-Subramanyam-Zhang: guiding firms invest 10% less in R&D. McKinsey/FCLT 2016: 60% of execs would delay projects and 80% would cut discretionary funding to meet earnings.
- *Buybacks:* Almeida/Fos/Kronlund — repurchases more likely when they push EPS over forecast; reduce employment and investment. Lazonick HBR 2014.
- *Unpredictability:* Ari Gabinet (Brown) — guidance allows numbers to be massaged legally; whisper numbers undermine CFO integrity. PSLRA 1995 safe harbor protects forward-looking statements unless knowingly misleading.
- *Accountability:* CFA 2006 — 76% of buy-side want move away from quarterly EPS guidance. Rivel Research — only 9% of investors view sub-annual guidance as important. 70% of shares held by long-term investors.

**Chapter 6 — Recommendation.**
The system is imperfect but the UK comparison (Pozen, Nallareddy & Rajgopal 2017) suggests change is not strictly necessary for mid/large caps. The paper recommends a **bifurcated reporting system**: small caps allowed semiannual, mid/large caps remain quarterly. Notes SEC Chairman Clayton's October 2018 statements confirming quarterly will stay for larger companies while the SEC explores semiannual for smaller. Grouping should be based on annual revenue (consistent with the JOBS Act 2012 EGC framework). Acknowledges Pozen's counter-concern that bifurcation reduces small-cap liquidity and raises their cost of capital — flags this as needing further empirical research before implementation. On guidance: there is no one-size-fits-all answer; companies with long operating cycles (construction, energy) may find quarterly guidance distracting; suggests KPIs (Facebook DAU/MAU/ARPU across 3-, 5-, 10-year horizons) as alternatives to point EPS estimates.
