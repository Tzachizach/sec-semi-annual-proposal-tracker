# Letter 26 — J R

- **Date:** 2026-05-06
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/S7-2026-15/s7202615-769587-2358754.html

---

I respectfully oppose the proposal to reduce the frequency of required reporting for public issuers from quarterly to semi-annual. While I appreciate the objective of easing compliance burdens, I believe the proposal would have several unintended consequences that could ultimately undermine the transparency, efficiency, and integrity of U.S. capital markets, which are currently the world standard.

First, reducing the cadence of financial reporting would materially diminish the timeliness of information available to investors. Quarterly reporting plays a critical role in providing up-to-date insight into an issuer's financial performance, liquidity, and emerging risks. Moving to a semi-annual model would lengthen the information gap between disclosures, increasing uncertainty and potentially impairing investors' ability to make informed decisions. In volatile or rapidly evolving industries this loss of timely visibility could be particularly significant.

Second, the proposal may lead to increased market volatility and information asymmetry. In the absence of regular, structured reporting, market participants may rely more heavily on informal disclosures, selective communications, or non-GAAP metrics, which may not be subject to the same level of rigor or internal control. This dynamic could disproportionately benefit well-connected or institutional investors, to the detriment of broader market fairness.

Third, quarterly reporting serves as a key discipline mechanism for issuers, reinforcing the importance of robust internal controls, close processes, and ongoing financial oversight. Reducing reporting frequency could inadvertently weaken these governance practices, leading to less frequent monitoring of financial trends, delayed identification of issues, and increased risk of error or misstatement accumulating over longer reporting periods.

Additionally, from an audit and assurance perspective, interim reporting supports continuous engagement between auditors and management, facilitating earlier identification and resolution of complex accounting issues. A shift to semi-annual reporting could compress review timelines and increase the complexity and risk associated with interim reviews and annual audits.

Finally, the U.S. reporting framework has long been viewed as a global benchmark for transparency and investor protection. A move away from quarterly reporting could represent a step backward relative to this standard and may negatively impact investor confidence in U.S. capital markets.

For these reasons, I believe the current quarterly reporting framework strikes an appropriate balance between transparency and compliance burden and should be retained. Any efforts to reduce regulatory burden would be better directed toward simplifying disclosures, modernizing reporting requirements, or providing targeted relief for smaller issuers rather than reducing the frequency of financial reporting.
