I respectfully oppose reducing public company reporting frequency from quarterly to semiannual reporting. 

First, transparency and accountability require regular disclosure and oversight. History demonstrates the risks that can arise when independent oversight is reduced or pushed further away from the underlying activity. For example, concerns have been raised about the extent to which regulatory oversight functions were delegated to manufacturers in the period preceding the Boeing 737 MAX accidents. Regardless of the specifics of that case, the broader lesson is that effective governance depends on maintaining strong mechanisms for independent review and timely visibility into potential problems. Less frequent reporting reduces transparency for investors and regulators and may allow issues to go undetected for longer periods. 

Second, the argument that semiannual reporting would encourage more long-term thinking is unpersuasive. Moving from a three-month reporting cycle to a six-month cycle does not fundamentally change managerial incentives, capital market pressures, or investment horizons. Companies would still be evaluated on financial performance, and there is little evidence that eliminating one quarterly report would materially increase R&D or other long-term investments. 

Third, advances in accounting systems, data integration, and AI-driven automation have significantly reduced the burden of preparing financial reports. As reporting processes become more automated, the cost savings from eliminating quarterly reporting are likely to be modest, while the loss of timely information for investors could be substantial. 

For these reasons, I believe quarterly reporting should be retained.
