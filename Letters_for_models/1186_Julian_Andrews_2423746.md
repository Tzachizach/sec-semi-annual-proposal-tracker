The cost of reporting is de minimis for most publicly traded companies, and the value of the information to investors is huge. Companies still need to track their finances after all. Some sort of targeted exception from quarterly reporting for small or newer companies might make sense. This blanket policy is unjustifiable on cost grounds.

The argument that the reduced reporting is optional, and therefore not a problem also makes no sense. There is basically no incentive for companies to report more often than required by law. If the requirement is semi-annual, most companies will report semi-annually. I struggle to even make this argument make sense.

The best rationale I've seen given is that this will free companies up from having to be responsive to the market in the short term, which will allow them to make better long term decisions. That's Orwellian double-speak. We want companies to be responsive to the market, and the idea that most of the time CEOs will use their new freedom to make better long term plans instead of to advance their own short term agendas is inconsistent with a rudimentary understanding of how most companies operate in the real world.

I have little doubt that the employees at the SEC understand all this. If this rule passes, we will all know this is regulatory capture.

- Julian Andrews
