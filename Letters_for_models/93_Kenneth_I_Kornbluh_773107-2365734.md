Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Kenneth I Kornbluh

May 11, 2026

To the Securities and Exchange Commission:

I submit this comment in opposition to the proposed amendments under File Number S7-2026-15, which would permit public companies to file semiannual reports in lieu of quarterly reports.

I manage a hedge fund operating a series of five funds with hundreds of millions of dollars in assets belonging to individual investors. Our investment process depends heavily on quarterly reports, annual reports, and all public company disclosures. This proposed rule change would directly and materially harm our ability to make informed investment decisions on behalf of our clients.

Quarterly earnings figures and forward-looking guidance are central to how we evaluate companies and construct our portfolios. We rely on these numbers not only to assess a company's current financial health, but to compare actual results against prior estimates and to track management's consistency and accuracy in projecting future performance. Reducing reporting to a semiannual cadence would double the information gap between reporting periods, leaving investors to make consequential decisions with materially less data — and giving company insiders a significant and troubling informational advantage over the markets.

This informational asymmetry is already a serious concern under the current quarterly regime. Company insiders — executives, board members, and others with access to internal data — know their company's financial trajectory months before the public does. They observe revenue trends, cost pressures, and operational developments in real time. Public investors, by contrast, must wait for scheduled disclosures. Moving to semiannual reporting would dramatically widen this gap, extending the period during which insiders hold material non-public information to an even greater degree.

Rather than moving in this direction, the Commission should consider whether an additional lighter and more frequent reporting regime — such as mandatory monthly disclosure of key performance metrics — might serve investors and market integrity. Some of the world's best-managed companies already voluntarily provide this level of transparency, demonstrating that it is both achievable and valuable.

Equally important to us is the qualitative dimension of quarterly reporting: the earnings calls and commentary delivered by senior executives in their own voices. These are irreplaceable. The way a CEO or CFO speaks about their business — their tone, their confidence, their command of the details — provides insight that no financial statement alone can convey. It allows sophisticated investors to assess management quality, detect uncertainty or overconfidence, and make more nuanced judgments about long-term prospects. Cutting the frequency of these interactions in half would be a serious loss to the investment community.

The United States' dominance in global financial markets is not accidental. It is built on a foundation of robust, frequent, and reliable public disclosure requirements. Quarterly reporting is a cornerstone of that foundation. It gives investors confidence that the companies they invest in are being held to consistent standards of transparency. Reducing that frequency undermines market fairness, erodes investor trust, and weakens the competitive advantage that U.S. markets have long held globally.

I strongly urge the Commission to reject this proposed rule change and instead consider moving in the opposite direction — toward more frequent and more transparent disclosure — in order to better protect investors and preserve the integrity of U.S. capital markets.

Respectfully submitted,
Ken Kornbluh
CEO
Kambio Partners LLC
