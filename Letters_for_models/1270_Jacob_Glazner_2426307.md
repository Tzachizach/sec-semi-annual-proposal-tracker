To Whom It May Concern,

My name is Jacob Glazner, and I am a retail investor, homeowner, and working professional in Louisiana. I currently work as a Program and Project Management professional in the electrical manufacturing industry and regularly invest a portion of my income into publicly traded companies and retirement accounts.

I am writing to express my support for maintaining quarterly reporting requirements for publicly traded companies.

As an individual investor, I do not have access to management teams, institutional research, private analyst meetings, or the resources available to large investment firms. Quarterly reporting is one of the few tools available to investors like me that provides consistent, transparent, and standardized information about a company’s financial health and operational performance.

Timely reporting helps create a level playing field between everyday investors and large institutional investors. Without quarterly reporting, the gap between those with access to information and those without would likely widen. Transparency is one of the primary reasons U.S. capital markets are viewed as the strongest and most trusted in the world.

In my professional career, I manage complex programs and projects worth millions of dollars. Decisions are made using regular reporting cycles, milestone reviews, schedule updates, and performance metrics. Waiting six months or a year to identify problems would make effective management significantly more difficult. The same principle applies to public companies. Investors need periodic visibility into performance, risks, cash flow, debt levels, and management execution in order to make informed decisions.

Quarterly reporting also serves as an important accountability mechanism. Public company executives and boards are entrusted with shareholder capital. Regular disclosures encourage disciplined management, allow investors to identify emerging issues sooner, and help maintain confidence in the market.

While I understand concerns that quarterly reporting may contribute to short-term thinking, I believe the solution is not to reduce transparency. Long-term investors can choose to focus on multi-year performance trends while still benefiting from quarterly updates. Less information does not necessarily promote long-term thinking; it often increases uncertainty.

As someone who invests regularly for retirement and long-term financial security, I strongly believe quarterly reporting remains an essential component of fair, efficient, and transparent capital markets.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,

Jacob Glazner
Metairie, Louisiana
