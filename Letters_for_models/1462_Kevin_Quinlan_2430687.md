As a senior software engineer working on systems for the International Space Station being, I rely on data integrity every day. If we do not have timely, accurate telemetry, we lose confidence in the system. Financial markets are no different.

Reducing public company reporting requirements from quarterly to every six months moves in the wrong direction. Investors already operate in an environment where monopolistic behavior, political conflicts of interest, excessive leverage, and declining trust in institutions have distorted markets. Transparency is one of the few protections ordinary investors still have.

Over the last decade we have watched corporate debt markets inflate, fraud enforcement struggles increase, and retail investors shoulder more responsibility for retirement security while having less visibility into the companies they are expected to invest in. At the same time, many Americans feel economic pressure while seeing political and corporate leadership accumulate extraordinary wealth and influence. That combination is not sustainable long term.

If anything, regulators should be strengthening confidence in markets through better enforcement, clearer disclosures, and faster accountability — not reducing the flow of information to investors.

The public is being asked to trust institutions at a time when trust is already fragile. Weakening reporting standards risks further damaging confidence in both the market and the regulatory system itself.
