I strongly oppose any proposal that would reduce the frequency of public company financial reporting from quarterly to semiannual reporting. 

As an individual investor, timely access to accurate financial information is one of the few tools I have to compete in a market dominated by institutional investors, hedge funds, analysts, and corporate insiders. Reducing public disclosures would create a significant information gap that favors large institutions while leaving ordinary investors at a disadvantage. 

Quarterly reporting promotes: 

* Transparency and accountability 
* Efficient price discovery 
* Stronger corporate governance 
* Greater investor confidence in U.S. capital markets 

The argument that less frequent reporting would reduce short-term thinking ignores the fact that management teams and institutional investors will continue to have access to far more information than the general public. The result would not be less short-termism—it would be less transparency. 

America’s financial markets are the strongest in the world because they are built on disclosure, trust, and equal access to information. Reducing reporting requirements risks increasing information asymmetry and undermining confidence among retail investors. 

The SEC’s mission includes protecting investors and maintaining fair, orderly, and efficient markets. This proposal would move in the opposite direction. 

I urge the SEC to preserve quarterly reporting requirements and reject any rule that would reduce the frequency of financial disclosures to the investing public.
