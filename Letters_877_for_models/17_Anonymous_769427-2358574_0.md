# Letter 17 — Anonymous

- **Date:** 2026-05-06
- **Source:** https://www.sec.gov/comments/S7-2026-15/s7202615-769427-2358574_0.html

Chair Atkins and Commissioners:
I appreciate the opportunity to comment on the Commission's proposed amendments that would permit registrants to elect semiannual reporting on new Form 10-S in lieu of quarterly reporting on Form 10-Q.

I support the Commission's objective of providing flexibility in interim reporting cadence and acknowledge that mandatory quarterly reporting may not optimally serve all issuers or investors. However, I respectfully recommend that, if adopted, the optional semiannual reporting framework be paired with an enhanced level of independent auditor assurance over the Form 10-S financial statements, beyond the level currently associated with quarterly Form 10-Q reporting.

Moving from three quarterly interim reports to a single semiannual report represents a material reduction in the frequency of standardized, periodic financial disclosure. While the proposal contemplates continued disclosure through Form 8-K and other channels, those mechanisms are episodic and event-driven and do not substitute for comprehensive interim financial statements subject to uniform presentation, comparability, and internal control discipline.

From an investor perspective, less frequent mandatory reporting inherently increases estimation uncertainty, lag in visibility into operating trends, liquidity, and capital structure, and the risk that adverse developments accumulate before being reflected in filed financial statements. A corresponding increase in assurance would help preserve investor confidence while allowing issuers to realize the compliance benefits of semiannual reporting.

Under the existing quarterly reporting regime, Form 10-Q financial statements are subject to a limited-assurance review by the independent auditor rather than a full audit. This framework was designed for shorter measurement periods and more frequent investor updates.

Applying the same limited-assurance review standard to a six-month reporting period creates a different risk profile. A semiannual period necessarily embeds more judgmental estimates, greater cumulative accrual activity, increased exposure to the risk of undetected misstatements, and longer periods between auditor involvement in interim results. As a result, the assurance framework that is acceptable for quarterly reporting may be misaligned with investor expectations when reporting frequency is reduced.

Importantly, I am not suggesting that the Commission require a full audit opinion on Form 10-S financial statements. Instead, the Commission could consider incremental and targeted enhancements that strike an appropriate balance between cost, feasibility, and investor protection.

Potential approaches include, for example, requiring the filing of an auditor's review report with Form 10-S (rather than permitting the review to be performed but not disclosed), improving transparency about the scope and outcome of auditor involvement. Mandating an expanded review standard for semiannual periods (e.g., enhanced procedures around risk assessment, significant estimates, and material changes in internal control). Requiring explicit auditor attestation as to whether they are aware of material modifications that should be made for the financial statements to conform with GAAP for a six-month period. Requiring management to disclose whether the scope of auditor review for Form 10-S differs from the scope historically applied to quarterly Form 10-Qs, enabling investors to assess assurance quality. Any of these approaches would represent a measured evolution of the current framework rather than a wholesale redesign.

The proposal envisions that issuers and investors will "determine for themselves" the most appropriate reporting cadence. For that market choice to function effectively, investors must have confidence in the reliability of the information produced under each option.

Requiring enhanced assurance for Form 10-S would reduce concerns that semiannual reporting is perceived as a weakening of investor protections, support comparability between quarterly and semiannual filers, and encourage issuers to make the election based on business suitability rather than perceived reductions in scrutiny. In this sense, enhanced assurance would strengthen, rather than undermine, the Commission's goal of flexibility.

If the Commission adopts an optional semiannual reporting framework, I respectfully recommend that it also require an increased level of independent auditor assurance for Form 10-S financial statements relative to quarterly Form 10-Q reporting, commensurate with the longer reporting period and reduced filing frequency. This could be achieved through enhanced review requirements, increased transparency around auditor involvement, or other targeted attestation mechanisms that do not impose the cost or complexity of a full interim audit.

Thank you for the opportunity to comment on this important proposal and for the Commission's continued focus on balancing regulatory flexibility with robust investor protection.
