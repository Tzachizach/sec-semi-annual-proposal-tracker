Subject: File No. S7-2026-15
From: Ben Brown

As an individual investor in US-listed clean energy equities who relies extensively on quarterly financials for my investment strategy, I strongly oppose the SEC's proposal to eliminate mandatory quarterly reporting for public companies. Quarterly disclosures are important for transparency and a critical safeguard for retail investors, who unlike institutional players lack direct access to management, private analyst calls, and proprietary data feeds. Reducing reporting frequency to semi-annual would widen the information gap between Wall Street insiders and ordinary shareholders like myself, creating longer windows in which business deterioration, accounting irregularities, or shifting fundamentals can go undetected. I understand that proponents argue that quarterly reporting fosters short-termism and imposes compliance costs, but the evidence for these claims is weak, and the costs of reduced transparency fall disproportionately on the individual investors least able to bear them like myself. Quarterly reports also serve as a discipline on management, providing regular checkpoints that deter fraud, encourage accurate forecasting, and allow markets to price shares efficiently. Eroding this discipline would undermine investor confidence, increase volatility around the less-frequent disclosures that remain, and ultimately raise the cost of capital for the very companies the proposal claims to help. I urge the Commission to preserve the current quarterly reporting framework, which has served US capital markets and retail investors well for decades.
