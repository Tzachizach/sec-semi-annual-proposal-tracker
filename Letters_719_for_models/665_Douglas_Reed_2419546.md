To whom it may concern, 

I’m writing as an individual retail investor who relies on regular, transparent disclosure to make decisions about my retirement savings and long-term investments. I am strongly opposed to the SEC’s proposal under File No. S7-2026-15 to allow companies to report only twice a year instead of quarterly. 

Cutting required reporting in half would leave ordinary investors effectively flying blind for six months at a time. In that period, a lot can change inside a business—failed product launches, rising debt, legal issues, or customer losses. Corporate insiders, their lawyers, and large institutional investors will still have access to up-to-date information, but people like me, investing through 401(k)s, IRAs, and brokerage accounts, would be the last to find out. That creates an unfair information advantage for insiders and increases the risk that bad news is quietly offloaded onto retail investors before it becomes public. 

Less frequent reporting also creates more room for fraud and misrepresentation to grow unnoticed. History has shown—from Enron and WorldCom to the run-up to 2008—that opacity and long gaps in disclosure make it easier for serious problems to fester. Quarterly filings don’t eliminate fraud, but they shorten the window in which it can go undetected. Extending that window to six months increases risk for the very “investing public” the SEC is supposed to protect. 

I’m also concerned that calling this change “optional” is misleading. In practice, once large issuers move to semiannual reporting, competitive and market pressure will push others to follow, and quarterly reporting will become the exception rather than the norm. Over time, this would likely turn a voluntary option into a de facto new standard, reducing transparency across the market. 

Finally, the benefits to issuers appear modest compared to the cost imposed on investors. Companies still maintain internal books, brief boards, and monitor performance. Skipping a public filing mainly saves on legal and accounting costs, which accrue to the company and its executives—not to investors. The cost of poorer, less timely information is borne by people like me who are trying to responsibly manage long-term savings. 

For these reasons, I urge the SEC to keep quarterly reporting requirements in place and reject the proposal to allow Form 10-S and six-month blackout periods. Frequent, consistent disclosure is fundamental to fair, efficient markets and to the trust that individual investors place in the U.S. public markets. 

Thank you for considering my comment. 

Sincerely, 
A concerned retail investor
